Linear Motion
Movement
Movement in a straight line in one direction. The object does not curve or rotate — it travels along a fixed path from A to B.
"A train moving along a straight track is an example of linear motion — it travels in one direction along a fixed line."
💡 Linear = relating to a line. Straight line, one direction. Examples: train on track, bullet fired from gun, drawer opening.
Rotary Motion
Movement
Movement in a circle around a central point. The object spins or rotates continuously. This is the most common type of motion in machines.
"A fan blade spinning around its central hub is rotary motion — it continuously revolves around a fixed central point."
💡 Rotate = to spin around a point. Rotary = always circular. Examples: fan blades, bicycle wheel, electric motor, gear, pulley.
Oscillating Motion
Movement
Movement that swings backwards and forwards in an arc around a central point. Unlike reciprocating motion, it follows a curved path, not a straight line.
"A child on a swing demonstrates oscillating motion — the swing arcs backward and forward around the pivot point at the top."
💡 Oscillate = to swing regularly back and forth. Key: the path is an ARC (curved), not straight. Examples: pendulum, metronome, windscreen wiper arc.
Reciprocating Motion
Movement
Movement that goes backwards and forwards in a straight line. Unlike oscillating motion, the path is straight, not curved. Many machines convert rotary motion into reciprocating motion.
"A sewing machine needle moves in reciprocating motion — it drives straight up and down repeatedly in a straight vertical line."
💡 Reciprocate = to go back and forth. Key distinction from oscillating: reciprocating = straight line, oscillating = curved arc. Examples: piston in engine, sewing needle, saw blade.
Lever
Levers
A fixed rigid beam that pivots around a point called the fulcrum. It requires three things: effort (input force), fulcrum (pivot point), and load (output force). Levers create mechanical advantage.
"A crowbar is a class 1 lever — a small effort applied at one end can lift a heavy load at the other, because the lever creates mechanical advantage."
💡 A lever = a rigid bar + a pivot (fulcrum). The position of the fulcrum determines the class (1, 2 or 3). Always three components: Effort → Fulcrum → Load (in different orders for each class).
Fulcrum
Levers
The fixed pivot point around which a lever turns or rotates. The position of the fulcrum relative to the effort and load determines what class of lever it is.
"In a seesaw (a Class 1 lever), the fulcrum is the central pivot in the middle — the two children sit on either side as the effort and load."
💡 The fulcrum = the pivot. Think of a seesaw — the metal triangle in the middle that it balances on is the fulcrum. Without a fulcrum, there is no lever.
Mechanical Advantage (MA)
Levers
A number that tells you how much a mechanism multiplies the input force. MA = Load ÷ Effort. An MA greater than 1 means the lever helps you — the output force is greater than the input force.
"A wheelbarrow has an MA of 6 — a 50N effort can lift a 300N load, because MA = 300 ÷ 50 = 6."
💡 MA > 1 = the machine helps you (less effort needed). MA < 1 = you need more effort than the output (like tweezers — less force but more precise control). Formula: MA = Load ÷ Effort.
Velocity Ratio (VR)
Maths
The ratio of how far the effort moves compared to how far the load moves. VR = Distance moved by effort ÷ Distance moved by load. A high VR means you move the effort a long way to move the load a small amount.
"A wheelbarrow has a VR of 8 — the handles move 800 mm while the load only rises 100 mm. VR = 800 ÷ 100 = 8."
💡 VR = how far you push ÷ how far the load moves. VR is always about DISTANCE, not force. High VR = large mechanical advantage but you move your hands further. VR = Distance by effort ÷ Distance by load.
Efficiency
Maths
A percentage showing how well a mechanism converts input energy into useful output energy. No mechanism is 100% efficient because friction always wastes some energy. Efficiency = (MA ÷ VR) × 100%.
"The wheelbarrow has an efficiency of 75% (MA=6, VR=8): Efficiency = 6/8 × 100 = 75%. The other 25% is lost to friction."
💡 Efficiency is always less than 100% in real life — friction steals energy as heat. Formula: Efficiency = MA ÷ VR × 100%. High efficiency = less energy wasted.
Cam
Cams
A mechanism that converts rotary motion into reciprocating or oscillating motion. A cam is an irregularly shaped disc attached to a rotating shaft. As it rotates, it pushes a follower up and down.
"Car engines use pear-shaped cams to open and close valves — as the cam rotates, it pushes the valve open (rise), holds it (dwell), then allows it to close (fall)."
💡 Cam = a shaped disc that converts spinning (rotary) → up-and-down (reciprocating). Always three stages of follower movement: RISE → DWELL → FALL. The shape of the cam controls the pattern.
Follower
Cams
A device that rests against the cam surface and follows its profile, moving up and down as the cam rotates. There are three types: roller (reduces friction), knife edge (accurate), and flat (high load bearing).
"The roller follower in a car engine uses a small wheel to roll along the cam surface, reducing friction and allowing the engine to run at high speeds."
💡 Three follower types: Roller = low friction (fast); Knife edge = accurate (embroidery); Flat = high load but more friction (steam engine). The follower translates the cam's shape into movement.
Dwell
Cams
The stage of a cam's rotation when the follower remains stationary (does not move up or down). This happens when the part of the cam in contact with the follower is equidistant from the centre of rotation.
"A pear-shaped cam dwells (keeps the follower still) for about half of each rotation, then rapidly rises and falls in the second half of the cycle."
💡 Dwell = pause. The follower is at rest during the dwell phase. Three phases: rise (goes up), dwell (stays still), fall (comes down). The pear cam has the longest dwell phase.
Pulley System
Pulleys
A mechanism using wheels with a belt running in their grooves to transmit rotary motion from a driver shaft to a driven shaft. When two pulleys of different sizes are used, the smaller one spins faster.
"A pillar drill uses a pulley system — by moving the belt to different sized wheels, the user can change the drill's speed without changing the motor."
💡 Driver pulley (connected to motor) → belt → driven pulley (connected to output). Smaller pulley = faster. Key: both pulleys rotate in the SAME direction in a standard configuration.
V-Belt
Pulleys
A belt with a V-shaped cross-section that fits into a matching groove in the pulley wheel. The V-shape wedges into the groove as it tightens under load, increasing grip and reducing slipping.
"Car engines use V-belts to drive accessories like the alternator and water pump — the V-shape ensures the belt does not slip even under high load."
💡 V-belt = V-shaped = wedges into groove = grips better = less slipping. Key advantage over flat belt: more efficient power transfer because it self-tightens as the load increases.
Gear
Gears
A toothed wheel fixed to a shaft that meshes (interlocks) with other gears to change the speed, torque, or direction of rotation. Because teeth mesh directly, gears cannot slip — unlike belts.
"A bicycle uses gears — a large front chainring drives a small rear sprocket, making the wheel rotate faster than the pedals with each turn."
💡 Key advantage over pulleys: no slipping (teeth interlock). Key fact: two meshed spur gears ALWAYS rotate in opposite directions. An idler gear reverses this.
Idler Gear
Gears
A gear placed between the driver and driven gear that reverses the direction of the driven gear — making driver and driven rotate in the same direction. It does not change the speed of the output.
"Adding an idler gear to a simple gear train makes the driver and driven gears rotate in the same direction, which is useful in clock mechanisms."
💡 Idler gear = middle gear = direction reverser ONLY. It does NOT affect speed or VR — the VR is still calculated from driver and driven gears only. It just changes the rotation direction.
Bevel Gear
Gears
Specialist gears with teeth cut at an angle that transmit rotary motion through 90 degrees. Two identically sized bevel gears are called mitre gears (equal input and output speed). A hand drill is a common example.
"A bevel gear in a hand drill converts the horizontal rotation of the handle into vertical rotation of the drill bit — changing the direction of motion by 90°."
💡 Bevel gear = transmits motion through 90° (a right angle). Same-size bevel gears = mitre gears (speed stays equal). Where else? Car differential, angle grinder, helicopter rotor.
Rack and Pinion
Gears
A gear system using a circular pinion gear meshing with a flat toothed rack to convert rotary motion into linear motion (or vice versa). Used in car steering systems and pillar drills.
"A car's rack and pinion steering system converts the rotary turning of the steering wheel into the linear side-to-side movement of the front wheels."
💡 Rack = flat toothed bar (linear). Pinion = circular gear (rotary). Rack and pinion converts between these two: rotary → linear (or linear → rotary). Examples: car steering, pillar drill depth stop.
Torque
Gears
A measure of a system's turning or rotational force (twisting power). A high torque means the mechanism can exert a large rotational force. When speed increases, torque decreases (and vice versa) in gear systems.
"A screwdriver with a long handle provides more torque because the increased distance from the turning axis multiplies the rotational force applied."
💡 Torque = turning power. In gears: speed up → torque goes DOWN. Slow down → torque goes UP. This is the trade-off in all gear systems — you can have speed or force, but not both at the same time.
Compound Gear Train
Gears
A gear system with more than one gear on a single shaft, allowing much higher velocity ratios than a simple gear train. The total VR is calculated by multiplying the VRs of each simple gear pair.
"A compound gear train with two pairs of gears (VR1 = 1:2, VR2 = 1:4) gives a total VR of 1:8 — the output rotates 8 times faster than the input."
💡 Compound = two or more gear pairs sharing shafts. Total VR = VR₁ × VR₂. Used when a large speed change is needed that a single pair of gears cannot achieve without being impractically large.

📍

🪝 Pulleys & Belts

Spec ref: 1.5.2 | Key question: How do pulleys transmit rotary motion and how do we calculate velocity ratio?

🎬
GIF Slot 8
Pulley system in motion
GIF showing two pulleys of different sizes connected by a belt. The smaller driven pulley should visibly rotate faster than the larger driver pulley.
🔍 Search: "pulley belt system animation" or "pulley velocity ratio animation gif"
⚙️
Driver Pulley
Connected to the motor or power source. This pulley provides the input motion. Its size determines the output speed of the driven pulley.
🔧
Driven Pulley
Connected to the output shaft. If the driven pulley is smaller than the driver, it spins faster. If larger, it spins slower.
〰️
V-Belt
V-shaped cross-section that wedges into the groove, increasing grip and preventing slipping under high loads. More efficient than a flat belt.
📐
VR Formula
VR = Driven pulley diameter ÷ Driver pulley diameter. The smaller the driven pulley, the higher the VR, the faster it spins.
💡 Direction note: In a standard pulley setup, both pulleys rotate in the same direction. If you cross the belt in a figure-of-8 pattern, the pulleys rotate in opposite directions. Gears always rotate in opposite directions without an idler.

🔩 Crank and Slider

Spec ref: 1.5.2 | Key question: How does a crank and slider convert rotary motion to reciprocating motion?

🎬
GIF Slot 9 ⭐ Very Important
Crank and slider — rotary → reciprocating
GIF showing a rotating crank arm connected to a slider via a connecting rod. As the crank rotates, the slider moves back and forth. Labels for crank, connecting rod and slider would help greatly.
🔍 Search: "crank slider mechanism animation" or "piston connecting rod crankshaft gif"
🔄
How it works
A crank (rotating arm) is connected to a slider by a connecting rod. As the crank rotates, the connecting rod pushes the slider back and forth.
📏
Size rule
The slider's maximum movement = twice the radius of the crank arm. A larger crank arm produces longer slider travel.
🚗
Applications
Car engine pistons and crankshaft; compressors; steam engines; sewing machine needle mechanism.
↔️
Reversible
The mechanism can work in reverse — the slider can drive the crank. In steam engines, steam pushes the piston (slider) which turns the wheels (crank).

🔢 Maths Corner

All the formulas you need for Topic 1.5. Each formula includes a worked example, step by step, exactly as you would need to show your working in the exam.

📌 ESL Tip — Formula First: For each formula, first learn what the letters stand for (the words behind the symbols). MA = Load ÷ Effort means nothing unless you know that Load = the output force and Effort = the input force. Write each formula in words AND letters in your notes.
⚖️ Mechanical Advantage (MA)
MA = Load ÷ Effort
Load = output force (Newtons, N)
Effort = input force (Newtons, N)
MA > 1 = machine helps you; MA < 1 = more effort than output
📝 Worked Example: Wheelbarrow
1
Load = 300 N, Effort = 50 N
2
MA = 300 ÷ 50
MA = 6
A 50 N effort can lift a 300 N load — the lever multiplies your force by 6.
📏 Velocity Ratio — Levers
VR = Distance by effort ÷ Distance by load
Both distances in the same unit (mm, cm, or m)
VR tells you how far the effort moves per unit of load movement
High VR = more mechanical advantage but effort moves further
📝 Worked Example: Wheelbarrow
1
Effort distance = 800 mm, Load distance = 100 mm
2
VR = 800 ÷ 100
VR = 8
📊 Efficiency
Efficiency = (MA ÷ VR) × 100%
Always a percentage (%). No mechanism is 100% efficient — friction always steals some energy. A higher % = less energy wasted.
📝 Worked Example: Wheelbarrow (MA=6, VR=8)
1
Efficiency = (6 ÷ 8) × 100%
2
= 0.75 × 100%
Efficiency = 75%
25% of input energy is lost to friction.
🪝 Velocity Ratio — Pulleys
VR = Driven diameter ÷ Driver diameter
Driven = the output pulley (connected to what you want to spin)
Driver = the input pulley (connected to the motor)
If driven < driver → VR < 1 → output spins FASTER
📝 Worked Example
1
Driver = 60 mm, Driven = 20 mm
2
VR = 20 ÷ 60 = 1/3
VR = 1:3
Output spins 3× faster than input.
⚙️ Velocity Ratio — Gears
VR = Driven teeth ÷ Driver teeth
Output speed = Input speed ÷ VR
Driver = gear connected to the motor (input)
Driven = gear on the output shaft
📝 Worked Example: Simple gear train
1
Driver = 32 teeth, Driven = 16 teeth
2
VR = 16 ÷ 32 = 1/2 = 1:2
3
If input = 100 rpm → Output = 100 ÷ (1/2) = 200 rpm
Output speed = 200 rpm
🔩 Rack & Pinion — Movement
Movement = (Pinion teeth ÷ TPM) × 1 metre
TPM = Teeth Per Metre on the rack
This gives the distance the rack moves for each full revolution of the pinion gear.
📝 Worked Example
1
Pinion = 60 teeth, Rack TPM = 200
2
= (60 ÷ 200) × 1 m = 0.3 m
Rack moves 300 mm per revolution

1. Oscillating motion and reciprocating motion are exactly the same — both move back and forth.
❌ False. They are similar but NOT the same. Both go back and forth, BUT: reciprocating motion follows a straight line (like a piston or sewing needle), whereas oscillating motion follows a curved arc (like a pendulum or swing). This distinction is worth 1 mark in the exam.
2. In a Class 2 lever, the fulcrum is positioned at one end, the effort at the other, and the load is in the middle.
✅ True. In a Class 2 lever, the order from one end to the other is: Fulcrum → Load → Effort. The load is always in the middle. A wheelbarrow is the classic example — the wheel is the fulcrum, the load sits in the barrow (middle), and the handles are where you apply effort (at the far end).
3. Adding an idler gear between a driver and driven gear makes the output speed twice as fast.
❌ False. An idler gear only changes the direction of rotation — it does NOT change the speed or velocity ratio at all. The VR is still calculated only from the driver and driven gears. The idler gear simply makes the driver and driven gear rotate in the same direction instead of opposite directions.
4. A mechanism with a Mechanical Advantage of 8 and a Velocity Ratio of 10 has an efficiency of 80%.
✅ True. Efficiency = (MA ÷ VR) × 100% = (8 ÷ 10) × 100% = 80%. This means 80% of the input energy is converted into useful output energy, and 20% is lost to friction. The mechanism is reasonably efficient but not perfect — no real mechanism achieves 100% efficiency.
5. In a standard pulley system, the driver and driven pulleys rotate in opposite directions — one clockwise, one anticlockwise.
❌ False. This is a common confusion with gears! In a standard pulley system, both pulleys rotate in the same direction — because the belt connects their top surfaces. Gears (without an idler) rotate in opposite directions. If you cross the belt in a figure-of-8, the pulleys then rotate in opposite directions — but this is not the standard configuration.
6. A drop (snail) cam is best suited to applications that require a slow, smooth rise followed by a sudden fall — such as a hammer or punch mechanism.
✅ True. The snail (drop) cam's spiral cross-section creates a very gradual rise as the cam rotates, then a sudden, rapid drop at one point in the cycle. This is exactly the motion needed for hammers, hole punches, and timing mechanisms where a controlled build-up followed by an abrupt strike is required.

Q1 · Types of Movement · 1 mark1 mark
State the type of movement of a car travelling along a motorway.
💡 Language hint: For a "State" question, just name the type. One word or short phrase is enough — no explanation needed.
1
Linear motion. The car moves in a straight line in one direction along the motorway.
✏️ Note: If the car goes around a bend, it would be oscillating. If the question says "along a straight motorway" → always linear.
Q2 · Levers · 2 marks2 marks
Explain how you can create a larger output movement from a reverse motion linkage.
💡 Language hint: Explain = what + why. Think about where the pivot is and how moving it changes the output. Use: "If the pivot is moved... this causes... because..."
1
To create a larger output movement from a reverse motion linkage, the fixed pivot point (fulcrum) should be moved closer to the input (effort) end of the linkage.
2
This increases the mechanical advantage because the distance from the pivot to the output side becomes greater than the distance to the input side, so the output arm travels a larger distance for the same input movement.
✏️ Key words: pivot position, mechanical advantage, distance from fulcrum, output arm.
Q3 · Cam Followers · 2 marks2 marks
Explain the movement of a follower while the cam is at the dwell stage of its rotation.
💡 Language hint: This says "explain" — give the what (what the follower does) AND why (what property of the cam causes this). Think about what "dwell" means.
1
During the dwell stage, the follower remains completely stationary — it does not rise or fall.
2
This is because during the dwell phase, the part of the cam surface in contact with the follower is at a constant, equal distance from the centre of rotation. Since the cam profile does not change height in this section, the follower has no movement to follow and stays still.
Q4 · Gears Calculation · 3 marks3 marks
A designer needs an input speed of 800 rpm and an output speed of 3200 rpm. If the driven gear has 20 teeth, calculate how many teeth the driver gear needs.
💡 Maths hint: Start with VR = Output speed ÷ Input speed, then rearrange: Driver teeth = Driven teeth ÷ VR. Show all three steps of working.
1
Step 1 — Find the VR: VR = Input speed ÷ Output speed = 800 ÷ 3200 = 1/4 = 1:4
2
Step 2 — Use VR formula: VR = Driven teeth ÷ Driver teeth → 1/4 = 20 ÷ Driver teeth → Driver teeth = 20 × 4
3
Step 3 — Answer: Driver gear needs 80 teeth.
Check: VR = 20 ÷ 80 = 1:4. Output = 800 ÷ (1/4) = 3200 rpm ✓
Q5 · Crank and Slider · 3 marks3 marks
Calculate the length of the crank arm used in a crank and slider mechanism if the slider has a maximum movement of 30 mm.
💡 Maths hint: Remember the key rule: slider movement = 2 × radius of crank arm. So crank arm radius = slider movement ÷ 2.
1
Rule: Maximum slider movement = 2 × crank arm radius (length)
2
Rearrange: Crank arm length = Maximum slider movement ÷ 2 = 30 ÷ 2
3
Answer: Crank arm length = 15 mm
