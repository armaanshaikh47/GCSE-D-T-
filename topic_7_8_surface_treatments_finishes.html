Key Vocabulary

12 key terms for Topic 7.8. The most important distinction is between finishes that SOAK IN (stain, oil, wax) and finishes that coat the SURFACE (paint, varnish, shellac). This determines how the finish wears and what happens when it is damaged.

📌 ESL Strategy — Two Columns: Draw two columns on paper: "Soaks into wood" and "Coats the surface." Place each finish in the correct column. This simple categorisation gives you the key to understanding how each finish behaves — and why some are more durable than others.
Filter:
Porous
Properties
A material that has tiny holes or gaps (pores) in its surface that allow liquids and gases to pass through or be absorbed. Timber is porous — without a finish it absorbs water, grease, and dirt, becoming stained, swollen, or damaged quickly.
"Because wood is porous, a surface finish is essential for any timber product that will be handled frequently or exposed to moisture — otherwise the wood quickly becomes discoloured and structurally weakened."
💡 Porous = full of tiny holes (pores). Like a sponge — it absorbs liquids. This is WHY we apply finishes. A finish either fills the pores (oil, wax, stain) or covers the surface with a protective layer (paint, varnish, shellac).
Functional Finish
Properties
A finish applied primarily to protect the timber and improve its performance — making it waterproof, easier to clean, resistant to scratches and chemicals, or more durable. The protective function is the main reason for its use.
"Oil is used on a kitchen worktop primarily as a functional finish — it soaks into the wood fibres and makes the surface waterproof and resistant to food stains, extending the life of the worktop significantly."
💡 Functional = it DOES something useful (protects, waterproofs, hardens). Examples: oil on a kitchen chopping board (waterproofs + food-safe), paint on exterior wood (protects from weather), varnish on a floor (protects against heavy foot traffic).
Aesthetic Finish
Properties
A finish applied primarily to improve the appearance of the timber — changing its colour, adding shine, revealing the grain, or creating a particular visual style. The decorative effect is the main reason for its use.
"Staining was chosen as an aesthetic finish for the pine shelves — it darkened the pale pine to resemble more expensive oak, at a fraction of the cost, while still allowing the natural grain pattern to show through."
💡 Aesthetic = how it LOOKS (from Greek 'aisthētikos' = relating to perception). Examples: shellac on expensive furniture (glossy lustre), staining pine to look like oak (colour change), wax on natural timber (warm natural appearance). Most finishes serve BOTH functional and aesthetic purposes.
Varnish
Surface Finish
A clear or slightly tinted liquid coating that dries to form a hard, protective surface layer over the timber. Unlike paint, it does not hide the grain — the natural wood pattern remains visible. Available in high gloss (very shiny) or matt (flat, non-reflective) finishes.
"The oak dining table was given two coats of matt varnish — the clear finish enhanced the natural grain pattern while providing a hardwearing, easy-to-clean surface that protects the wood from everyday use."
💡 Varnish = clear coat that shows grain + protects. Advantage: hardwearing, shows grain, range of sheen levels. Disadvantage: can scratch or chip, exposing unprotected wood beneath — once the surface film is broken, moisture can get underneath and lift the remaining varnish.
Shellac / French Polish
Surface Finish
A cloudy liquid made from resin secreted by the lac beetle, dissolved in methylated spirits. Applied in many thin layers with a cloth pad (a "rubber") and polished between each coat to build up a deep, glass-like glossy surface known as French polish. Very skilled and time-consuming to apply.
"The Victorian mahogany writing desk was finished with French polish — many thin layers of shellac rubbed on with a pad and carefully polished created the deep, mirror-like lustre characteristic of high-quality 19th-century furniture."
💡 Shellac = luxury, traditional, very glossy finish. Made from BEETLE secretion (unusual — remember this detail). Advantage: extremely beautiful high-gloss lustre. Disadvantage: easily damaged by water (rings from wet glasses) and heat (hot plates). Used only on fine furniture kept away from heat and moisture.
Solvent Fumes
Health & Safety
Gases released by solvent-based finishes (varnish, shellac, contact adhesive, some stains and paints) as they dry. These fumes are harmful to the respiratory system (lungs and airways) if inhaled and may cause dizziness, headaches, or longer-term health damage with prolonged exposure.
"When applying solvent-based varnish in the workshop, ensure all windows and doors are open for maximum ventilation, wear an appropriate respirator mask, and never use near open flames — the vapour is highly flammable."
💡 Solvent = a liquid that dissolves other substances (carries the finish). Solvent fumes = the gas that evaporates from the liquid finish as it dries. Safety measures: VENTILATION (open windows/doors), PPE (respirator mask, gloves), NO FLAMES (solvent vapours are often flammable).

Why Surface Finishes Are Essential

🛡️ Functional Reasons

  • Wood is porous — it absorbs moisture which causes swelling, warping, and splitting
  • Grease and dirt soak into unfinished timber, leaving permanent stains
  • Unprotected outdoor timber rots quickly from rain, frost, and UV light
  • A finish creates a protective barrier that is easier to wipe clean
  • Some finishes (tanalising) penetrate deep into the wood to resist rot and insect attack

🎨 Aesthetic Reasons

  • A finish can dramatically change the colour of timber (staining pine to look like oak)
  • Varnish and shellac enhance the natural grain and add depth and lustre
  • Paint covers the grain completely, allowing any colour to be applied
  • Wax gives a warm, natural appearance that many designers prefer for quality furniture
  • The sheen level (high gloss, satin, matt) creates different visual moods
💡 Preparation is essential — and often forgotten in exam answers: Before applying ANY finish, timber must be prepared. (1) Sand progressively: P80 (coarse, removes saw marks) → P120 (medium) → P240 (fine). Always sand in the direction of the grain. (2) Apply knotting solution to any knots in softwood — this prevents resin from bleeding through paint or stain. (3) Apply grain filler to open-grained hardwoods (oak, ash) before painting or polishing, or the finish will sink into the vessels.

Product Application Guide — Which Finish for Which Product?

The exam will always give a specific product context. Use the criteria below to select and justify the most appropriate finish.

🍽️ Kitchen worktop / chopping board

✓ Best: Oil (vegetable)
Soaks in and waterproofs; food-safe (vegetable oil is non-toxic); easy to reapply. Grain visible and attractive.
✗ Not paint/varnish — these may chip into food; not shellac — damaged by heat and water.

🛋️ Living room furniture (visible grain)

✓ Best: Varnish (satin) or Wax
Varnish: hardwearing, shows grain, easy to clean. Wax: warm natural appearance, easy to touch up. Both preserve the aesthetic of the wood.
✗ Not paint — hides grain. Not shellac — damaged by everyday heat and moisture from drinks.

🏡 Exterior garden furniture

✓ Best: Paint or Oil
Paint: forms a waterproof barrier against rain; UV pigments protect against sun bleaching. Oil: soaks in and protects wood fibres from within; can be reapplied without stripping.
✗ Not shellac — destroyed by rain. Not wax — washes off quickly outdoors.

🖼️ High-quality antique-style furniture

✓ Best: Shellac (French polish)
Creates the deep, mirror-like lustre traditionally associated with fine furniture. The aesthetic effect is impossible to replicate with other finishes.
✗ Not suitable for everyday use — too easily damaged. Only appropriate where the furniture will be carefully maintained.

🪟 Interior doors and skirting boards

✓ Best: Paint (gloss)
Protects against everyday knocks and dirt; wide colour choice to match interior décor; gloss paint easy to wipe clean; hides grain (appropriate for joinery, not furniture).
✗ Not varnish — looks wrong on painted joinery. Not wax — insufficient protection.

🌲 Pine shelves to look more expensive

✓ Best: Stain then Varnish
Stain darkens the pale pine to resemble oak or walnut (aesthetic). Varnish on top provides the protective hardwearing coat the stain cannot provide alone.
✗ Stain alone is not enough — it provides minimal protection and needs a top coat.

⚠️ Health & Safety When Applying Finishes

Ventilation

  • All solvent-based finishes (varnish, shellac, oil-based paint) release harmful fumes as they dry
  • Always apply in a well-ventilated space — open all windows and doors
  • Use extraction fans in professional workshops
  • Water-based finishes (acrylic paint, water-based varnish) produce fewer fumes but still require ventilation

Fire Risk

  • Solvent vapours are highly flammable — never apply solvent-based finishes near open flames, sparks, or heat sources
  • Oil-soaked rags can self-ignite (spontaneous combustion) — soak in water and dispose of carefully in a metal bin
  • Keep lids on all finish containers when not in use to reduce vapour build-up

Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)

  • Wear chemical-resistant gloves when handling solvent-based finishes — solvents are absorbed through skin
  • Wear a respirator mask (not a dust mask) when applying spray finishes or solvent-based products
  • Wear goggles when spraying to prevent droplets reaching eyes

Timber Preparation Safety

  • MDF dust is extremely harmful — always wear a P3 dust mask when sanding MDF
  • Many tropical hardwood dusts are toxic — e.g. mahogany dust causes respiratory issues
  • Use dust extraction when machine sanding — never sand without extraction in a workshop
  • Wear safety goggles when using power sanders — dust particles can cause serious eye damage

Activity 3 — True or False?

Test understanding
📝 These statements test the most common exam misconceptions about surface finishes. Read each explanation carefully.
1. Staining a piece of pine makes it look exactly like mahogany — after staining, no one can tell the difference.
❌ False — and this is specifically noted in the textbook. Staining changes the colour of pine to be darker and closer to the tone of mahogany or oak, but it does not look exactly like another wood. The reason is that the stain soaks into the grain of the PINE — so the pine's distinctive grain pattern is still visible through the colour. A trained eye can always identify the original timber species from its grain structure, even after staining. This is the key disadvantage of staining compared to veneering (which actually uses the real wood on the surface).
2. Varnish is a better choice than shellac (French polish) for a kitchen dining table because varnish is more hardwearing and resistant to water and heat.
✅ True. Shellac (French polish) is easily damaged by water (causing white ring marks from wet glasses) and heat (causing the finish to cloud and blister). A kitchen dining table is regularly exposed to both — hot dishes and glasses of cold drinks with condensation. Varnish forms a much harder, more resilient surface film that is more resistant to both. French polish is reserved for high-quality furniture kept in controlled conditions, not everyday dining tables.
3. A veneered surface does not need any further finish applied to it because the veneer is real wood and already provides a complete surface.
❌ False — this is explicitly stated as a disadvantage of veneering in the textbook. The veneer is a very thin slice of natural wood, and natural wood is porous. Without a surface finish, the veneer will absorb moisture, grease, and dirt just like any other piece of bare timber. A veneered surface still needs varnish, wax, oil, or shellac applied on top to protect it and complete the finish. The veneer only provides the aesthetic appearance of expensive timber — the protective finish must come from a separate coating.
4. Oil is the most appropriate finish for a solid oak garden bench that will be left outdoors year-round.
✅ True — oil is one of the best finishes for outdoor timber. Outdoor furniture is constantly exposed to rain, frost, UV radiation, and temperature changes. Oil (teak oil or Danish oil) soaks deep into the wood fibres and waterproofs them from within — it does not form a surface film that can chip, crack, or peel like varnish or paint. When the oil eventually wears out, a new coat can simply be applied without any stripping or sanding back of the old finish. Paint is also acceptable, but once paint chips, moisture enters under the film. Oil is often preferred for quality outdoor hardwood furniture.
5. You do not need to wear any personal protective equipment (PPE) when applying water-based paint or varnish because water-based products contain no harmful chemicals.
❌ False — water-based finishes still require appropriate PPE. While water-based products produce fewer and less toxic fumes than solvent-based finishes, they are not completely harmless. They still contain biocides (to prevent mould growth in the can), pigments, and other additives that can irritate skin and eyes. Gloves should still be worn to prevent skin sensitisation with repeated exposure. Additionally, even water-based spray finishes require respiratory protection because fine droplets of any liquid, including water-based paint, can cause lung irritation if inhaled continuously. Good ventilation is always required regardless of the product type.

Exam Language & Practice

Surface finish questions almost always involve justifying a choice for a specific product context. The three-part answer structure — finish name + key property + why that property matters for THIS product — is essential for full marks.

📌 ESL Strategy — Property → Consequence → Context: Before writing your answer, identify three things from the question: (1) what property does the product NEED (waterproof? colourful? shows grain? hardwearing?), (2) which finish PROVIDES that property, (3) WHY that property specifically matters for THAT product context. Write the answer in that order.
Justifying a finish choice — 3 marks
[Finish] would be the most appropriate finish for [product] because [key property]. This is important because [why that property is needed for this product]. A disadvantage is that [honest limitation].
"Oil would be the most appropriate finish for a solid oak kitchen chopping board because it soaks into the wood fibres and provides waterproofing from within without forming a surface film. This is important because a chopping board is repeatedly exposed to water during washing, and oil's waterproofing prevents the timber swelling and cracking. Furthermore, vegetable oil is food-safe and non-toxic — essential for a surface that comes into direct contact with food. A disadvantage is that the surface retains a slightly oily feel even after drying."
Comparing two finishes for a product — 4 marks
While [finish A] would [advantage of A], [finish B] would be more appropriate for this product because [why B is better for this context]. However, a disadvantage of [B] is that [honest limitation].
"While painting would provide good weather protection and a wide colour choice, oil would be more appropriate for this garden teak table because it soaks into the wood rather than forming a surface film that can chip and peel in outdoor conditions — once the film of paint breaks, moisture enters beneath and accelerates deterioration. However, a disadvantage of oil is that it requires reapplication every year or two, whereas paint lasts longer before needing maintenance if it remains intact."

📝 Practice Questions

Q1 · Choose a Finish · 4 marks4 marks
A designer has made a small side table from solid pine. The client wants the table to look like it is made from walnut, while still showing the wood grain. Identify the most appropriate surface treatment and explain two reasons why it is suitable.
💡 The client wants: (1) colour change, (2) grain still visible. Which finish does both? Then two separate "because..." explanations, each earning 1 mark.
1
Finish: Staining (followed by a clear top coat such as varnish or wax).
2
Reason 1: Staining soaks into the wood surface and changes its colour to a darker, richer brown that resembles walnut — this directly achieves the client's desire for a more expensive-looking timber at significantly lower cost than solid walnut.
3
Reason 2: Unlike paint, a stain does not cover the surface with an opaque film — the natural grain pattern of the pine remains visible through the stain, fulfilling the client's requirement that the grain should still show. The resulting appearance resembles a walnut-grained surface, even though the pine grain is still what is actually visible.
Note: a good answer would add that staining alone provides minimal protection and should be sealed with a varnish or wax top coat.
Q2 · Functional vs Aesthetic · 3 marks3 marks
Explain the difference between a functional surface finish and an aesthetic surface finish, giving one example of each.
💡 Define functional (what it DOES for the wood) and aesthetic (how it looks). Give one specific finish for each with a brief justification. Do not use the same finish for both.
1
Functional finish: A finish applied primarily to PROTECT the timber and improve its performance — making it waterproof, resistant to dirt, or more durable. Example: oil applied to a garden bench — it soaks into the wood fibres and waterproofs them from within, preventing rain damage and extending the product's lifespan.
2
Aesthetic finish: A finish applied primarily to IMPROVE THE APPEARANCE of the timber — changing its colour, adding lustre, or revealing the grain pattern. Example: shellac (French polish) applied to a mahogany writing desk — the many layers build up to a deep, mirror-like gloss that enhances the beauty of the grain, making it a premium visual statement entirely for aesthetic effect.
3
Important note: In practice, most finishes serve BOTH purposes simultaneously — varnish is both hardwearing (functional) and attractive (aesthetic). The distinction is about which purpose is PRIMARY for that application.
Q3 · Health & Safety · 3 marks3 marks
Identify three health and safety measures a student should take when applying a solvent-based varnish in a school workshop.
💡 Three separate, specific measures — not vague ("be careful"). Think: fumes, fire, skin, eyes. Each measure should name the hazard AND the control.
1
Ventilation: Open all windows and doors in the workshop before beginning. Solvent-based varnish releases harmful vapours as it dries — without ventilation, these accumulate to a concentration that can cause dizziness, headaches, and respiratory damage.
2
PPE — Gloves: Wear chemical-resistant (nitrile) gloves throughout. The solvents in varnish are absorbed through the skin with repeated contact, causing skin sensitisation and possible longer-term health effects.
3
Fire precautions: Remove all ignition sources (lighters, Bunsen burners, power tools that create sparks) from the working area before opening the varnish. Solvent vapours are highly flammable — a spark or flame can cause a flash fire. Any oil-soaked rags must be stored in a sealed metal container or soaked in water — oil-soaked rags can self-ignite through spontaneous combustion.

"Varnish is a type of paint""Varnish is a clear coating — it shows the natural grain. Paint is opaque — it covers the grain completely. They are different products with different effects."Varnish ≠ paint. The key difference is transparency vs opacity
"Use shellac for the garden table""Shellac is not suitable for outdoor use — it is easily damaged by water and heat. Oil or exterior paint would be more appropriate for outdoor timber."Shellac fails outdoors — this is one of the most common mistakes in finish selection
"Apply the finish directly to the wood without any preparation""Before applying any finish: sand progressively P80→P120→P240, apply knotting solution to knots in softwood, and use grain filler on open-grained hardwoods"Preparation is a required step — forgetting to mention it loses marks on "describe the finishing process" questions
"Veneered furniture doesn't need a finish""The veneer is natural wood and therefore porous — it still requires a surface finish such as varnish, wax, or oil to be applied on top to protect it"Veneer ≠ complete finish. This is explicitly stated in the spec and frequently tested
"Water-based paint is safe so no PPE is needed""Even water-based finishes require gloves to prevent skin sensitisation and ventilation — all finishes contain additives that can irritate skin and respiratory system with repeated exposure"PPE is always required — the type varies, but never "no PPE at all"
