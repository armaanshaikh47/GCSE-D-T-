Mechanical Properties
Properties
Characteristics of a material that describe how it responds to external forces or loads. They determine whether a material will bend, break, stretch, or stay rigid under stress. Essential for selecting materials for structural components.
"The designer selected aluminium for the bicycle frame because of its mechanical properties — specifically its high strength-to-weight ratio, meaning it resists the forces of cycling without adding excessive mass to the frame."
💡 Mechanical = about force and movement. Examples: strength, hardness, toughness, ductility, elasticity. These properties determine whether a material will SURVIVE the forces it will experience in use. Contrast with physical properties (measured without applying force).
Physical Properties
Properties
Characteristics of a material that can be defined and measured without applying force, such as colour, size, weight, density, electrical conductivity, and thermal conductivity. They determine how a material looks and how it behaves with electricity, heat, or light.
"Copper is used for electrical wiring because of its physical property of high electrical conductivity — it allows electric current to flow through it with minimal resistance."
💡 Physical = can be measured without breaking or deforming the material. Examples: density, conductivity, colour, magnetism. Some overlap with mechanical (hardness is sometimes both), but physical properties are generally about measurement, not force response.
Prototype
Properties
A full-size, three-dimensional model of the finished design, created for testing before mass production begins. A prototype is made using the same (or similar) manufacturing processes that will be used for the final product, so that material and process choices can be tested and refined.
"Before committing to mass production, the company produced a prototype of the new kettle — testing its heat resistance, electrical safety, ergonomics, and aesthetic appearance with real users before finalising the manufacturing specification."
💡 Proto = first (Greek). A prototype is the FIRST version made before production. Key difference from a model: a prototype is full-size and made using actual manufacturing processes. A scale model might just show appearance. Prototypes test EVERYTHING — materials, processes, ergonomics, safety.
Life-Cycle Analysis (LCA)
Environmental
A systematic method for assessing the total environmental impact of a product at every stage of its life — from extraction of raw materials, through manufacturing and use, to disposal. The LCA reveals which stages produce the most environmental impact and where improvements can be made.
"The LCA for the plastic bottle revealed that the most significant environmental impact came not from manufacturing, but from the fossil fuels burned during transportation to retail stores — prompting the company to redesign for local production."
💡 LCA = counting ALL environmental costs across ALL stages. Stages: extraction → transport → processing → manufacture → transport → distribution → USE → disposal/recovery. LCA makes hidden impacts visible — e.g. the transport stage is often the biggest impact, not the manufacturing stage.
Green Design
Environmental
Designing products and systems with minimal environmental impact, without compromising function, quality, or performance. Includes designing for energy efficiency, using renewable or biodegradable materials, minimising waste, and enabling recycling and disassembly.
"The designer used a green design approach for the new packaging — choosing recycled cardboard (renewable, biodegradable) over single-use plastic, and designing the box shape to nest together during transport, reducing the number of delivery vehicles needed."
💡 Five green design strategies: (1) energy efficiency in use, (2) less waste/fewer materials, (3) renewable energy in manufacture, (4) non-toxic/recyclable/reusable materials, (5) biodegradable materials. The exam often asks for "two ways a designer can promote green design" — use these five.
Fairtrade Foundation
Social
An organisation that tackles poverty and injustice in global trade by ensuring that farmers and producers in developing countries receive a fair, guaranteed price for their goods. It also works to improve working conditions and prevent child or enforced labour and discrimination by gender.
"The chocolate manufacturer chose to use Fairtrade certified cocoa — ensuring that the farmers in Ghana who grew the cocoa beans were paid above the market rate, could invest in their communities, and worked in safe conditions."
💡 Fairtrade = fair payment to farmers + safe working conditions + no child or forced labour + no gender discrimination. Products with the Fairtrade mark include coffee, chocolate, bananas, cotton. The premium paid goes into a community development fund controlled by the farmers.
Carbon Offsetting
Environmental
A strategy that allows companies or individuals to compensate for their carbon dioxide emissions by funding activities elsewhere that absorb or prevent an equivalent amount of CO₂ — such as planting trees, installing renewable energy, or protecting forests. This can make an organisation "carbon neutral."
"The airline company used carbon offsetting to compensate for the CO₂ emissions of its flights — for every tonne of CO₂ emitted, it funded the planting of trees in South America that would absorb an equivalent amount of carbon over their lifetimes."
💡 Carbon offset = compensate (balance out) your emissions by supporting something that removes or prevents the same amount of CO₂ elsewhere. Examples: planting trees, funding solar panels in developing countries, encouraging staff to cycle to work. Criticism: it doesn't reduce YOUR emissions — it just balances them.
Design for Manufacture (DFM)
Manufacturing
A design approach that simplifies a product so that it can be manufactured more easily, quickly, and cheaply. DFM techniques include using standardised parts, simplifying or making processes repeatable, making the design modular, and designing for disassembly, servicing, and recycling.
"The new blender was redesigned using DFM — the number of unique parts was reduced from 47 to 23, all screws were standardised to the same size, and the housing was designed with snap-fit clips rather than screws to speed up assembly and disassembly."
💡 DFM = making the product easier and cheaper to manufacture. Six DFM strategies: (1) standardised parts, (2) simplify/repeat processes, (3) reduce complexity/make modular, (4) simple quality control tests, (5) reduce tolerances, (6) design for disassembly. All reduce cost per unit.
Modular Design
Manufacturing
A design approach where a product is built from standardised, interchangeable components (modules) of standard sizes that can be combined in different ways. Each module works independently and can be replaced or upgraded without affecting the rest of the product.
"The office furniture system used modular design — the same standard desk top, leg, and drawer unit modules could be combined in dozens of different configurations, allowing each office to customise its layout without needing bespoke components."
💡 Modular = made from modules (standard blocks). Like LEGO — each piece is standard but can be combined in many ways. Advantages: parts are interchangeable; one broken module is replaced, not the whole product; modules can be upgraded independently; reduces inventory of spare parts.

1.13 — Using Material Properties to Inform Design Decisions

Topic 1.13 is about how designers select appropriate materials by understanding mechanical and physical properties, and how to justify those choices.

📌 ESL Strategy — Properties as Answers: Every "Explain why [material] is used for [product]" question requires you to name a SPECIFIC property and explain what that property DOES for the product. Don't say "steel is strong" — say "mild steel has high tensile strength, which means it can resist the pulling forces acting on the bicycle frame without stretching or breaking." The specific mechanism is what earns the mark.

📊 Mechanical & Physical Properties (Table 1.13.1)

You must know and be able to apply these properties. The exam will give you a product and ask which properties are most important — and why.

Mechanical Properties — Respond to Force

Physical Properties — Measured Without Force

PropertyDefinition
DensityCompactness of a material — defined as mass per unit volume (kg/m³). High density = heavy for its size
Electrical conductivityAbility to conduct (allow the flow of) electricity. Copper has very high conductivity — ideal for wiring
Thermal conductivityAbility to conduct (transfer) heat. Metals are good conductors; polymers are poor conductors (insulators)

CorrosionThe process by which a metal is eaten away by reaction with oxygen and water. Iron and steel rust; aluminium oxidises but forms a protective layer
AestheticsThe appearance of the material — including colour, texture, grain pattern, sheen, and visual appeal

💡 Brittleness vs Toughness: These are OPPOSITES. Brittle materials break suddenly without bending (glass, cast iron, urea formaldehyde). Tough materials absorb impact energy and deform before breaking (mild steel, nylon, polycarbonate). The exam often tests whether you understand which is desirable for a specific product.

🎯 Elements Analysed When Selecting Materials (Figure 1.13.3)

Before choosing a material, manufacturers analyse 8 factors beyond just the material properties.

📦 Available Stock Forms

What shapes and sizes is the material available in? (sheets, rods, tubes, sections) Designers use standard stock sizes to avoid waste and reduce cutting.

⚗️ Mechanical & Physical Properties

Does the material have the required strength, hardness, conductivity etc. to perform its function safely and reliably throughout the product's life?

💰 Cost & Availability

Is the material affordable in the required quantities? Is it readily available from suppliers, or are lead times long? Material cost directly affects product price.

🔧 Tools, Equipment & Processes

Does the school/factory have the tools and machines needed to shape, cut, and join this material? Can it be processed with available equipment?

⚡ Energy Demands

How much energy does the construction process require? High-energy processes (e.g. melting metal) increase costs and environmental impact.

🏭 Scale of Production

Is the scale of production (one-off, batch, mass) compatible with the material and process? Some materials are only economical at large volumes.

♻️ Level of Waste

How much material is wasted during processing? Wastage adds cost. Designing to standard sizes or using additive manufacturing reduces waste.

✂️ Ease of Processing

Is the material easy to cut, shape, join, and finish? Complex processing increases time, cost, and the chance of defects.

Justifying Material and Process Choices

A manufacturer can justify its material and process choices in several ways — the exam may ask you to "explain how a manufacturer can justify its choice of material."

🧪 Test Pieces

Produce sample pieces from the material and test them to prove that the material has the required mechanical and physical properties under real conditions.

🔲 Prototypes

Build a full-size, 3D prototype using the same manufacturing processes planned for production — tests how well the material and processes work together in the final product context.

💷 Economic Case

Produce a detailed cost analysis: material cost + labour + energy + waste + transportation + hidden costs. Prove the choice makes commercial sense at the required scale of production.

🌍 Environmental Evaluation

Evaluate the materials and production methods in relation to environmental and social impact — using LCA data, recycled content percentages, and sustainability certifications.

🔗 Mixed Materials — Why Combine Different Materials?

Most real products use multiple materials — each chosen for a specific property that no single material can provide alone.

💡 Key exam question pattern: "Explain why [material] is used for [specific part] of the product." Your answer must name the specific property AND explain what it does for that part — not for the product as a whole. The beech is hard (not "the chair is good") — the hardness of the beech specifically prevents the chair legs from being scratched by floor contact.

1.14 — Challenges that Influence the Processes of Design and Making

Topic 1.14 covers the broader factors that constrain and influence design decisions — social responsibility, environmental impact, human capabilities, cost, and manufacturing efficiency.

📌 ESL Strategy — Connect to the Real World: All the concepts in 1.14 are visible in real products around you right now. Your phone was designed with a specific social group in mind. The packaging on your food may have a Fairtrade mark. The chair you're sitting on may have been manufactured using DFM principles. Connecting each concept to something you can see or touch makes abstract ideas concrete and memorable.

👥 Respect for Social, Ethnic & Economic Groups

All designers must consider who will use their product and ensure it is appropriate, respectful, and accessible to different groups of people.

👥 Social Groups

Share a common social aspect such as education level, age, or gender. Designs should incorporate the specific needs and desires of the target social group.

Example: phones designed for young people incorporate large screens and fast processors for easy access to social media, games, and video calling — features prioritised for that social group's usage patterns.

🌍 Ethnic Groups

Share a common cultural background — ancestry, homeland, dialect, and cultural heritage. Products must be sympathetic to that culture and must not offend through insensitive use of symbols, clothing representations, or labels.

Example: care must be taken when using cultural symbols, patterns, clothing references, or colour associations in product design — meanings vary significantly between cultures and an insensitive choice can cause serious offence and commercial damage.

💰 Economic Groups

Share similar income levels. Different economic groups have different purchasing power and may be driven by cost, quality, or brand prestige when choosing products.

Example: kettles are available from a basic, cheap functional model (~£15) to premium designer versions (~£200) — both boil water, but target completely different economic groups with different priorities (cost vs aesthetic quality vs brand).

Environmental, Social & Economic Initiatives

🌿 Fairtrade Foundation

Tackles poverty and injustice across the global supply chain. Ensures farmers and producers in developing countries receive a fair, guaranteed price. Prevents child or forced labour and gender discrimination. Products include coffee, chocolate, cotton, bananas.

🌳 Carbon Offsetting

Companies compensate for their carbon emissions by funding activities that absorb CO₂ (tree planting) or prevent emissions elsewhere (renewable energy). Encourages staff to walk, cycle, or use public transport rather than drive. Aim: carbon neutral status.

🔧 Product Disassembly

Design products so they can be easily taken apart at end of life — enabling parts to be recycled or reused. Reduce the number of components. Label each part by material type. Design for disassembly (DfD) extends product life and reduces landfill.

📜 Disposal of Waste

Governed by laws at international, EU, national, and local levels. Laws control the collection, transportation, recovery, and disposal of waste to minimise environmental harm. Producers are increasingly responsible for the end-of-life disposal of their products (Extended Producer Responsibility).

🌱 Environmental Design — Green Design, Recycling & LCA

Global warming and rising energy costs have pushed environmental factors to the centre of design practice. A designer who ignores environmental impact is increasingly viewed as professionally negligent.

Five Green Design Strategies

⚡ Energy Efficiency

Design the product to use as little energy as possible during use. Example: LED lighting uses 90% less electricity than traditional incandescent bulbs for the same light output.

📉 Less Waste / Fewer Materials

Reduce the amount of material used in the product (lightweighting). Design parts to minimise cutting waste. Use standard stock sizes to avoid off-cuts.

🌞 Renewable Energy

Use renewable energy sources (solar, wind, hydro) or more efficient energy in the manufacturing process. Carbon footprint of manufacturing is significantly reduced.

♻️ Recyclable Materials

Use non-toxic, recyclable, or reusable materials and components. Make it easy to separate different materials at end of life. Label all materials clearly.

🌱 Biodegradable Materials

Use materials that decompose naturally (paper, cardboard, PLA bioplastic, natural textiles) instead of persistent plastics that remain in the environment for hundreds of years.

Life-Cycle Analysis (LCA)

An LCA systematically counts the environmental impact at every single stage of a product's life — not just manufacturing. This often reveals surprising results: for many products, the USE phase produces more impact than the manufacturing phase.

🏭 Human Capability, Cost of Materials & Design for Manufacture

These three factors constrain how products are designed — every design decision affects how easily a product can be made, how much it costs, and whether real people can use it safely.

🧠 Human Capability

A product must meet the needs of its users AND operate within their physical and cognitive capabilities. Any product that stretches the capabilities of its users is likely to be unsafe.

Example: if the controls on an electric heater are unclear or difficult to operate, there is a risk of accidental burns. Simple design changes — larger text, clearer icons, colour-coded buttons — can dramatically reduce accidents.

💷 True Cost of Materials

The real cost of a material is not just its purchase price. It includes:

• Ongoing maintenance costs
• Transportation costs
• Recycling and disposal costs at end of life
• Environmental cost — damage to landscape, emissions from conversion, energy in production process

Design for Manufacture (DFM) — Making Products Easier to Build

The easier a product is to construct, the lower the manufacturing costs per unit. DFM is about simplifying the design without compromising on performance or quality.

Standardised Parts

Use the same screws, bolts, and fixings throughout — reduces the number of different components and simplifies assembly. One screwdriver fits all.

Simplify Processes

Use repeatable, simple manufacturing steps rather than complex, one-off operations. Automation works best with simple, consistent processes.

Modular Design

Build from standard-sized interchangeable modules. If one module fails, only that module needs replacing — not the whole product.

Simple QC Tests

Design the product so that quality can be checked with simple go/no-go gauges or visual inspection — rather than complex measurement equipment at every stage.

Reduce Tolerances

Where possible, design parts so they can fit together even if they are not perfectly precise — reduces the cost of machining and the number of rejected parts.

Design for Disassembly

Design the product so that it can be easily taken apart for servicing, repair, and recycling at end of life. Reduces long-term support costs and landfill.

Activity 3 — True or False?

Test understanding
📝 These statements test the most common exam misconceptions across Topics 1.13 and 1.14. Read each explanation carefully.
1. Toughness and hardness are the same property — both describe a material's ability to resist damage.
❌ False — these are completely different properties. Hardness is the ability to resist surface indentation or scratching — a hard material is difficult to scratch or dent (diamond is the hardest material). Toughness is the ability to withstand sudden impact without fracturing — a tough material absorbs energy and bends before breaking (polycarbonate, mild steel). In fact, the hardest materials are often brittle, not tough: glass is hard but shatters on impact.
2. A Life-Cycle Analysis (LCA) only considers the environmental impact of manufacturing a product.
❌ False — an LCA covers EVERY stage of a product's life, not just manufacturing. The stages are: raw material extraction → transportation → processing → manufacture → transportation to market → distribution → USE by consumer → disposal or recovery at end of life. The USE stage is often the most significant — a car, for example, produces far more CO₂ during its operational life from burning fuel than was produced during its manufacture.
3. Recycling is always better for the environment than sending materials to landfill.
❌ False — recycling has significant disadvantages that must be weighed against its benefits. The recycling process itself requires energy for transportation, sorting, reprocessing, and reassembly — and this energy is often generated from fossil fuels. Some recycling processes produce their own waste streams and pollutants. The quality of recycled material is often inferior to virgin material. In some cases, the total environmental cost of recycling a material exceeds the cost of sending it to a modern, energy-recovering landfill. A nuanced answer that acknowledges both sides will always score better in the exam.
4. Design for Manufacture (DFM) is about making products look simpler — it reduces the aesthetic quality of the product.
❌ False — DFM is about simplifying the MANUFACTURING PROCESS, not the appearance. DFM means using standardised parts, repeatable processes, modular construction, and designs for disassembly — all of which reduce manufacturing cost and complexity without necessarily affecting how the product looks. In fact, some of the most aesthetically beautiful products (Apple's iPhone, for example) are also masterpieces of DFM — minimising part count dramatically while maintaining excellent visual design.

Exam Language & Practice

Topics 1.13 and 1.14 generate "explain," "describe," "evaluate," and "justify" questions. These are extended response questions worth 4–6 marks — they require structured, detailed answers using specific terminology.

📌 ESL Strategy — The Property-Consequence-Context Structure: For any material property question: (1) NAME the property, (2) DEFINE it precisely, (3) explain the CONSEQUENCE of that property for the product, (4) link to the specific CONTEXT given in the question. All four parts are needed for full marks.
Explaining why a material property is important — 2 marks
[Material] has [property], which means that it [what that property enables]. This is important for this product because [specific product requirement].
"Polycarbonate has high toughness, which means that it can absorb sudden impact energy without shattering. This is important for a safety helmet because if the helmet fractured on impact (like a brittle material would), it would no longer protect the wearer's head from the continuing force of the collision."
Explaining how a designer can promote green design — 4 marks
A designer can promote green design by [method 1], which would reduce [environmental impact 1]. They could also [method 2], which would [environmental benefit 2].
"A designer can promote green design by using biodegradable packaging materials instead of single-use plastic, which would reduce the amount of persistent plastic waste entering the environment and the fossil fuels used to produce it. They could also design the product for disassembly — reducing the number of parts and labelling each component by material type, which would make end-of-life recycling straightforward and increase the proportion of materials recovered rather than sent to landfill."
Describing an LCA — 3 marks
A life-cycle analysis is [what it is]. It covers every stage from [first stage] to [final stage]. The purpose is to [why it is done].
"A life-cycle analysis is a systematic inventory of the environmental impact of a product at every stage of its life. It covers every stage from the extraction of raw materials to the disposal or recovery of materials at the end of the product's useful life. The purpose is to identify which stages produce the greatest environmental impact, so that designers can focus their improvement efforts where they will make the biggest difference."

Q1 · Material Properties · 4 marks4 marks
A designer is creating a children's outdoor play set from a combination of materials. Explain why toughness is an important mechanical property for this product, and give one physical property that would also be important. Justify your choice.
💡 Toughness explanation: define it + why it matters for children's outdoor equipment specifically (2 marks). Physical property: name it + justify (2 marks).
1
Toughness: Toughness is the ability of a material to withstand sudden stress or shocks without fracturing. For a children's outdoor play set, toughness is essential because children will inevitably subject the equipment to sudden impacts — dropping it, sitting on it forcefully, hitting it. A tough material absorbs this impact energy and deforms rather than breaking suddenly, reducing the risk of sharp fracture surfaces that could injure a child.
2
Physical property — Corrosion resistance: Since the play set will be used outdoors year-round, the materials must be resistant to corrosion from rain, moisture, and temperature changes. If the metal components corrode (rust), they weaken structurally and also produce sharp edges that could cut a child. Materials such as aluminium (which forms its own protective oxide layer) or stainless steel would be appropriate choices.
Q2 · Green Design · 4 marks4 marks
Describe what is meant by a "life-cycle analysis," and explain why it is a useful tool for a product designer.
💡 Two marks for describing LCA (what it is + what stages it covers). Two marks for why it is useful (what it enables designers to do).
1
What it is: A life-cycle analysis (LCA) is a systematic method that assesses the total environmental impact of a product at every stage of its life — from the extraction of raw materials, through processing, manufacture, transportation, and use by the consumer, to the disposal or recovery of materials at the end of the product's life.
2
Why it is useful: An LCA is useful because it reveals which stages of a product's life produce the greatest environmental impact — information that is not obvious without systematic analysis. For example, an LCA might reveal that the use phase (e.g. electricity consumed by an appliance over its lifetime) produces far more impact than the manufacturing phase. This directs the designer to focus improvements on energy efficiency during use rather than just on material choices in manufacturing, producing a much greater reduction in total environmental impact.
Q3 · Challenges · 4 marks4 marks
A manufacturer is designing a new range of kitchen kettles to sell globally. Explain two challenges the designer must consider to ensure the product is appropriate for different groups of customers.
💡 Two separate challenges worth 2 marks each — name the challenge type (social/ethnic/economic), then explain specifically how it affects the kettle design.
1
Economic groups: Different economic groups have different purchasing power — a single kettle design cannot satisfy customers who prioritise low cost and those who prioritise premium aesthetics and brand prestige. The designer should consider creating a product range: a basic functional model with simple controls and low manufacturing cost for price-sensitive customers, and a premium version with high-quality materials and an iconic design for customers prepared to pay for aesthetic quality and status.
2
Human capability: The kettle will be used by people of different ages, physical abilities, and levels of technological familiarity. The controls must be intuitive and clearly labelled — unclear controls on an electric kettle (e.g. an ambiguous on/off indicator) create a risk of accidental burns from assuming the kettle has boiled when it has not. The grip must be comfortable and secure for people with arthritis or reduced hand strength, and the weight when full must be manageable for elderly users.

🔄 Technical Term → Plain English
Mechanical property→How a material behaves when force is applied to it
Physical property→A measurable characteristic without applying force (colour, conductivity, density)
Toughness→Can survive a sudden hit without breaking — absorbs impact
Brittleness→Breaks suddenly under impact without bending — the opposite of toughness
Life-cycle analysis→Counting ALL environmental costs at EVERY stage of a product's life
Green design→Designing to minimise environmental harm — less energy, less waste, natural materials
Design for Manufacture→Simplifying a product so it is easier and cheaper to make
Modular design→Built from interchangeable standard-size blocks that can be combined differently
Fairtrade→Fair pay and safe conditions for farmers in developing countries
Carbon offsetting→Paying for something (tree planting) to balance out your CO₂ emissions
