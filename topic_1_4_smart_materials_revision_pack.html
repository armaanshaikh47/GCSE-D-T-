Smart Material
Smart Materials
A material that senses and responds to changes in its environment — such as temperature, light, moisture, or electrical current — and can often return to its original state when conditions change.
"A smart material like shape-memory alloy can be bent out of shape but will return to its original form when heated."
💡 Smart = able to sense and respond. A smart material "knows" what is happening around it and reacts. Compare with a regular material that just sits there passively.
Shape-Memory Alloy (SMA)
Smart Materials
A metal alloy that can be deformed (bent, stretched, crumpled) but returns to its original shape when heated or when an electrical current is applied. The most common example is nickel-titanium (nitinol).
"Orthodontic braces use shape-memory alloy wire — the wire is fitted at room temperature but body heat causes it to contract, gently pulling teeth into position."
💡 Memory = it remembers its original shape. Alloy = a metal made from two or more metals. Key examples: glasses frames, medical stents, orthodontic wires.
Nanomaterial
Smart Materials
Materials made of tiny components less than 100 nanometres (nm) in at least one direction. A nanometre is one millionth of a millimetre. At this tiny scale, materials develop unusual properties — greater strength, conductivity, or chemical reactivity.
"Tennis rackets reinforced with nanomaterials are lighter and stronger than traditional rackets because nanoscale particles increase the strength-to-weight ratio."
💡 Nano = one billionth (10⁻⁹). A nanometre is incredibly tiny — a human hair is about 80,000 nm wide! At this scale, materials behave differently to how they do in larger quantities.
Photochromic Glass
Smart Materials
Glass that darkens automatically when exposed to ultraviolet (UV) light (sunlight) and becomes clear again in the dark. It contains tiny particles of silver halide that react to UV radiation, triggering a colour change.
"Photochromic lenses in sunglasses become darker in bright sunlight and clear indoors, removing the need to carry separate sunglasses."
💡 Photo = light. Chromic = colour changing. Photochromic = changes colour when light hits it. Key examples: transition lenses, aircraft cockpit windows.
Reactive Glass (Electrochromic)
Smart Materials
Glass that changes from transparent to opaque (and back) when a small electrical voltage is applied. Unlike photochromic glass, the user can control the reaction. It retains heat and allows instant privacy without blocking light entirely.
"Hospital wards now use reactive glass partitions — at the press of a button, the glass turns opaque for patient privacy, then clear again for monitoring."
💡 Electro = electricity. Chromic = colour/transparency. Reactive glass = glass controlled by electricity. Key difference from photochromic: YOU control it; photochromic reacts automatically to light.
Piezoelectric Material
Smart Materials
A material that generates a small electric charge when compressed or squeezed (sensor). It also works in reverse — applying an electric charge causes it to move or vibrate (actuator). Used in sensors, alarms, and precise positioning systems.
"A burglar alarm uses a piezoelectric sensor on the window — if the glass breaks, the vibration generates an electric charge that triggers the alarm."
💡 Piezo = press/squeeze (Greek). Piezoelectric = electricity from pressing. Two roles: as a sensor (pressure → electricity) and as an actuator (electricity → movement).
Conductive Ink
Smart Materials
Ink containing metal particles (silver, carbon, graphite) that allow electricity to flow through it when dry. It can be used in a pen on any surface — paper, fabric, plastic — to draw working electrical circuits without soldering.
"A designer used conductive ink to draw a working LED circuit directly onto a greeting card, making the card light up when touched."
💡 Conductive = allows electricity to flow. Ink = liquid you can print/draw with. Conductive ink = printable electricity! Key advantage: lighter and more flexible than traditional circuit boards.
Composite Material
Composites
A material made by combining two or more different materials to create a new material with better properties than either component alone. A composite always has a reinforcing material (provides strength) and a matrix (the bonding agent that holds it together).
"Concrete is a composite material — the aggregate (gravel/sand) provides bulk and the cement acts as the matrix, binding everything together."
💡 Composite = made of different parts together. Think of it like a team — each member has weaknesses, but together they are stronger. Matrix = the "glue"; Reinforcer = the "skeleton".
Compressive Strength
Composites
The ability of a material to resist being squashed or compressed. Concrete has excellent compressive strength — it can bear heavy loads pressing down on it — but poor tensile strength (resisting being pulled apart).
"Concrete has excellent compressive strength, which is why it is used in foundations and columns that must bear the weight of buildings pushing down on them."
💡 Compress = to squeeze/push together. Compressive strength = resistance to squashing. Opposite = tensile strength (resistance to stretching/pulling). Concrete = strong in compression, weak in tension.
Veneer
Composites
A thin slice of wood (3 mm or less) used to build up manufactured boards (like plywood) or to give a decorative surface to cheaper wood underneath. Plywood always has an odd number of veneer layers for structural stability.
"Plywood is made from multiple veneers — thin wood slices — bonded together at 90° angles to each other, which gives the board strength in all directions."
💡 A veneer is like a slice of bread — very thin. Plywood = many thin slices (veneers) glued together. Key exam fact: always an odd number of layers (3, 5, 7...) so the grain directions balance.
GRP / CFRP
Composites
GRP (Glass Reinforced Plastic) = plastic reinforced with fine glass fibres. Easily formed into shapes. Used in boat hulls and car bodies. CFRP (Carbon Fibre Reinforced Plastic) = same but with carbon fibres — much stronger and lighter, used in aerospace, sports equipment and armour.
"Racing bicycles use CFRP frames because carbon fibre gives an exceptional strength-to-weight ratio — lighter than aluminium but stronger than steel."
💡 Both are reinforced plastics. GRP = glass fibres (cheaper, good for complex shapes). CFRP = carbon fibres (stronger, more expensive, used where weight is critical). Key: fibre = reinforcer; polyester resin = matrix.
Technical Textile
Technical Textiles
A fabric or textile developed primarily for its functional performance (strength, protection, filtration, durability) rather than its appearance or fashion. Technical textiles can be strong, waterproof, lightweight, fireproof, or biodegradable.
"A firefighter's suit is made from technical textiles — not chosen for their look, but for their ability to protect against extreme heat, flames, and toxic chemicals."
💡 Technical = designed for a technical purpose (function, not fashion). Ask: was this textile chosen for how it looks, or what it can DO? Technical textiles = chosen for what they DO.
Agrotextile
Technical Textiles
Textiles used in agriculture to improve crop production. They can shade crops, provide thermal insulation, act as windbreaks, or suppress weeds — reducing the need for herbicides and pesticides. Made from nylon, polyester, or biodegradable natural materials.
"Farmers use agrotextile netting over strawberry crops to protect them from birds, frost, and excessive sunlight, resulting in a larger and better-quality harvest."
💡 Agro = agriculture/farming (like agronomist = farming scientist). Agrotextile = textile used in farming. Key advantage: reduces chemical use (fewer pesticides/herbicides).
Geotextile
Technical Textiles
Textiles used in civil engineering to stabilise, filter, drain, or reinforce soil, rock, and other geotechnical materials. They are placed under roads, embankments, and drainage channels and retain their structure in the ground.
"A geotextile membrane laid under a new road prevents the sub-base gravel from mixing with the soft soil below, maintaining the structural integrity of the road."
💡 Geo = earth/ground. Geotextile = a textile used in the ground or with soil/rock. Key function: stabilise, filter, drain. Key disadvantage: can be blocked by sediment and become ineffective.
Strength-to-Weight Ratio
Composites
A measure of how strong a material is compared to how heavy it is. A high strength-to-weight ratio means the material is both strong and lightweight. Composites like CFRP and reinforced polymers excel at this.
"CFRP has an excellent strength-to-weight ratio — it is lighter than aluminium but stronger than steel, making it ideal for aircraft and racing cars where every gram matters."
💡 Think of it as a comparison: strength ÷ weight. A high number = strong AND light (good for aerospace). Composites are valued precisely because they have high strength-to-weight ratios.

🔮 Modern & Smart Materials (1.4.1)

Spec ref: 1.4.1a–f | Key question: What are the properties, applications, advantages and disadvantages of each smart material?

Modern materials do not occur naturally but are existing materials that have been altered to improve their properties. Smart materials sense and respond to external conditions and some return to their original state when conditions change.

🧱 Composite Materials (1.4.2)

Spec ref: 1.4.2a–e | Key question: What makes a composite, and what are the properties and uses of each type?

A composite consists of a reinforcing material (provides strength/stiffness) and a matrix (the bonding agent that holds it together). The resulting material has enhanced properties compared to either material alone. Most composites have excellent strength-to-weight ratios.

📸 Reinforced concrete — steel rebar provides missing tensile strength

📸 Plywood — alternating veneer layers at 90° angles

CompositeDescriptionExamples / Uses✓ Key Benefits✗ Disadvantages
Concrete
  • Coarse aggregate (gravel 14–40 mm), fine aggregate (sand), cement, water
  • Excellent compressive strength; low tensile strength
  • Can last 100 years; can be made waterproof or acid-resistant
  • Construction (foundations, columns, floors)
  • Park benches, bins, street furniture
  • Excellent compressive strength and good heat/sound insulator
  • Can be moulded into complex shapes; manufactured on site
  • Can be damaged by corrosion of steel reinforcement, fire, or freezing water trapped inside
Plywood
  • Veneers (thin wood slices) bonded with glue
  • Always odd number of layers (min. 3)
  • Layers at 90° to each other — stable in all directions
  • Sheds, cladding, flooring, furniture
  • Exterior or interior depending on waterproof glue used
  • High strength-to-weight ratio; strong in all directions
  • Versatile (indoor/outdoor); economical use of wood
  • Some grades delaminate (layers come apart) when wet
GRP (Glass Reinforced Plastic)
  • Plastic reinforced with fine glass fibres; built up in layers with polyester resin
  • Can be sanded and painted for a smooth finish
  • Boat hulls, pond liners, baths, showers, car bodies
  • Low maintenance; resistant to chemicals, UV, and weathering
  • Easily formed into complex 3D shapes
  • Breathing in fibres during production can cause respiratory problems
CFRP (Carbon Fibre Reinforced Plastic)
  • Same as GRP but with carbon fibres instead of glass
  • Much stronger and lighter than GRP
  • Propeller blades, body armour, golf clubs, aerospace
  • Exceptional strength-to-weight ratio
  • Lightweight with excellent structural performance
  • More expensive than GRP; fibre inhalation risk during manufacturing
Reinforced Polymers
  • Phenolic resins combined with cotton fabrics → laminated plastic sheets, rods, tubes
  • About half the weight of aluminium
  • Gears, bearings, non-metallic engineering components
  • Weatherproof exterior timber substitute
  • Good wear resistance; insulates heat and electricity
  • Good dimensional stability in heat and moisture
  • Can be expensive
Robotic Materials
  • Couple sensing, activation (movement), computation and communication
  • Can react to their surroundings autonomously
  • Uniforms or vehicles that change colour to match surroundings
  • Prosthetics with a sense of touch
  • Plane wings that change shape with wind conditions
  • Can react without connection to an external computer
  • React quickly and independently
  • Expensive and complex to manufacture

Activity 3 — True or False?

Test understanding
📝 Read each statement. Press True or False. You will get immediate feedback with an explanation. Focus on WHY the answer is correct — this helps exam performance more than just knowing the answer.
1. Photochromic glass and reactive (electrochromic) glass are the same thing — both darken automatically when exposed to sunlight.
❌ False. This is a very common exam mistake! Photochromic glass darkens automatically in UV light — the user has no control. Reactive (electrochromic) glass is controlled by the user through applying a small electrical voltage. You choose when it goes opaque; photochromic reacts automatically.
2. Plywood always uses an odd number of veneer layers (e.g. 3, 5, 7) because this balances the stresses and makes the board stable in all directions.
✅ True. According to the textbook, plywood always has an odd number of layers with their grain directions at 90° to each other. An odd number means the top and bottom veneers always have their grain running in the same direction, which balances the stresses and prevents warping. This also gives plywood strength in all directions.
3. A piezoelectric material can only work as a sensor — it converts pressure into electricity but cannot work in reverse.
❌ False. Piezoelectric materials work in both directions. As a sensor: compression generates an electric charge. As an actuator: applying an electric charge generates movement. This bidirectional property makes them very useful — they are used in both burglar alarms (sensors) AND in precise positioning systems like camera actuators and fast-acting nozzles.
4. Technical textiles are primarily chosen for their attractive appearance and fashionable look rather than their functional properties.
❌ False. This is the exact opposite of the definition. Technical textiles are developed specifically for their functional performance — strength, protection, waterproofing, filtration, durability — rather than their appearance. A firefighter's suit is chosen to protect against heat and chemicals, not because it looks stylish.
5. Concrete has excellent tensile strength (resistance to being pulled apart) but poor compressive strength (resistance to being squashed).
❌ False — the description is the wrong way round! Concrete has excellent compressive strength (resists being squashed, which is why it works in foundations and columns) but poor tensile strength (it cracks when pulled or bent). This is exactly why steel reinforcement bars are embedded in concrete — the steel provides the missing tensile strength.
6. Nanomaterials may require specialist risk assessment because their tiny size gives them unusual chemical and physical properties that could affect health and the environment.
✅ True. The textbook specifically notes that nanomaterials may need specialist risk assessment relating to health and the environment. Because their properties at the nanoscale can be very different from the same material in bulk form, the health effects of inhaling or absorbing nanomaterials are still being researched. This is an important disadvantage to mention in exam answers.

✍️ Sentence Frames for Smart Materials Questions

Describing what a smart material does (2 marks)
[Material name] is a smart material that responds to [stimulus: temperature / light / pressure / voltage] by [what it does]. This means that [practical benefit].
"Shape-memory alloy is a smart material that responds to heat by returning to its original pre-set shape. This means that it can be used in orthodontic braces — the body's warmth slowly moves the teeth into position without manual adjustment."
Explaining an advantage of a composite (2 marks)
One advantage of using [composite] for [application] is [advantage]. This is because [reason linked to the material's properties].
"One advantage of using CFRP for racing bicycle frames is its exceptional strength-to-weight ratio. This is because the carbon fibres provide greater structural strength than steel while being significantly lighter, reducing the total mass of the bicycle and improving the rider's performance."
Comparing two smart materials (4 marks)
Both [material A] and [material B] respond to external stimuli, however they differ in that [key difference]. [Material A] is more suitable for [context A] because [reason], whereas [material B] is better suited to [context B].
"Both photochromic glass and reactive glass can change their transparency, however they differ in that photochromic glass reacts automatically to UV light while reactive glass is controlled by the user through electricity. Photochromic glass is more suitable for sunglasses because it responds to natural light without any user action, whereas reactive glass is better suited to office partitions where the user needs to choose when to create privacy."

💡 Language hint: For 6 marks you need at least 3 developed points — include advantages AND at least one disadvantage, with explanations. End with an overall judgement. Use: Furthermore, However, Overall...
1
Advantage 1 — Shape-recovery: A stent made from SMA can be compressed into a small diameter for insertion into the artery, then expand to its pre-set shape when warmed by body heat. This means the stent can be inserted through a much smaller incision than a rigid stent, reducing patient trauma and recovery time.
2
Advantage 2 — Reduced size and complexity: Because SMAs return automatically to their original shape, the stent does not require additional mechanical components to expand it. This reduces the complexity of the device and lowers the risk of mechanical failure inside the body.
3
Advantage 3 — Longevity: SMAs can lengthen the life of the product because the material's shape-recovery means it can continue functioning correctly even if slightly deformed during use.
4
Disadvantage — Cost and fatigue: However, SMA materials such as nitinol are expensive to manufacture and source. Furthermore, continuous repeated deformation and recovery cycles over many years could lead to metal fatigue, potentially reducing the long-term reliability of the stent.
Overall: Shape-memory alloy is a highly appropriate material for medical stents because its ability to expand from a compressed state in response to body heat enables minimally invasive surgery. Despite being expensive, the clinical benefits — smaller incisions, automatic expansion, and reduced mechanical complexity — outweigh the cost disadvantage in a critical medical application.

🚨 Common ESL Mistakes — Smart Materials Topic
❌ Avoid writing this✅ Write this insteadWhy?
"SMA remembers its shape""SMA returns to its pre-set shape when heated or a current is applied"Explain the mechanism — what causes the shape change?
"Nanomaterials are very small""Nanomaterials have components less than 100 nanometres in size, giving them unusual strength and conductivity properties"Use the specific measurement (100 nm) and explain what difference size makes
"Concrete is strong""Concrete has excellent compressive strength but low tensile strength, which is why steel rods are embedded to provide the missing tensile resistance"Be precise — there are two different types of strength!
"CFRP is light and strong""CFRP has an exceptional strength-to-weight ratio — it is stronger than steel but lighter than aluminium"Use the correct technical term strength-to-weight ratio
"Technical textiles look good""Technical textiles are selected for their functional performance — such as strength, waterproofing, or heat resistance — rather than their appearance"Technical textiles are about function, NOT fashion
"Piezoelectric makes electricity from pressure""Piezoelectric materials generate a small electric charge when compressed (sensor) and also create movement when a charge is applied (actuator)"Always mention BOTH directions — sensor AND actuator
