One-Off Production
Scales
A single product made by a craftsperson, one at a time, either for an individual client who wants something unique or as a prototype to test a design. No expensive set-up is needed, but each item takes a long time to make.
"A bespoke dining table hand-crafted by a furniture maker is a one-off — every measurement and detail is tailored to a specific client, but the time required makes it expensive."
💡 One-off = ONE product, ONE time. No set-up cost (advantage) but slow and expensive per unit (disadvantage). Examples: bespoke furniture, prototypes, surgical implants, custom instruments.
Batch Production
Scales
Making several copies of the same product at the same time, using jigs, templates, and moulds to speed up the process. No special machinery is needed, making set-up costs low — but each item is still relatively expensive because of the labour involved.
"A school workshop making 20 identical stools uses batch production — a drilling jig ensures all screw holes are in exactly the same position on every leg, saving time and improving consistency."
💡 Batch = a GROUP made together. Uses jigs and templates (advantage: no special machinery needed). Still labour intensive (disadvantage). Examples: school furniture, restaurant chairs, limited-edition products.
Jig
Manufacturing
A device that is placed over a workpiece to guide a drill or saw to cut in exactly the correct position. A jig is positioned accurately once, then the same cut or hole can be reproduced identically on multiple pieces — making it ideal for batch production.
"A jig clamped to the corner of a chair leg guided the drill to make screw holes in exactly the right position on all 20 chair legs in the batch — without the jig, each hole would have to be measured and marked individually."
💡 Jig = guides the TOOL (drill or saw) to the correct position. Different from template (which you draw around). Key exam point: jigs are most useful for batch production — once you have the jig, you can replicate the cut quickly on many identical pieces.
Quality Control
Manufacturing
A system for checking that manufactured products meet the required standard at each stage of production. A sample of products is inspected at regular intervals. If a fault is found, production stops so it can be corrected before more faulty products are made.
"At the flat-pack furniture factory, quality control workers check a sample of chipboard panels at each stage — measuring hole positions with a go/no-go gauge to ensure all fittings will align when the customer assembles the product."
💡 Quality control = checking products DURING production (not just at the end). It includes working within tolerance — the acceptable range of sizes (e.g. ±0.5 mm). Go/no-go gauges check whether a part is within tolerance without measuring every dimension.
Tenon Saw
Tools
A hand saw with a stiffened back (spine) running along the top of the blade to keep it rigid and prevent bending. Used for cutting smaller pieces of timber accurately and for cutting joints. The stiffened back means it cannot cut deeper than the blade length.
"The tenon saw's rigid back prevented the blade from wandering, making it ideal for cutting the tenon of a mortise and tenon joint where accuracy of a fraction of a millimetre is essential."
💡 Tenon saw = small + accurate + stiff back. The stiffened back (spine) is BOTH the advantage (keeps blade straight) AND the disadvantage (limits cutting depth — cannot cut deeper than the blade is long). Used for: joints, small accurate cuts, detail work.
Forstner Bit
Tools
A drill bit specifically designed to produce flat-bottomed blind holes (holes that do not go all the way through). A small centre spur provides an accurate starting point. Produces a clean, flat base — essential for hinge recesses and concealed fittings.
"To fit a concealed hinge to a cabinet door, the cabinetmaker used a forstner bit — the flat-bottomed hole it produced gave the hinge cup a perfectly flat seating at exactly the correct depth."
💡 Forstner = FLAT bottom hole (blind hole). Key difference from flat bit: flat bit drills quickly but leaves a pointed bottom (spike). Forstner is slower but leaves a perfectly flat base. Used for: concealed hinges, knock-down fittings, hinge seatings.
Mortise and Tenon Joint
Joints
A strong mechanical joint where a projecting piece (the tenon) fits tightly into a matching rectangular hole (the mortise) in the other piece. The interlocking shapes resist pulling forces. Used for chair and table frames, doors, and anywhere structural strength is needed.
"The dining chair frame used mortise and tenon joints at every leg-to-rail connection — the mechanical interlock means the joints resist the racking forces from someone leaning back in the chair."
💡 Mortise = the HOLE. Tenon = the PEG (projection). Together they interlock mechanically. Strong joint because the shapes resist both pulling and rotating. Disadvantage: time-consuming to cut accurately by hand. A mortiser machine can cut mortises quickly.
Dovetail Joint
Joints
The strongest wood joint — interlocking fan-shaped tails and pins that lock together mechanically, preventing the joint from being pulled apart in the direction of the tails. The angled shape means the joint is virtually impossible to pull open once glued. Very difficult to cut accurately by hand.
"Traditional drawer boxes use dovetail joints — when you pull the drawer open, the handles pull the front outward, but the dovetails lock the sides to the front, making it virtually impossible to pull the drawer apart."
💡 Dovetail = STRONGEST joint. The fan shape (like a dove's tail) locks the joint against being pulled apart. Advantage: extremely strong in tension. Disadvantage: very tricky to cut by hand — requires skill and practice. CNC routers can cut them quickly and accurately.
Housing Joint
Joints
A joint where a full-width groove (housing) is cut across one piece of timber, and the end of the other piece sits in it. Holds a shelf or divider securely in the middle of a carcass. Works well in combination with corner lap joints.
"The bookcase shelves used housing joints — a groove routed across the full width of each side panel ensured the shelves were held level and could not sag at the ends under the weight of books."
💡 Housing = a CHANNEL cut across the full width of a board. The shelf end sits in the groove — this is what keeps it level and prevents it drooping. Key exam point: a housing joint must be very accurately cut to ensure shelves are perfectly level.
PVA Adhesive
Manufacturing
Polyvinyl acetate — a thick white liquid wood glue that becomes clear when dry. Creates an extremely strong bond between wood surfaces — when cured, it is almost impossible to disassemble the joint without destroying the wood. Pieces must be clamped tightly while the glue dries.
"The bookcase joints were glued with PVA and clamped overnight — when the glue had fully cured, the mortise and tenon joints were stronger than the surrounding wood itself."
💡 PVA = the white glue. Advantages: strong bond, cheap, water-based (not solvent), dries clear. Disadvantages: must clamp while drying; almost impossible to open joint once cured. Different from contact adhesive: PVA bonds wood-to-wood; contact adhesive bonds different materials.

7.6 — Manufacturing Processes

Topic 7.6 covers the machinery used to cut and shape timber, the four scales of production, and the production aids and quality systems used in manufacturing. The scales of production table is the most commonly examined part of this topic.

📌 ESL Strategy — Compare and Contrast: The scales of production are best revised by comparing them against each other, not by memorising each one in isolation. After reading, try answering: "What is the difference between batch and mass production?" and "Why would a company switch from batch to mass production?" These comparison questions are exactly what the exam sets.

⚙️ Processes to Cut and Shape Timber

Machinery used in workshop and industrial manufacturing of timber products.

🔄 Routing

A router contains a rapidly rotating cutter. It is used to cut straight slots, profile edges, and curved shapes. It can be used with a jig to cut the same shape repeatedly. Computer-controlled CNC routers can cut very accurately and operate continuously.

⚠️ Large cuts may burn/blacken timber — move the router slowly and use sharp cutters.

🪚 Sawing Machines

The circular saw (table saw) uses a rotating blade to make straight cuts through timber. The bandsaw uses a continuous band blade that can make both straight and curved cuts. Sawmills use very large versions to cut whole tree trunks into planks.

⚠️ Cutting thicker timber on a bandsaw may result in edges that are not perfectly square.

🔲 Mortiser

A bench-top machine that makes square holes for mortise and tenon joints. A round central chisel drills a round hole, then a square outer chisel cuts the corners square. Produces mortises quickly and accurately but requires careful marking out beforehand.

Best for: batch production of identical furniture frames — chairs, tables, cabinet carcasses.

🛄 Bag Press

A bag that can be sealed and have the air sucked out of it. A mould and laminate strips are placed inside. When air is removed, atmospheric pressure forces the laminates evenly onto the mould shape while the glue dries. Presses equally on all surface areas but may not work with very thick laminates.

Best for: laminating curved chair seats and backs where even pressure across all surfaces is essential.

💻 CAD/CAM & CNC

Computer-Aided Manufacturing (CAM) uses a computer to send cutting instructions to a CNC (Computer Numerically Controlled) machine. The cutter is moved by electric motors with extreme precision. Can operate 24/7 with minimal human supervision once set up.

High initial cost and training required; extremely accurate; suitable for all scales of production.

🎯 Planer/Thicknesser

A machine with a rotating cutter block that planes timber to get flat, true faces and edges, then feeds it through to plane it to an exact set thickness. Essential for preparing sawn timber for furniture-making where consistent dimensions are needed throughout a batch.

Produces: consistent thickness, flat faces, square edges — all essential for accurate joints.

🛠️ Production Aids, Marking Out & Quality Control

Techniques and systems that improve accuracy, consistency, and quality in manufacturing.

📐 Jig

A device placed over a workpiece to guide a drill or saw to cut in exactly the correct place. Very useful for batch production — once set up, the same cut can be made on many pieces quickly and accurately.

Exam use: explain how a jig improves consistency in batch production by ensuring every piece is drilled or cut in identical positions without individual marking out.

📋 Template

A cut-out shape drawn around to mark the same shape on multiple pieces of material. Paper or card for single use; thin wood or metal for repeated use. Allows the same shape to be reproduced accurately without re-measuring each time.

Key exam point: templates also allow efficient cutting layout — shapes can be arranged closely together to minimise waste material.

🗜️ Fixture

A device that holds the workpiece securely in position while it is being cut or shaped. Speeds up the cutting process significantly. However, different products may need different fixtures, adding to initial setup costs.

Different from a jig: a fixture holds the WORKPIECE; a jig guides the TOOL.

📏 Marking Out

Transferring measurements onto the material before cutting. A reference point (datum) is essential — usually the bottom left corner, or the face side and face edge. All measurements are taken from the same reference to prevent cumulative errors.

Face side = the best, flattest face. Face edge = the best, squarest edge. All marking out starts from these surfaces.

✅ Quality Control

A system for checking that products meet the required standard at each stage of production. Samples are inspected at regular intervals. If a fault is found, production stops to correct it before more faulty products are made.

Go/no-go gauges: check whether a part is within tolerance without measuring. If it fits the "go" gauge but not the "no-go" gauge, the part is acceptable.

⚡ CAM & Efficient Cutting

Material costs money so it is important to minimise waste. Using a template, arrange parts as close together as possible. CAM software can automatically calculate the most efficient cutting layout (nesting) to reduce material waste to a minimum.

Waste reduction = lower cost per unit and better environmental impact — important for both exam answers.

🔢 Maths Corner — Calculating Wastage

Section B includes calculation marks. Wastage questions require you to calculate the area of shapes and subtract them from the overall sheet area. You need three formulae:

Rectangle
A = width × length
Triangle
A = ½ × base × height
Circle
A = π × r²
(π = 3.14, r = radius)
Worked Example: A shape (rectangle + circle) is cut from 6 mm MDF. Sheet = 60 mm × 160 mm. Shape = 100 mm × 60 mm rectangle + one whole circle (radius 30 mm). Calculate the area wasted.
Sheet area: 60 × 160 = 9,600 mm²
Rectangle area: 100 × 60 = 6,000 mm²
Circle area: 3.14 × 30 × 30 = 2,826 mm²
Shape area total: 6,000 + 2,826 = 8,826 mm²
Wastage: 9,600 − 8,826 = 774 mm²
⚠️ Key points:
• Always show working — partial marks awarded
• State all units (mm²)
• π = 3.14 (not 3.14159...)
• Radius = diameter ÷ 2
• r² = r × r (not r × 2)

🔨 Shaping, Planing & Abrading

Tools for removing material to get the final shape, smooth the surface, and prepare timber for finishing.

Planing & Chiselling

✂️ Hand Plane

A sharp blade protrudes slightly from a flat base plate. Used to remove thin shavings from the edge or face of timber to get a straight, flat surface. Different lengths: jack plane (long — good for large flat surfaces or edges), smoothing plane (short — easier to handle on small pieces).

Key exam point: a plane removes very thin, controlled shavings — far more precise than a saw, which removes material in wider, less controlled cuts.

🏭 Planer/Thicknesser

A rotating cutter block planes timber flat and square. The top table planes to get a flat, true face. The thicknesser section draws the timber in under the cutter and planes it to an exact set thickness — essential for preparing sawn timber to precise dimensions before joint-cutting.

Cannot be used as a hand-held tool — always a fixed workshop machine. Requires correct and consistent grain direction to prevent tearing.

🔪 Chisel

Used for paring (slicing wood away between grains) and cutting joints. A mortise chisel has a thicker, heavier blade and stronger handle — designed to be struck with a mallet for cutting the mortise slot. Chisels must be kept sharp — a blunt chisel is more dangerous and less effective than a sharp one.

⚠️ Hard to use across end grain — it can split. Always work WITH the grain where possible. Sharp chisel = safer chisel.

🪵 Wood-Turning Lathe

Holds a piece of timber between centres and rotates it at speed. The operator holds a chisel against a tool rest and moves it carefully against the spinning wood to remove material and create circular cross-sections. Requires careful preparation and machine setup.

Uses: table legs, chair spindles, wooden bowls, decorative knobs and handles — any product with a circular cross-section.

Abrading Tools

💡 Sandpaper grit guide: P80 = coarse (initial rough shaping, removing saw marks). P120 = medium (removing P80 scratches, general smoothing). P240 = fine (final smooth surface ready for finishing). Always sand IN THE DIRECTION of the grain — sanding across the grain tears the fibres and leaves scratches that are very difficult to remove.

7.7 — Joints, Adhesives & Fittings

You must know all seven wood joints by name, strength, use, advantage and disadvantage. The dovetail and mortise and tenon are the most commonly examined. Hinges and knock-down fittings are also examined.

📌 ESL Strategy — Rank by Strength: Rank the seven joints from weakest to strongest, then write this ranking from memory. This forces you to understand the structural reason for each joint's strength, which is exactly what exam "explain" questions test. Ranking order: Butt (weakest) → Mitre → Lap → Housing → Dowel → Mortise & Tenon → Dovetail (strongest).

🔗 Wood Joints — All Seven Types

Ranked from weakest to strongest. Each joint's strength comes from its structure — not just the glue.

Butt Joint
⚠️ Weakest

Two square-cut ends are glued together. No mechanical interlocking — strength comes entirely from the glue. Very easy to make with a tenon saw and bench hook.

Uses: simple boxes, internal construction where joint will not be stressed.

Mitre Joint
⚠️ Weak

Both pieces are cut at 45° so no end grain is visible on the surface — gives a clean, professional appearance at corners. Structurally it is just a butt joint at an angle.

Uses: picture frames, decorative boxes, skirting board corners where appearance matters most.

Lap Joint
▲ Medium

Half the thickness of each piece is removed so they overlap and sit flush. Quite easy to cut with a tenon saw and chisel. Provides a larger gluing surface than a butt joint.

Uses: frame construction, joining battens, making lightweight boxes.

Housing Joint
▲ Medium

A full-width groove (housing) is cut across one piece. The end of the other piece sits in it. Must be very accurately cut to ensure the shelf sits level. Works well with corner lap joints.

Uses: bookshelves, cabinet shelves, dividers — anywhere a shelf must be held level in the middle of a carcass.

Dowel Joint
▲ Medium–Strong

Wooden dowel rods are pressed into holes drilled in both pieces. Automated machines can drill holes quickly and accurately. Very difficult to align holes accurately by hand.

Uses: flat-pack furniture, cabinet assembly, anywhere a hidden joint with good alignment is needed.

Mortise & Tenon
✓ Strong

A projecting tenon fits into a matching rectangular mortise hole. Interlocking shapes resist both pulling and rotating forces. Time-consuming to cut by hand; a mortiser machine cuts it quickly.

Uses: chair and table frames, door construction, anywhere structural strength must resist racking forces.

Dovetail Joint
★ Strongest

Fan-shaped tails and matching pins interlock mechanically — the angled shape prevents the joint from being pulled apart in the direction of the tails. Once glued, virtually indestructible. Very difficult to cut accurately by hand.

Uses: drawer boxes (resists being pulled open), high-quality cabinet construction, hardwood tool boxes.

🧪 Adhesives, Screws & Nails

Choosing the right fixing method is as important as choosing the right joint. Some fixings are permanent (PVA); others are removable (screws).

🟤 PVA Adhesive

Polyvinyl acetate — a thick white liquid wood glue. Becomes clear when dry. Creates a very strong bond — once fully cured, it is almost impossible to disassemble the joint without destroying the wood. Pieces must be clamped together tightly while the glue cures.

Best for: permanent timber-to-timber bonds. Water-based (safe), cheap, strong. Not suitable for bonding different materials (wood to metal, wood to plastic).

🔴 Contact Adhesive

Applied in a thin film to BOTH surfaces. Wait until the glue is almost dry (tacky but not wet). Press the two surfaces firmly together. The bond forms instantly on contact — no clamping needed. However, repositioning is almost impossible once contact is made.

⚠️ Gives off solvent fumes — ventilation essential. Best for: bonding plastic laminate (Formica) onto chipboard or MDF worktops; sticking fabric or other materials onto timber.

🔩 Countersunk Screws

The most useful fixing for joining pieces of timber — creates a tight fit, is very strong, and can be removed if needed. Slotted (flat-head) and Phillips (cross-head) types. For a countersunk screw: drill a clearance hole (wider than screw shank) in the top piece first, then a pilot hole (narrower than threads) in the bottom piece to prevent splitting.

Three holes: countersink recess → clearance hole (in top piece) → pilot hole (in bottom piece). All three are essential for a neat, strong screw fixing.

🔨 Nails

Hammered into wood — the grain pinches around the nail, holding it. Fast to use and cheap. Holes may need to be pre-drilled to prevent splitting near edges.
• Round wire nails: large flat head, won't pull through thin material
• Oval nails: oval cross-section, spreads grain less, less likely to split
• Panel pins: small nails for thin material and model-making

Nails vs screws: nails are faster but cannot be removed cleanly; screws are stronger and removable but slower to insert.

🪵 Veneering

A veneer is a thin layer of high-quality (often expensive) wood. It is glued onto the surface of a cheaper material — usually MDF or chipboard — to make the product look like it is made from solid expensive timber. MDF is often bought pre-veneered with oak, walnut, or other hardwoods.

Advantage: the appearance of expensive hardwood at a fraction of the cost. Disadvantage: if the surface is scratched or chipped, the cheap substrate beneath shows through.

⚙️ Hinges, Knock-Down Fittings & Ironmongery

Fittings are bought-in components that add functionality to a product. Knowing when to specify each type — and what its trade-offs are — is what the exam tests.

Knock-Down Fittings

📦 Knock-Down Fittings

A range of blocks and fittings designed so that a product can be assembled and disassembled easily without specialist tools — ideal for flat-pack furniture. The customer can assemble the product themselves using only the provided Allen key.

Advantages: easy for customer assembly; can be packed flat (reduces transport cost and volume); pieces can be replaced if damaged. Disadvantages: the joint is weaker than a glued mortise and tenon; visible fitting may be unattractive.

🔧 Ironmongery

A collective term for the range of metal hardware that can be added to a timber product to provide functionality. Items include: hinges, handles, knobs, hooks, drawer runners, locks, screws, and bolts. Ironmongery is usually bought-in rather than made from scratch, and is specified by the designer.

Good ironmongery improves both the function AND aesthetics of a product. Poor choice of ironmongery (wrong size, wrong style, wrong finish) can undermine an otherwise well-made product.

Activity 3 — True or False?

Test understanding
📝 These statements test the most common exam misconceptions in Topics 7.6 and 7.7. Read each explanation carefully after answering.
1. A dovetail joint is stronger than a mortise and tenon joint because it uses more glue.
❌ False — and the reason given is completely wrong. A dovetail joint is stronger than most other joints because of its MECHANICAL STRUCTURE — the fan-shaped tails lock together physically, making it virtually impossible to pull apart in the direction of the tails, regardless of the amount of glue. The strength comes from the geometry of the joint, not from the quantity of adhesive. In fact, all wood joints rely on the mechanical interlock first; the adhesive enhances this by filling any small gaps and bonding the surfaces.
2. The stiffened spine of a tenon saw is a disadvantage because it limits how deep you can cut.
✅ True — but this is only half the answer the exam expects. The stiffened back IS the disadvantage (it limits cutting depth). However, the same spine is also the advantage: it prevents the blade from bending and ensures very accurate, straight cuts. A good exam answer always gives BOTH sides: "The stiffened back keeps the blade rigid for accurate cuts (advantage), but prevents the saw from cutting deeper than the length of the blade below the spine (disadvantage)."
3. A butt hinge should be specified for a flat-pack kitchen cupboard door because it is hidden from view when the door is closed.
❌ False — a butt hinge is the wrong choice for flat-pack furniture. Although a butt hinge is hidden when the door is closed, it requires an accurately cut slot (housing) to be routed into both the door edge and the frame — a skilled, time-consuming process entirely unsuitable for flat-pack furniture that customers assemble themselves. A flush hinge or concealed (European-style) hinge would be more appropriate because they require only screwing onto or into the surface, with no slot cutting needed. The exam frequently tests this distinction.
4. A housing joint and a butt joint are both weak because neither uses a mechanical interlock — they rely entirely on the adhesive for strength.
❌ False — a housing joint does have a mechanical interlock. A butt joint has no mechanical interlock and relies entirely on adhesive. However, a housing joint has a groove (the housing) cut across the full width of one piece — the end of the shelf physically sits inside this groove, and the groove's walls mechanically prevent the shelf from moving vertically. This mechanical element gives the housing joint significantly greater strength than a simple butt joint, especially for holding shelves level against the downward force of items placed on them.
5. Mass production always produces better quality products than one-off production because automated machinery is more precise than handwork.
❌ False — mass production produces more CONSISTENT products, not necessarily better quality ones. Mass production machinery produces identical items repeatedly within a tight tolerance. A skilled craftsperson making a one-off product can achieve a level of care, finishing quality, and attention to individual detail that no production line can match — which is why bespoke furniture, handmade instruments, and custom products can command a price 10–100× higher than mass-produced equivalents. The exam may ask you to compare quality and cost considerations across scales of production — always consider both sides.

📝 Practice Questions

Q1 · Scales · 4 marks4 marks
A company has been batch producing coffee tables. A large retailer has just ordered 3,000 of its tables. Explain two reasons why the directors should change to mass production.
💡 Two developed reasons: each needs a property of mass production AND the consequence for the company/retailer. Use: "Mass production allows… which means that…"
1
Speed and volume: Mass production uses dedicated factory machinery that makes the same product continuously, far faster than batch production with jigs and templates. This means the company can fulfil the retailer's order of 3,000 tables within a reasonable timescale — batch production would be too slow to meet the deadline for such a large order.
2
Reduced cost per unit: Although the machinery investment is high, the cost is spread across 3,000 units — dramatically reducing the cost per table compared to batch production. This allows the company to offer the retailer a competitive price that makes the order commercially viable, while still maintaining a profit margin.
Q2 · Joints · 4 marks4 marks
A designer is making a solid oak bookcase with adjustable shelves. Explain why a housing joint is appropriate for fixing the shelves, and give one disadvantage of this joint.
💡 Advantage: 2 marks (what the joint does + why it matters for this product). Disadvantage: 1–2 marks (specific difficulty + consequence).
1
Advantage: A housing joint cuts a full-width groove (the housing) across the inner face of each side panel. The full width of the shelf end sits inside this groove, giving mechanical support across the entire shelf width. This prevents the shelf from sagging or pulling away at the ends when heavy books are placed on it — the groove walls bear the load directly rather than relying on adhesive alone.
2
Disadvantage: The housing must be cut very accurately to the correct depth and width — if it is even slightly uneven, the shelf will not sit perfectly level, and a visibly sloping shelf is both aesthetically unacceptable and structurally weaker. This requires very careful marking out and skilled use of a router or saw and chisel.

A rectangular piece of plywood measures 80 mm × 200 mm. A designer cuts out a triangle with a base of 40 mm and a height of 60 mm, and a circle with a radius of 20 mm. Calculate the area of plywood that is wasted. Show all working.

2
