Specification Criteria
Analysis
The set of requirements a product must satisfy — used both to guide the design process and to evaluate existing products. Includes form, function, user requirements, performance requirements, materials, scale of production, sustainability, aesthetics, marketability, and innovation.
"Before designing the new chair, the design team established the specification criteria — it must support 150 kg, be made from recyclable materials, weigh under 4 kg, and have an RRP under £80. Every design decision was checked against these criteria."
💡 Specification = the complete list of requirements. A product that meets ALL criteria is a successful design. A product that fails one criterion fails — even if it excels in all others. The exam will give you criteria and ask you to evaluate how well a product meets them.
Ergonomics
Analysis
The science of designing products that account for the strengths and limitations of people — so that the product fits the user well, reduces effort, and increases performance and safety. An ergonomically designed product feels natural and intuitive to use.
"The new garden tool was redesigned with ergonomic handles — angled to reduce wrist strain during repeated use, with a textured grip that requires less force to hold, significantly reducing fatigue and the risk of repetitive strain injury."
💡 Ergonomics = designing for human comfort, safety, and performance. Greek: ergon (work) + nomos (law). Related to anthropometric data (measurements of the human body) — ergonomics uses those measurements to design products that fit people. Examples: chair height, keyboard angle, car seat position, tool grip size.
SCAMPER
Strategies
A structured creative thinking technique used to generate new design ideas by systematically asking seven different types of question about an existing product or design. Developed by Alex Osborn (1953) and refined by Bob Eberle (1971). Forces designers to consider angles they might otherwise overlook.
"Applying SCAMPER to a standard bicycle: Substitute the chain with a belt drive? Combine with electric motor? Adapt for people with limited mobility? Modify the frame for cargo carrying? Put the handlebars to use as a storage bar? Eliminate the saddle for a standing design? Reverse the wheel sizes for a different aesthetic?"
💡 S=Substitute, C=Combine, A=Adapt, M=Modify, P=Put to other use, E=Eliminate, R=Reverse. A mnemonic that ensures you systematically explore many possible design directions. Use it in the exam if asked "how could this design be improved?" — work through SCAMPER letters.
User-Centred Design (UCD)
Strategies
A design approach that places the needs, preferences, and limitations of the end user at the centre of every decision throughout the entire design process. User feedback is gathered and incorporated at every stage to ensure the final product feels natural and intuitive to its intended users.
"When designing the new hospital website, the team used user-centred design — they tested every page with actual patients, including elderly users and those with visual impairments, adjusting font sizes, button positions, and colour contrast based on real user feedback before any code was finalised."
💡 UCD = users first, always. Not: "I think this is a good design." But: "Our users tested this and found it easy/hard to use." Methods: user testing, interviews, surveys, anthropometric data, observational studies. Websites, apps, medical devices, vehicles — all use UCD.
Systems Thinking
Strategies
A design strategy that analyses a product or problem as a complete system — looking at how each part or stage interacts with all others. Designers think backwards from the desired output to identify what processes and inputs are needed. Represented as input → process → output diagrams.
"The designers used systems thinking for the new heating controller: they first defined the output (comfortable room temperature), then the process (temperature sensing and valve control), then the inputs needed (electricity, target temperature setting, current temperature reading)."
💡 Systems thinking = start from the END and work backwards. 1) What do I want to OUTPUT? 2) What PROCESS achieves that? 3) What INPUTS does that process need? Signal types connect the stages: electrical, mechanical, light, sound. Used for electronics, apps, industrial processes, vending machines.
Isometric Drawing
Drawing
A 3D drawing technique where all lines recede at 30° to the horizontal and all edges are drawn at their true length (no foreshortening). More realistic looking than oblique because the scale is consistent throughout. Uses isometric grid paper for accuracy.
"The product designer produced an isometric drawing of the packaging prototype — the 30° grid ensured that all three visible faces appeared proportionally correct and all dimensions could be accurately scaled from the drawing."
💡 Isometric = 30° lines. ALL edges = true length (no foreshortening). Looks more realistic than oblique. Steps: draw a vertical line (front edge) → 30° construction lines left and right → fill in vertical lines → draw product. Contrast with oblique: oblique uses 45° and the receding edge is HALF true length.
Orthographic Projection
Drawing
A technical drawing method that shows a product from multiple views (front, side, top) in 2D with accurate dimensions and projection lines. Anyone — including a manufacturer who has never seen the product — can use an orthographic projection to make the product accurately without needing to speak to the designer.
"The furniture workshop produced the chair from an orthographic projection — the drawing showed the front, side, and top views with full dimensions, cutting lists, and material specifications, allowing the craftsperson to make every component accurately without any further instruction."
💡 Orthographic = multi-view technical drawing with dimensions. Two types: First angle (views projected in the direction of viewing — British standard) and Third angle (views projected in opposite direction — American standard). Used for engineering and manufacturing because it contains all the information needed to make the product.
CAD / CAM
CAD
CAD (Computer-Aided Design) = using computer software to create, develop, and modify designs digitally. CAM (Computer-Aided Manufacture) = using a computer to control manufacturing machines (laser cutter, CNC router, 3D printer) to produce components directly from CAD files.
"The designer created the product in CAD (using ProDesktop to model the 3D shape), then sent the file directly to the CAM machine — the CNC router cut the component from sheet material exactly as specified in the digital model, without any manual marking out or cutting."
💡 CAD = design on computer. CAM = make using computer-controlled machine. Key link: CAD file → CAM machine. Types of CAM: laser cutter (cuts 2D shapes), CNC router (cuts 3D shapes in solid material), 3D printer (adds material layer by layer). Advantage: eliminates human error in marking out and cutting.

Every product should be evaluated against its environmental impact across its FULL lifecycle — not just "it's made from recycled material." Consider energy use in manufacture AND during use, disposal at end of life, and whether components can be separated for recycling.

🎨 Influential Designers & Companies (Table 1.15.2)

You do not need to memorise all 8 — but you should know the key contribution of each, and be able to explain WHY they are influential, not just WHAT they produced.

AlessiCompany · Italy · est. 1921

Designer and mass producer of functional but visually appealing homeware and kitchen products. Commissions famous designers including Philippe Starck to create iconic products.

Why influential: Set the standard for other homeware companies by proving that everyday kitchen objects could be beautifully designed — including the Juicy Salif lemon squeezer and retro-styled kettles.
AppleCompany · USA · est. 1976

Producer of consumer electronics and software using cutting-edge technologies — iPad, iWatch, iPod, iPhone, iTunes.

Why influential: Ground-breaking design that looked completely different to anything before. The iPod made digital music accessible and mainstream. Developed one of the most loyal customer bases in the world.
Heatherwick StudioDesign studio · UK · est. 1994

Around 200 designers, architects, and makers who have worked on projects ranging from perfume bottles to Routemaster buses to Singapore University buildings.

Why influential: Stretches the boundaries of materials, craftsmanship, and artistic thinking — showing that products and buildings can be unusual, experimental, and visually extraordinary.
Joe Casely-HayfordFashion designer · born 1956

Original but wearable designs that push the barriers of conformity, made by master craftspeople using traditional English tailoring methods.

Why influential: Sets standards for British tailoring that combines style with character — popular with celebrities and influential in the fashion industry for pushing the boundaries of traditional British menswear.
PixarAnimation studio · USA · est. 1979

Among the first to develop computer-animated feature films, using new techniques and technologies to make commercially successful films.

Why influential: Pioneered computer animation as a mainstream art form with Toy Story and Finding Nemo — changing what was technically and artistically possible in animation and visual storytelling.
Raymond LoewyIndustrial designer · 1893–1986

"The father of modern design." Emphasised combining simplicity with functionality across 200+ companies and products ranging from refrigerators to planes and trains.

Why influential: Introduced the principle that if two products have the same price, function, and quality, the one with better aesthetics will always be more popular. Iconic work: Coca-Cola bottle, Le Creuset Coquelle, Shell and BP logos.
Tesla, Inc.Company · USA · est. 2008

Produces electric cars that don't compromise on power or quality, have zero emissions, are affordable, and can be charged at home. Tesla Model X SUV (2016).

Why influential: Leads electric vehicle design and technology — proving that sustainable, zero-emission cars can be desirable, high-performance vehicles rather than compromises.
Zaha HadidArchitect · 1950–2016

Integrated geometric forms with expressive, sweeping fluid shapes. Promoted architecture as a visual art form where buildings are intended to give aesthetic pleasure.

Why influential: Overcame racial and gender barriers to establish an architecture practice that designed over 1,000 iconic buildings worldwide — and proved that architecture can be a form of art.

1.16 — Use of Different Design Strategies

Topic 1.16 covers three key approaches to generating design ideas. The exam may ask you to "discuss which design strategies a designer might use" for a given product — and expects you to describe HOW each strategy would work in that context, not just name it.

📌 ESL Strategy — SCAMPER in Chinese: SCAMPER can be practised in your first language (L1). Take a familiar product and ask each SCAMPER question in Chinese (替代？结合？改编？修改？改变用途？消除？颠倒？). Thinking through creative challenges in L1 first, then translating the answers into English exam language, is a legitimate and effective strategy for ESL learners.

🔀 Collaboration & SCAMPER

Rarely does one designer produce everything alone. Collaboration — sharing perspectives — generates far more ideas. SCAMPER is a structured technique to force creative thinking.

👥 Why Collaboration?

In design companies, it is very rare for one person to be responsible for the whole design process. Collaboration means looking at problems from different perspectives, which generates many more ideas than working alone. It means asking other users or designers to spark your imagination.

SCAMPER — 7 Creative Questions

SSubstitute

Could you use different materials, components, or sources of energy? What if you replaced one key material with something new?

CCombine

Could you take successful parts from other ideas and form new combinations? What happens if you merge two different product functions?

AAdapt

Could you incorporate a different function or make use of a different technology? How could this product adapt to a different user's needs?

MModify

Could you change the aesthetics — shrink it, expand it, change the shape or colour? What if the form was completely redesigned?

PPut to use

Could this design have a second or third purpose? How could the same form serve a completely different function elsewhere?

EEliminate

Could you take away part of the design? Have you over-complicated it? What is truly essential vs what could be removed?

RReverse

Could you look at the design completely differently — inside out, upside down? Could you move parts around or flip the assembly order?

💡 SCAMPER in the exam: If a question asks "Suggest how the designer could generate more design ideas using a systematic technique" — describe SCAMPER. Explain that it stands for 7 types of question (name them), and then apply 2–3 of them to the specific product in the question. Always apply to the product — don't just list the letters.

👤 User-Centred Design

The most important design philosophy in modern product design — every decision is made with the user's needs, preferences, and limitations as the primary consideration.

🎯 What Is User-Centred Design?

User-centred design revolves around putting users' needs at the centre of every decision throughout the whole design process. This ensures that users' opinions are considered at every stage so that the design cannot go off at a tangent or get lost. It is used extensively in web design, app development, medical devices, and consumer products.

In your classroom, you put the user at the heart of design decisions — particularly in the generation of design ideas. Methods include:

  • Using user requirements as the starting point for all design ideas
  • Asking your user to draw out some ideas or make a simple model as a starting point to generate further ideas
  • Modelling your ideas in 3D to gain user feedback and aid development
  • Using anthropometric data from your user to directly influence design decisions — e.g. using hand measurements to determine the correct grip size

📏 Anthropometric Data

Anthropometric data = measurements of the human body. Designers use percentile data (e.g. 5th percentile, 50th percentile, 95th percentile) to ensure products fit the widest possible range of users.

  • Car seats must accommodate the smallest to the tallest drivers
  • Keyboard key size must suit the range of adult finger sizes
  • Children's furniture must be proportioned for children's bodies, not scaled-down adult furniture
  • Safety equipment (helmets, gloves) must fit the full range of head/hand sizes

⚙️ Systems Thinking

Systems thinking is used by designers to solve complex problems. It looks at a whole product or system and how each part contributes and feeds back into the whole.

🔄 How Systems Thinking Works

The simplest method is to create a flowchart — but start backwards from what you want to achieve:

  • Output first: What do you want your product to achieve? (e.g. dispense a drink)
  • Process: How is it going to achieve that function? (e.g. count coins, release mechanism)
  • Inputs: What inputs or energies does it need to do that? (e.g. user selection, coins, electricity)

This backwards thinking helps designers to not miss any critical component or stage. Product designers and engineers use this for mobile phones, baby monitors, kitchen timers, car alarms, heating systems — anything with multiple interacting parts.

Example: Vending Machine

📥 Inputs

  • User selection
  • Coins inserted
  • Electricity (power)
→

⚙️ Processes

  • Coins are counted
  • Selection is verified
  • Release mechanism activates
→

📤 Output

  • Drink is dispensed
  • Change is returned
💡 Signal types: Each arrow between stages carries a signal. Signals can be: electrical (voltage/current), mechanical (force/movement), light (optical), sound (acoustic), or thermal (heat). In the exam, name the signal type for each arrow in your systems diagram.

1.17 — Communication Techniques for Design Ideas

Topic 1.17 covers all the methods designers use to communicate their ideas — from quick freehand sketches to precise technical drawings and computer modelling. You must know the name, purpose, key characteristics, and typical use of each technique.

📌 ESL Strategy — Draw the Differences: The most reliable way to learn the 3D drawing types (oblique, isometric, perspective) is to draw the same simple object (a cube or a shoe box) using each technique. The act of drawing forces you to understand the geometric rules — which is exactly what the exam tests. Do this once, and you will remember the differences permanently.

✏️ Drawing Types — All Seven Techniques

Designers choose different drawing types for different stages of the design process and for different audiences. Know which type to use when.

Drawing TypeKey FeaturesWhen UsedAudience
Oblique
45°
 Simplest 3D. Draw front view in 2D → construction lines project back at 45°. Receding edges drawn at HALF true length. Use oblique grid paper. Quick 3D representations in early idea generation. Easy to learn and draw rapidly. Designer's own use; early-stage sketches; non-technical audience
Isometric
30°
 More realistic than oblique. All lines recede at 30° to horizontal. All edges drawn at TRUE length (no foreshortening). Start with a vertical line. Use isometric grid paper. Product visualisation, technical illustrations, assembly guides. More proportionally accurate than oblique. Designer, client, manufacturer; technical and non-technical
One-point perspective All lines project to ONE vanishing point in the distance. Start with front view → construction lines to single vanishing point → verticals between lines. Interior design; gives a strong sense of depth. Shows a viewer how a space or product would look from a specific viewpoint. Clients, customers, interior designers; non-technical audience
Two-point perspective Two vanishing points on either side of the object. Begin with a vertical line (front edge) → construction lines to both vanishing points. Most realistic of all 3D techniques. Architectural drawings, product exterior views. Most realistic visual appearance. Used by architects to show clients building exteriors. Clients, architects, planning applications; non-technical
Orthographic projection Multiple 2D views (front, side, top) with precise dimensions, projection lines, and material/finish notes. First angle (British) or third angle (American). Anyone can use to manufacture. Final engineering drawings for manufacture. All information needed to make the product without further clarification. Used in engineering and product manufacturing. Manufacturer, engineer, toolmaker; technical audience

Design development and final concept drawings; patent applications; understanding assembly sequences; design reviews. Designer, manufacturer, patent office; semi-technical
Assembly drawing Shows how all parts fit together and which component goes where. Each part numbered and named. Dimensioned detail drawings for each part. Tells manufacturer and customer how to assemble. Flat-pack furniture instructions (IKEA), construction manuals, product assembly guides. Crucial for products assembled by non-specialists. Manufacturer and END USER (customer assembling themselves); non-technical
💡 Oblique vs Isometric — the key exam distinction: Both are 3D drawing techniques. Oblique uses 45° lines and the receding edges are HALF true length. Isometric uses 30° lines and ALL edges are true length. Isometric looks more proportionally realistic. If the exam asks "which 3D drawing technique shows all edges at their true length?" — the answer is isometric.
💡 Construction lines: Faint sketched lines that help you build up the rough shape of a product before drawing the final lines within them. Construction lines should NOT be rubbed out — leave them faint as they show your working method.

🧊 Freehand Sketching, Models & Other Communication

Beyond formal drawing types, designers use a range of informal and physical communication methods — especially in early-stage ideation where speed is more important than precision.

✏️ Freehand Sketching

Quick generation of 2D or 3D ideas — gets thoughts from head to paper rapidly. Can be developed further with other techniques.

Mediums: pencils, ballpoint pens, fine liners, markers, coloured pencils. Grid paper or templates (e.g. human figure outline for scale). Use arrows to show movement (lids, buttons, zips).
📸 Digital Photography

Graphic designers use edited photos and digital media to develop complex or futuristic design ideas. Product designers place tracing paper over a photograph and adapt the image into a new product.

Useful when a design idea is too complex or futuristic to sketch easily. Also used for mood boards and design inspiration research.
✂️ Cut & Paste

Copy and paste parts of photographs, existing product images, or own design work — then edit and develop. Placing tracing paper over an image and adapting it is a low-tech version.

Creates rapid visual collages; useful for exploring colour, proportion, and form combinations without drawing from scratch.
🧊 3D Physical Models

When drawings are hard to visualise, 3D models make the design physical. Can be full size or scaled down depending on size and complexity.

Materials: paper, fabric, cardboard, Styrofoam (modelling foam shaped with hot wire cutter), HIPS, small building bricks. Allows user testing and feedback on physical form before committing to production.
📊 Systems Diagrams

Visual representation of a product or system showing inputs → processes → outputs with signal types. Used in electronics design to show how a system will function before schematic drawings are made.

Create a flowchart first (simple boxes and arrows), then develop into a full systems diagram with named signal types on each arrow.
📋 Schematic Diagrams

Detailed diagrams showing exactly what electronic components will be in a circuit — component names, values, and how they will be connected. Hand drawn or drawn using programs like SmartDraw.

Used by engineers to specify and communicate circuit designs precisely. A successful schematic clearly shows all component names, values, and provides labels to explain the intended purpose of each section.
🔮 3D Computer Models

Computer modelling allows designers to create, rotate, section, and simulate products before any physical material is used. Models can be sent directly to 3D printers or CNC machines.

System modelling uses stripboards or breadboards (electronics prototyping boards) to test circuits physically before committing to PCB manufacture.

💻 Computer-Aided Design (CAD) & CAM

CAD is now used by designers across all disciplines. The key advantage is that a CAD model can be directly connected to manufacturing (CAM) — eliminating manual marking out and reducing human error.

✏️ CAD Freehand Sketching

Programs such as Adobe Photoshop, 2D Design, and Pro Sketch allow designers to draw design ideas directly on screen — either from scratch or by enhancing hand-drawn sketches photographed and imported.

Advantage: easy to edit, undo, adjust colours and proportions without redrawing from scratch. Produces professional-looking presentations.
📐 2D CAD Modelling

Programs such as 2D Design and Adobe Illustrator create 2D designs that can be sent directly to a laser cutter (CAM) to produce cut-out models or final product components.

CAD → laser cutter workflow: design in 2D → set material and cut parameters → laser cutter cuts precisely from flat sheet. Used for: signs, acrylic components, packaging nets, model parts.
🧊 3D CAD Modelling

Programs such as ProDesktop, Fusion 360, and SolidWorks create 3D designs that can be sent to 3D printers or CNC routers to produce physical models or final components.

3D CAD → 3D printer: design model → slice software → print layer by layer. 3D CAD → CNC router: design model → toolpath software → CNC cuts from solid block. No manual marking out required.
⚡ System Design (CAD)

Programs such as Yenka Technology and PCB Wizard allow engineers to build and test electrical/electronic systems on screen — allowing components and layouts to be changed electronically before any physical components are purchased.

Virtual simulation = no wasted components. Changes are instant and free. The virtual system can be tested for faults before any physical prototype is built.

🔵 CAD — Computer-Aided Design

Using computer software to create, develop, modify, and visualise designs. Designs look realistic and professional. Easy to present to clients. Infinite undo. Files can be shared globally instantly.

🟢 CAM — Computer-Aided Manufacture

Using a computer to control manufacturing machines — laser cutter, CNC router, 3D printer. Eliminates human error in marking out and cutting. Machines can run 24/7. Very high precision and repeatability.

Activity 3 — True or False?

Test understanding
📝 Common misconceptions about drawing techniques and design strategies.
1. In an oblique drawing, all edges are drawn at their true length — the same as in an isometric drawing.
❌ False — this is the key distinction between oblique and isometric. In an isometric drawing, all edges ARE drawn at true length (the 30° grid ensures all dimensions are consistent). In an oblique drawing, the front face is drawn at true size in 2D, but the receding edges projecting backwards at 45° are drawn at HALF their true length. This is why oblique drawings can look slightly distorted compared to isometric ones.
2. An orthographic projection is primarily used to communicate design ideas to clients who are not technical specialists.
❌ False — orthographic projection is a technical drawing type aimed at manufacturers and engineers, not at non-technical clients. It shows the front, side, and top views in 2D with precise dimensions and projection lines — a format that requires technical knowledge to interpret. Non-technical clients are better served by perspective drawings (one-point or two-point) or isometric drawings, which show the product in a way that is immediately recognisable and intuitive without technical training.
3. User-centred design means that the designer simply asks the user what they want and then builds exactly that — without making any independent design judgements.
❌ False — user-centred design is not the same as "doing whatever the user says." Users often cannot articulate what they need — they describe what they already know. Henry Ford reportedly said that if he'd asked his customers what they wanted, they would have said "a faster horse." User-centred design means continuously testing designs with real users and gathering their feedback — but the designer still makes independent creative and technical judgements. The user's needs inform the design; the user doesn't create the design.
4. CAD (Computer-Aided Design) and CAM (Computer-Aided Manufacture) are two separate and unconnected processes — CAD is for designers and CAM is for factory engineers.
❌ False — the power of CAD/CAM is that they are directly connected. A design created in CAD software can be sent DIRECTLY to a CAM machine (laser cutter, CNC router, 3D printer) which reads the digital file and produces the component without any manual marking out. This CAD→CAM link eliminates human error in the transfer from design to manufacture, ensures perfect repeatability, and allows the same design to be produced identically thousands of times. The connection between CAD and CAM is what makes modern precision manufacturing possible.

Exam Language & Practice

Topics 1.15–1.17 generate "describe," "explain," "identify," and "discuss" questions. The most common question types: identifying the correct drawing technique for a given purpose, applying SCAMPER to a specific product, and explaining a designer's influence.

📌 ESL Strategy — Name → Define → Apply: For any drawing type question, use: (1) Name the technique, (2) Define its key characteristics (angle, true length, number of views), (3) Explain why it is most appropriate for this specific use/audience. Three steps = full marks.
Identifying and justifying the correct drawing type — 3 marks
For [purpose/audience], a [drawing type] would be most appropriate because [key property of that drawing type]. This suits this purpose because [how that property matches the requirement].
"For showing a furniture manufacturer exactly how to make each component of the chair design, an orthographic projection would be most appropriate because it shows the front, side, and top views in 2D with full dimensions and projection lines. This suits this purpose because a manufacturer who has never seen the product can use the dimensions shown to cut, shape, and assemble all components accurately without needing to consult the designer."
Applying SCAMPER to generate design ideas — 4 marks
Using the [letter] from SCAMPER, the designer could [specific idea]. This would [benefit or change]. Using the [letter] from SCAMPER, the designer could also [second idea].
"Using the S (Substitute) from SCAMPER, the designer could replace the standard plastic casing of the headphones with a recycled aluminium shell. This would improve sustainability and give a premium aesthetic feel that appeals to a different market segment. Using the E (Eliminate) from SCAMPER, the designer could also remove the cable entirely and make the headphones fully wireless, which would eliminate cable tangling and improve user freedom of movement."
Explaining a designer's influence — 3 marks
[Designer/company] is influential because [key contribution to design]. This influenced other designers by [broader impact on the design world].
"Raymond Loewy is influential because he introduced the principle that if two products have the same price, quality, and function, the one with more attractive aesthetics will always be more commercially successful. This influenced other designers by demonstrating that aesthetic quality is not a luxury but a commercial necessity — inspiring generations of product designers to prioritise visual appeal alongside function, rather than treating them as separate concerns."

📝 Practice Questions

Q1 · Drawing Techniques · 4 marks4 marks
A designer needs to communicate their new product idea. Explain the difference between an oblique drawing and an isometric drawing, and state ONE situation where each would be preferred.
💡 Oblique vs isometric: define the angle AND the key difference in edge lengths (1 mark each). Then one appropriate use for each (1 mark each).
1
Oblique: Lines project backwards at 45° to the horizontal. The front face is drawn at true size in 2D, but the receding edges going back are drawn at HALF their true length. It is the simplest 3D drawing technique. Best used for: quick idea generation sketches in early design stages, where speed is more important than precise proportions.
2
Isometric: All lines recede at 30° to the horizontal, and all edges are drawn at their TRUE length throughout — there is no foreshortening. This makes it more proportionally accurate and more realistic-looking than oblique. Best used for: product visualisation and technical illustrations where accurate proportions matter, such as assembly guides or concept presentations to clients.
Q2 · Design Strategies · 6 marks6 marks
Discuss which design strategies a designer might use when designing a new pair of wireless headphones. Refer to at least two different strategies and include a judgement about which would be most beneficial overall.
💡 High-mark question: describe at least 2 strategies applied TO headphones (not just defined generally). Include a justified judgement about which is most valuable. Well-balanced discussion scores highest.
1
User-centred design: The designer would continuously test prototypes with real users — music listeners, athletes, gamers — gathering feedback on comfort, sound quality, and ease of pairing. Anthropometric data of ear and head sizes would be used to design ear cups that fit the widest possible range of users. User testing at every stage ensures the final product feels intuitive and comfortable.
2
SCAMPER: Applying S (Substitute) — could the plastic headband be replaced with a flexible titanium alloy? A (Adapt) — could noise cancellation technology from aviation headsets be incorporated? E (Eliminate) — could the physical controls be eliminated in favour of touch gestures? This generates many more ideas than simply improving the existing design.
3
Judgement: User-centred design is likely to be the most beneficial strategy for wireless headphones because the product's success depends entirely on how comfortable and intuitive it is for the end user — a product that sounds technically excellent but is uncomfortable to wear for more than 30 minutes will fail commercially regardless of its specifications. SCAMPER is valuable for generating creative features but user-centred design ensures those features actually serve user needs.
