Filter:
Compression
Forces
A squashing or pushing force — forces push INWARD from both ends, trying to shorten and crush the material. Diagram arrows always point inward: ➡ beam ⬅
"Table legs are under compression — the weight of the table top and anything placed on it pushes down, trying to crush and shorten the legs."
💡 Compress = squeeze together. Arrows → ← point INWARD. Examples in timber: table legs, chair legs, columns, posts, the top face of a beam that is bending under load.
Tension
Forces
A stretching or pulling force — forces pull OUTWARD from both ends, trying to lengthen and tear the material apart. Timber's tensile strength is 3–4× greater than its compressive strength along the grain.
"The bottom face of a shelf under heavy load is in tension — as the shelf bends, the lower fibres are being stretched and pulled apart."
💡 Tension = stretching. Arrows ← → point OUTWARD. Timber is stronger in tension than compression. Examples: the underside of a bending beam, tie bars, ropes, a stretched guitar string.
Shear
Forces
Forces acting in opposite parallel directions, trying to slide adjacent layers of material apart — like the cutting action of scissors. Common at joints and when cutting across the grain.
"When a chisel is struck across the grain, it applies a shear force — pushing one layer of wood fibres sideways relative to the next layer until they separate."
💡 Shear = sliding apart. Arrows ↑ on one side, ↓ on the other. Think of scissors — two blades moving in opposite directions. Timber joints are often most vulnerable to shear forces.
Lamination
Strengthening
Bonding several thin strips of timber together using adhesive, bent into shape inside a former (mould). When the glue sets, the curve is permanent. Used to produce curved furniture components and large structural beams (Glulam, LVL).
"The curved chair back was made by lamination — thin beech strips were coated with PVA, placed in a shaped former and clamped overnight. When released, they held the curve permanently."
💡 Laminate = to layer. Many thin strips → each bends easily → glued in a mould → permanent shape. Glulam = Glued Laminated Timber (structural beams). LVL = Laminated Veneer Lumber. Both are stronger than solid timber of the same cross-section.
Triangulation
Strengthening
Adding diagonal members to a rectangular frame to create triangles. Triangles are rigid — their internal angles cannot change without a member breaking. Rectangles are not rigid — they rack (distort into a parallelogram) under sideways force.
"The bookcase frame was strengthened by triangulation — a diagonal brace from the top-left corner to the bottom-right corner created two rigid triangles, preventing the frame from racking sideways."
💡 Rule: TRIANGLES are rigid. RECTANGLES are NOT. One diagonal brace = two triangles. A triangle's angles cannot change without breaking a side — this is why bridges, roof trusses, and strong furniture frames all use triangulated structures.
Brace / Tie Bar
Strengthening
A brace is a diagonal bar working in COMPRESSION — it pushes back against racking. A tie bar (usually steel rod) works in TENSION — it cannot stretch, so it holds corners square. Both are diagonal across a rectangular frame.
"Steel tie bars are used in old brick buildings to hold the walls from spreading outward — the rod is in tension, pulling both walls back toward the centre."
💡 Brace = compression (pushes). Tie bar = tension (pulls). Steel is often used for tie bars because steel has extremely high tensile strength. Key question: is the diagonal member being pushed or pulled? That tells you which type it is.
Moulding
Stock Forms
A length of timber that has been machine-cut into a specific decorative cross-sectional profile along its whole length — skirting board, architrave, coving, dado rail, decorative edging. Sold in lengths and cut to size on site.
"Instead of routing a decorative edge on each shelf individually, the designer specified a ready-made hardwood moulding — saving significant time while ensuring a consistent, professional profile on every piece."
💡 A moulding = a decorative profile in a ready-made length. You cut it to size on site — no machining needed. Advantage: saves time. Disadvantage: more expensive per metre. Common types: skirting board (along walls at floor level), architrave (around door frames), coving (at wall-ceiling joint).
Dowel
Stock Forms
A cylindrical wooden rod available in standard diameters (4–25 mm). Short lengths are pressed into precisely drilled holes in both pieces of timber to create a hidden, aligned joint. Longer lengths are used as handles, rails, and model-making rods.
"The flat-pack bookcase used dowel joints — 8 mm wooden rods pressed into pre-drilled holes in both the shelf and the side panel, allowing accurate self-alignment without visible screws."
💡 Dowels = round wooden pegs. Common exam question: give one advantage and one disadvantage of a dowel joint. Advantage: machines can drill holes quickly and accurately. Disadvantage: very difficult to align holes accurately by hand — requires a dowelling jig.
PAR — Planed All Round
Sizes
Timber with all four faces machine-planed smooth and edges slightly rounded. Used mainly for hidden structural timber (studwork, joists, floor framing) where the appearance of the timber itself does not matter. Actual size is approximately 4 mm less than the stated nominal size.
"The stud wall was framed with PAR 100 × 50 mm — the rounded edges made it safer to handle on site, even though it actually measured only 96 × 47 mm after planing."
💡 PAR = Planed ALL Round = smooth all 4 faces + ROUNDED edges. Used for HIDDEN structure. Actual size is always smaller than the nominal size: 100 × 50 PAR ≈ 96 × 47 mm actual. Rounded edges = PAR. Do not confuse with PSE.
PSE — Planed Square Edge
Sizes
Timber with all four faces machine-planed smooth and edges left sharp and square. Used for visible joinery — window frames, door linings, skirting boards, and furniture — where a precise, clean edge is essential for neat joints and a good painted or varnished finish.
"The window frame was made from PSE softwood — the sharp square edges gave clean 90° corner joints and a smooth surface ready for painting without any additional preparation."
💡 PSE = Planed SQUARE Edge = smooth all 4 faces + SQUARE (sharp) edges. Used for VISIBLE joinery. Key difference from PAR: PSE has SQUARE edges; PAR has ROUNDED edges. PSE costs more — only specify it where timber will be seen.

🌳 Natural Forces in Growing Timber
As a tree grows, wind loading, the weight of branches, and the tree's own mass create internal stresses within the wood. When the tree is felled and sawn into planks, these stresses are suddenly released — the plank can bend, twist, crack, or bow as it dries. This is why freshly sawn green timber must be seasoned before use — not only to remove moisture, but to allow internal stresses to stabilise before the wood is cut to final dimensions.
⚡ Pre-Stressed Construction Beams
During manufacture, the bottom rail of a structural beam is deliberately placed in tension — stretched before the beam is assembled. When a load is placed on the beam in use, the bottom naturally goes into tension from bending. The pre-existing tension partly cancels this additional tension, so the beam bends less and carries more load for the same amount of timber. This is widely used in floor joists, glulam beams, and bridge girders.
💡 Bending beam key point: When a beam bends under load, the TOP face is in COMPRESSION (being squashed) and the BOTTOM face is in TENSION (being stretched). This is why pre-stressing puts tension into the bottom — it is the tension side that needs to be strengthened.

🔺 Frame Structures & Triangulation
A square or rectangular frame can distort under sideways force (racking). Solutions: (1) thin panel inside the frame, (2) diagonal strut across one corner, (3) diagonal corner-to-corner creating two triangles.
Rule: Triangles are rigid. Rectangles are not. One diagonal converts a rectangle into two rigid triangles that cannot change shape without a member breaking.
🌀 Lamination
Thin timber strips are coated with adhesive, placed in a shaped former (mould) and clamped until set. The curved shape is held permanently. Also greatly increases strength compared to solid timber of the same size.
Glulam = Glued Laminated Timber — large structural beams for buildings
LVL = Laminated Veneer Lumber — precision engineered beams
↗️ Braces & Tie Bars
Both are diagonal members added to rectangular frames to prevent racking:
Brace = diagonal bar working in COMPRESSION — it pushes back
Tie bar = diagonal rod (often steel) working in TENSION — it cannot stretch, so it holds corners square
Steel tie bars are common because steel has very high tensile strength.
⚙️ Fabrication & Joint Selection
The right joint type for the expected force direction significantly affects structural strength. Strong joints for load-bearing: mortise and tenon, dovetail, housing joint. Weaker joints: butt joint, mitre joint.

🔬 Embedding Composite Materials
Adding other materials into or around timber to improve specific properties:
GRP (Glass Reinforced Plastic / fibreglass): waterproof, hardwearing shell around a beam
Steel strips: very high tensile strength, can be bonded along the tension face

📐 Sizes, PAR, PSE & Calculations (7.5.2)

PAR vs PSE — The Essential Distinction

PAR — Planed All Round

  • All four faces machine-planed smooth
  • Edges are ROUNDED for safer handling
  • Used for HIDDEN structural timber
  • Studwork, floor joists, noggins, roof rafters
  • Actual size ~4 mm less than nominal stated size
  • 100 × 50 mm PAR → actual ~96 × 47 mm

PSE — Planed Square Edge

  • All four faces machine-planed smooth
  • Edges are SQUARE (sharp) for neat joints
  • Used for VISIBLE joinery work
  • Door frames, window linings, skirting boards, furniture
  • Sold in 1.8 m lengths — check for warp before use
  • More expensive than PAR — only specify when seen

🔢 Maths Corner — Cross-Sectional Area

Section B includes 5 calculation marks. Cross-sectional area is one of the most likely calculations. Always show your working — partial marks are awarded even if your final answer is wrong.

Cross-Sectional Area (mm²) = Width (mm) × Thickness (mm)
Worked Example: Cross-sectional area of a 100 mm × 50 mm timber section.
1
Write the formula: Area = Width × Thickness
2
Substitute the values: Area = 100 × 50
3
Calculate: Area = 5,000 mm²
⚠️ Always write mm² — the squared unit. Writing "5000" or "5000 mm" is wrong and costs marks. The common mistake is ADDING instead of MULTIPLYING: 100 + 50 = 150 is perimeter (not area).

Activity 4 — True or False?

Test understanding
📝 These statements test the most common misconceptions in Topics 7.4 and 7.5. Read the explanation carefully after each question.
1. A rectangular timber frame is structurally strong because all four sides support each other against forces acting from any direction.
❌ False. A rectangular frame is inherently weak — it racks (distorts into a parallelogram) under sideways force because the corner angles are free to change. The only way to fix this is triangulation: adding a diagonal brace converts the rectangle into two triangles, and triangles cannot change their internal angles without a member breaking. This is why all strong frames (bridge trusses, roof trusses, braced furniture) use triangulation.
2. Lamination can produce curved timber shapes that would be impossible to achieve from a single solid piece, and also produces a stronger result than solid timber.
✅ True — both claims are correct. A thick solid piece of timber is too stiff to bend into a curve without breaking. Lamination solves this because each individual thin strip bends easily — they are then glued together in a mould (former) and held until the glue sets permanently. Additionally, the resulting laminated component is generally stronger than solid timber of the same cross-section because the alternating grain directions in the strips mutually reinforce each other, similar to the way plywood works.
3. PSE timber should be specified for hidden structural work such as stud wall framing, because its sharp square edges make it easier to nail pieces together at accurate right angles.
❌ False — this confuses PAR and PSE. PAR (Planed All Round) is the correct specification for hidden structural timber. Its rounded edges make it safer to handle on site. PSE (Planed Square Edge) is for VISIBLE joinery — window frames, door linings, skirting boards — where a precise, sharp edge is needed for neat joints and a clean painted finish. Specifying PSE for stud wall framing would waste money unnecessarily on a premium timber finish that will never be seen.
4. The cross-sectional area of a 50 mm × 75 mm timber section is 125 mm².
❌ False — 125 mm is the result of adding instead of multiplying. Cross-sectional AREA = 50 × 75 = 3,750 mm². Adding 50 + 75 = 125 gives you the sum of two sides — not area. Remember: Area = width × thickness (MULTIPLY). The unit is always mm² (millimetres squared). This is one of the most common calculation mistakes in the exam.
5. In a pre-stressed construction beam, the top face of the beam is deliberately tensioned during manufacture because this is where the greatest bending force acts.
❌ False — it is the BOTTOM face (bottom rail), not the top. When a beam bends under load, the top face is in COMPRESSION (being squashed) and the bottom face is in TENSION (being stretched). It is the bottom face that needs to be pre-tensioned — because pre-existing tension in the bottom rail partly cancels the additional tension caused by bending, meaning the beam bends less and carries more load. The top face is already under compression from the load, so pre-tensioning the top would actually make it worse.

Describe the forces of compression and tension. For each force, give one example of where it acts in a timber furniture product.
💡 Two marks per force: 1 mark for a correct definition + 1 mark for a specific named product example. Use different products for each force.
1
Compression: A squashing force that pushes inward from both ends, trying to shorten and crush the material. Example: the legs of a dining table are under compression — the weight of the table top and anything placed on it acts downward, compressing the legs.
2
Tension: A stretching force that pulls outward from both ends, trying to lengthen and tear the material apart. Example: the bottom rail of a shelf unit is under tension — the weight of books on the shelf causes the shelf to bow slightly, which stretches the lower fibres of the timber apart.
Q2 · Reinforcement · 3 marks3 marks
Explain how lamination can be used to produce a curved component for a chair back, and give one advantage of lamination compared to cutting the same curve from a solid piece of timber.
💡 Explain the process (thin strips → glue → former → clamp → set) for 2 marks, then one specific advantage for 1 mark.
1
Process: Thin strips of timber (2–3 mm thick) are coated with adhesive on each face. The strips are stacked together and placed in a shaped former (mould). Clamps hold the strips in the curved shape until the adhesive has fully cured. When released from the former, the laminated strips permanently hold the curve.
2
Advantage: The laminated curved component is stronger than one cut from solid timber — cutting a curve from solid timber cuts across the grain at the curved sections, creating a short-grain weakness. The laminated version maintains grain direction throughout, producing a much stronger, more reliable component with no weak short-grain sections.

🔄 Technical Term → Plain English
Compression→A squashing force — pushes inward from both ends
Tension→A pulling/stretching force — pulls outward from both ends
Shear→A sliding force in opposite parallel directions — like scissors
Triangulation→Adding diagonals to make triangles — rigid, cannot rack
Lamination→Gluing thin layers together in a mould to make a curved, strong piece
Racking→When a rectangular frame twists sideways into a diamond/parallelogram shape
PAR→Planed All Round — smooth 4 faces, rounded edges, hidden structural use
PSE→Planed Square Edge — smooth 4 faces, sharp square edges, visible joinery
Nominal size→The stated (named) size — actual planed size is a few mm smaller
Cross-sectional area→The area of the cut end = width × thickness, in mm²
🚨 Common Exam Mistakes — Forces & Stock Forms
❌ Avoid writing this✅ Write this insteadWhy?
"The force squeezes the wood""Compression is a squashing force acting inward from both ends, trying to shorten and crush the material"Always name the force type and state the direction of action
"Area = 50 + 100 = 150 mm""Area = 50 × 100 = 5,000 mm²"Area = MULTIPLY. Adding gives perimeter. Unit MUST be mm² not mm
"Use PSE timber for the stud wall""Use PAR for hidden structural work (studwork). PSE is for visible joinery only."PSE = visible joinery. PAR = hidden structure. This distinction is worth 1 mark.
"Lamination makes wood stronger by adding layers""Lamination bonds thin strips in a former to create permanent curves and produces beams with higher strength than solid timber of the same cross-section"Name both advantages — curved shapes AND increased strength
"A tie bar and a brace are the same thing""A brace resists compression; a tie bar (usually steel rod) resists tension — they work differently"Different force directions — this distinction is frequently examined
"Rectangles are strong structures""Rectangles are inherently weak — they rack under sideways force. Triangulation must be added to make them rigid."A very common misconception — rectangles look stable but are not
