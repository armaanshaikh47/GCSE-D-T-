Key Vocabulary

24 key terms across all four material categories. These words appear most often in exam questions — knowing their exact definitions is essential for "explain" and "describe" questions.

📌 ESL Strategy — Precision Vocabulary: In material science, two words that look similar often mean very different things (e.g. ductility vs malleability; thermoforming vs thermosetting). Study these pairs by writing one clear sentence that includes BOTH words and explains the difference between them. This forces precise understanding rather than approximate memory.
Filter:
Ferrous Metal
Metals
A metal that contains iron (ferrite). Most ferrous metals are magnetic and will rust when exposed to moisture — except for stainless steel and wrought iron. Small amounts of other elements can be added to improve properties.
"Ferrous metals such as mild steel are magnetic and will corrode in damp conditions unless protected by paint, galvanising, or plating."
💡 Ferro = iron (Latin). Ferrous = contains iron. Easy test: if a magnet sticks to it, it is probably ferrous. Ferrous metals: mild steel, stainless steel, cast iron. Key weakness: most will RUST (except stainless steel).
Alloy
Metals
A mixture of two or more metals (or a metal and a non-metal) that has improved properties compared to either element alone. Alloys are engineered for specific applications where a pure metal lacks a required property.
"Stainless steel is an alloy of carbon steel with chromium and nickel added — the pure iron would rust, but the chromium addition makes it highly corrosion resistant."
💡 Alloy = a designed mix. Brass = copper + zinc. Stainless steel = steel + chromium + nickel. The additions improve the base metal — stronger, more resistant, harder, lighter. Pure metals are rarely used in engineering.
Ductility
Property
The ability of a material to deform under tensile (pulling) force without fracturing — being drawn out into a thin wire while remaining intact. Highly ductile metals: copper, aluminium, mild steel.
"Copper has very high ductility — it can be drawn into electrical wire thinner than a human hair without breaking, making it essential for wiring and electrical components."
💡 Ductile = can be drawn (pulled into wire). Duct = tube/channel — imagine pulling metal through a tiny hole. Opposite: brittle (breaks instead of bending). DUCTILITY = wire drawing. Do not confuse with malleability.
Malleability
Property
The ability of a material to be permanently deformed in all directions without fracturing — beaten, pressed, or rolled into sheets. Highly malleable metals: aluminium, copper, gold, lead.
"Aluminium has excellent malleability — it can be pressed or rolled into very thin foil or formed into complex shapes using dies without cracking."
💡 Malleable = can be shaped with a hammer (mallet). Malleability = shape by pressing/hammering in ANY direction. Ductility = shape by PULLING into wire. The key difference: ductility = one direction (wire); malleability = all directions (sheet).
Corrugated Board
Papers & Boards
A sandwich structure consisting of a fluted (wavy) paper core bonded between two flat liner boards. The fluted centre creates air gaps which absorb impact, making it the best board for protective packaging. It is recyclable but not water resistant.
"The electronic components were shipped in a corrugated board box — the fluted core absorbed the vibrations and impacts of transit, protecting the fragile items inside."
💡 Corrugated = wavy/ridged shape. The flutes (waves) in the middle act like a spring — they absorb energy from impacts. Advantage: strong and protective. Disadvantage: absorbs water and loses strength when wet. Fully recyclable.
Biodegradability
Papers & Boards
The ability of a material to be broken down by bacteria and other micro-organisms into natural compounds. Most uncoated papers and boards are biodegradable. Compostable materials biodegrade within 12 weeks. Coated or laminated papers may not biodegrade.
"Uncoated kraft paper is fully biodegradable — it breaks down in soil within weeks, unlike plastic-coated paper which resists decomposition for many years."
💡 Bio = life. Degrade = break down. Biodegradable = broken down by living things (bacteria). Key distinction: biodegradable ≠ recyclable. Some things biodegrade but cannot be recycled (food-contaminated paper). Compostable = biodegrades in <12 weeks.
Thermoforming Polymer
Polymers
A polymer that softens when heated and can be reshaped. The polymer chains are linear (not cross-linked), so they can slide past each other when hot. Can be recycled by reheating and remoulding. Examples: acrylic, HIPS, Biopol®.
"HIPS is a thermoforming polymer — it softens at around 100°C and can be vacuum formed over a mould to create a tray. The same material can be melted again and remoulded, making it recyclable."
💡 Thermo = heat. Forming = shaping. Thermoforming = shaped by heat. KEY: polymer chains are LINEAR (like spaghetti strands) → they slide past each other when hot → CAN reshape → CAN recycle. Recycling symbol on packaging → thermoforming.
Thermosetting Polymer
Polymers
A polymer that permanently hardens when heated for the first time and cannot be reshaped by reheating. The polymer chains form permanent cross-links during curing, locking the shape. Cannot be recycled. Examples: polyester resin, urea formaldehyde.
"Urea formaldehyde is a thermosetting polymer used in electrical plug casings — once moulded and cured, it cannot be re-melted, making it safe near heat sources but impossible to recycle."
💡 Thermosetting = SET by heat (permanently). KEY: polymer chains form CROSS-LINKS when cured → locked in place → CANNOT reshape → CANNOT recycle. Think of it like baking a cake: you cannot un-bake it. Cross-links = the locked bridge between chains.
Natural Fibre
Textiles
Fibres obtained from plant or animal sources rather than manufactured chemically. Plant sources: cotton, flax, hemp, sisal, jute. Animal sources: silk, wool, mohair. Natural fibres are generally biodegradable but require significant land and water to produce.
"Wool is a natural fibre from sheep — its protein structure creates a crimped, scaly surface that traps air, giving it excellent thermal insulation properties that synthetic fibres struggle to match."
💡 Natural = from nature. Two sources: PLANT (cotton, linen/flax, hemp — made of cellulose) and ANIMAL (wool, silk, mohair — made of protein). Generally biodegradable and breathable. Disadvantage: can shrink, crease, be attacked by insects (wool → moths; cotton → mildew).
Resilience (Textiles)
Property
The ability of a fabric to return to its original shape after being stretched, compressed, or creased. Resilient fabrics resist permanent deformation. Wool has excellent resilience — creases fall out. Polyester is also highly resilient and resists creasing.
"Wool's natural resilience means that a wool suit will return to its shape after wearing — creases fall out overnight, reducing the need for ironing compared to cotton garments."
💡 Resilient = bounces back. A resilient fabric "remembers" its original shape and returns to it. High resilience = fewer creases, retains shape after use. Wool and polyester = high resilience. Cotton = low resilience (creases easily).
Elasticity (Textiles)
Property
The ability of a fabric to stretch under force and then return to its original length when the force is removed. Elastane (Lycra) has very high elasticity. Knitted fabrics have more elasticity than woven ones due to their looped structure. Not the same as resilience.
"Swimwear uses fabrics blended with elastane for their elasticity — the fabric stretches with the swimmer's body movements and springs back to shape when relaxed."
💡 Elastic = stretchy and returns. Elasticity specifically refers to STRETCHING → RETURNING. Different from resilience (shape recovery from general deformation). Knitted fabrics are more elastic than woven. Elastane/Lycra is the most elastic fibre.

🔩 1.8 — Ferrous & Non-Ferrous Metals

Spec ref: 1.8.1–1.8.3 | Know the composition, key properties, and applications of all six metals. The most examined question: "Explain why [metal X] is used for [product Y]."

🔑
The Key Distinction
Ferrous metals contain iron and are usually magnetic. Most rust when exposed to moisture. Non-ferrous metals do not contain iron — they are generally lighter, more corrosion-resistant, and better electrical conductors, but often more expensive.

Key Properties — Definitions

💪 Ductility

Ability to be drawn out into a wire under tensile force without fracturing. Most important for: electrical wiring, copper pipes. High ductility: copper, aluminium, mild steel.

🔨 Malleability

Ability to be permanently deformed in ALL directions (pressed, rolled, beaten into sheets) without fracturing. High malleability: aluminium, copper, gold, lead.

💎 Hardness

Resistance to indentation, scratching, or surface deformation. Important for: cutting tools, dies, brake components. High hardness: cast iron skin, stainless steel, tool steel.

💡 Ductility vs Malleability — the exam distinction: Ductility = pulling in ONE direction into wire. Malleability = deforming in ALL directions into sheets. Copper is both highly ductile AND highly malleable — but the exam may ask which property is most relevant for a specific use. Copper wiring → ductility. Copper roofing → malleability.

📄 1.9 — Papers & Boards

Spec ref: 1.9.1–1.9.3 | Key distinction: paper is under 220 gsm; board is over 220 gsm (measured in microns for thickness). Know the three-layer structure of folding boxboard.

🔑
Paper vs Board
Paper = under 220 gsm (grams per square metre). Thin, flexible, used for writing/drawing/printing. Board = over 220 gsm. Thick, stiff, used for packaging and structural products. GSM = a measure of weight/thickness — higher gsm = heavier/thicker.

Key Properties

🌀 Flexibility

For boards: the combination of stiffness (flexural stiffness — resists bending) and handling stiffness (feels rigid in hand). Corrugated board has high flexibility tolerance due to its sandwich structure.

🖨️ Printability

The ability to accept a printed image clearly and accurately. Affected by surface smoothness, finish, and coating. Folding boxboard and solid white board have excellent printability due to their smooth bleached surfaces.

🌱 Biodegradability

Most uncoated papers and boards biodegrade naturally. Compostable = biodegrades in under 12 weeks. Coated or plastic-laminated papers may not biodegrade and cannot be recycled normally.

🧪 1.10 — Thermoforming & Thermosetting Polymers

Spec ref: 1.10.1–1.10.3 | The single most important distinction in this topic: thermoforming CAN be recycled; thermosetting CANNOT. This comes down to whether the molecular chains are cross-linked.

♻️
Thermoforming Polymers
Polymer chains are LINEAR — they slide past each other when heated, allowing reshaping. Heat softens → reshape → cool to re-harden. CAN be recycled.
🚫
Thermosetting Polymers
Polymer chains form permanent CROSS-LINKS during curing. These locks cannot be broken by heat. Burns/chars instead of melting. CANNOT be recycled.

Key Properties

🌡️ Insulator of Heat

Most polymers are poor conductors of heat — they do not transfer heat well. This makes them ideal for handles, casings, and anything that must be safe to touch near heat sources. Urea formaldehyde is used for kettle and iron handles.

⚡ Insulator of Electricity

Polymers do not conduct electricity — electrons cannot flow through them. This is why PVC coats electrical cables and urea formaldehyde is used for plug casings. Critical for safety in all electrical applications.

🤜 Toughness

The ability to withstand sudden impact without breaking. HIPS (High Impact Polystyrene) is specifically engineered for impact resistance. Acrylic is tougher than standard glass but less tough than polycarbonate.

Key Properties

↕️ Elasticity

The ability to stretch under force and return to original shape. Knitted fabrics are more elastic than woven. Elastane/Lycra has the highest elasticity. Important for sportswear, swimwear, and fitted garments.

🔁 Resilience

The ability to recover original shape after being stretched or compressed. Wool has very high resilience — creases fall out naturally. Polyester is also highly resilient. Cotton has low resilience (creases easily).

🛡️ Durability

The ability to resist wear, abrasion, and repeated washing without degrading. Polyester and nylon are very durable. Cotton is durable but can weaken with repeated bleaching. Wool can be damaged by incorrect washing.

Activity 3 — True or False?

Test understanding
📝 These statements target the most common misconceptions across all four material topics. Many students get these wrong in the exam — read the explanations carefully.
1. All ferrous metals rust — including stainless steel — because they all contain iron which reacts with water and oxygen.
❌ False. Stainless steel is a ferrous metal (it contains iron) but it does NOT rust. The chromium content (10.5–18%) reacts with oxygen to form an invisible, protective chromium oxide layer on the surface. This self-repairing layer prevents moisture from reaching the iron underneath, making stainless steel highly corrosion resistant. Wrought iron is also relatively rust-resistant despite containing iron.
2. Thermoforming polymers can be recycled because their molecular chains are linear (not cross-linked) — allowing them to soften and be reshaped when heated.
✅ True — and this is the most important distinction in Topic 1.10. Thermoforming polymers have linear (straight) molecular chains. When heated, these chains can slide past each other, allowing the material to soften and be reshaped. When cooled, they harden again. This process can be repeated — hence they can be recycled. Thermosetting polymers form permanent cross-links between chains during curing, locking the structure permanently so they cannot be reheated and remoulded.
3. Ductility and malleability are the same property described in different ways — both refer to a metal's ability to change shape without breaking.
❌ False — these are different properties with specific definitions. Ductility = the ability to be drawn out into a wire under TENSILE (pulling) force — deformation in ONE direction (lengthways). Malleability = the ability to be deformed permanently in ALL directions by pressing, rolling, or hammering — without fracturing. A metal can be highly ductile but less malleable (e.g. steel wire drawing works, but sheet pressing would crack it at some thicknesses).
4. Corrugated board is a better choice than folding boxboard for cereal packaging because it is stronger and more impact resistant.
❌ False — folding boxboard is more appropriate for cereal packaging. Folding boxboard has a white, bleached outer surface that accepts high-quality full-colour printing, a food-safe inner layer, and can be scored and folded cleanly into box nets. Corrugated board is too thick and its fluted structure does not accept high-quality printing. Corrugated board is better for shipping boxes where impact resistance is needed, not retail food packaging where printability and appearance matter most.
5. Weft-knitted fabrics are more stable and less likely to ladder than warp-knitted fabrics.
❌ False — this is the opposite. Warp-knitted fabrics are MORE stable and ladder-resistant. In warp knitting, many yarns loop along the length simultaneously — if one loop breaks, the structure does not unravel easily. Weft-knitted fabrics (like most jumpers and T-shirts) use a single yarn looping across the width — if a loop breaks, a ladder can run down the fabric. Tights use warp knitting specifically because ladder-resistance is essential.

Exam Language & Practice

Material categorisation questions follow predictable patterns. The two most common types are "Explain why [material] is suitable for [product]" and "Give the difference between [material A] and [material B]."

📌 ESL Strategy — Property → Consequence → Product: The strongest exam answers always link three things: the PROPERTY of the material → the CONSEQUENCE of that property → why it matters for the SPECIFIC PRODUCT. E.g. "Aluminium has a high strength-to-weight ratio [PROPERTY] which means the drinks can is strong enough to hold the pressurised liquid [CONSEQUENCE] while remaining light enough to be carried and transported efficiently [PRODUCT RELEVANCE]."
Explain why [material] is used for [product] — 2 marks
[Material] is suitable for [product] because it has [specific property]. This is important because [why that property matters for that product].
"Stainless steel is suitable for kitchen sinks because it has excellent corrosion resistance due to the chromium content forming a protective oxide layer. This is important because sinks are constantly exposed to water and cleaning chemicals that would cause mild steel to rust rapidly."
Give the difference between thermoforming and thermosetting — 2 marks
[Thermoforming/Thermosetting] polymers have [molecular structure] which means they [can/cannot] be reshaped by heating. Unlike [the other type], they [can/cannot] be recycled.
"Thermoforming polymers have linear (non-cross-linked) molecular chains which means they can be softened and reshaped by reheating. Unlike thermosetting polymers (which have permanent cross-links and cannot be reheated), they can be recycled by remelting and remoulding."
Compare natural and synthetic fibres — 4 marks
[Natural fibre] is suitable for [use] because [advantage]. However, [synthetic fibre] may be preferred when [use requiring different property] because [advantage of synthetic].
"Wool is suitable for winter coats because its crimped fibres trap air, providing excellent thermal insulation and keeping the wearer warm. However, polyester may be preferred when making sportswear or raincoats because it dries quickly, resists staining, and does not absorb moisture — unlike wool which becomes heavy and dries slowly when wet."

📝 Practice Questions

Q1 · Metals · 2 marks2 marks
Explain two reasons why aluminium is used for drinks cans rather than mild steel.
💡 Two marks = two separate explained reasons. Each needs a property AND a consequence. Think: weight, corrosion resistance, recyclability.
1
Corrosion resistance: Aluminium naturally forms a protective oxide layer that prevents rusting — this is essential for drinks cans that contain acidic or carbonated liquids that would rapidly corrode mild steel.
2
Lightweight / excellent strength-to-weight ratio: Aluminium is significantly lighter than steel for the same strength — this reduces transportation costs and makes the can comfortable to hold and carry, while still withstanding the internal pressure of carbonated drinks.
Q2 · Papers · 4 marks4 marks
A company is choosing between corrugated board and folding boxboard for a new product. Give one reason why corrugated board is suitable for shipping the product, and one reason why it might not be suitable for the retail packaging of the same product.
💡 One advantage for shipping (impact resistance) + one disadvantage for retail (printability/appearance). Each answer needs property + consequence.
1
Suitable for shipping: The fluted core of corrugated board absorbs impact and vibration during transit, protecting fragile products from damage. The air trapped in the flutes acts like a cushion against shocks and drops during handling and transportation.
2
Not suitable for retail packaging: The corrugated surface does not accept high-quality printing — the uneven fluted texture makes it impossible to print sharp, detailed graphics and text with the colour accuracy required for premium retail shelf display. Folding boxboard, with its smooth bleached white outer surface, would give a far superior printed result.
Q3 · Polymers · 4 marks4 marks
Explain the difference in molecular structure between a thermoforming polymer and a thermosetting polymer, and state one consequence of this difference in terms of recycling.
💡 Describe LINEAR chains (thermoforming) vs CROSS-LINKED chains (thermosetting) for 2 marks. State the recycling consequence for 2 marks.
1
Thermoforming structure: Thermoforming polymers have linear (straight) molecular chains that are not bonded to each other. When heated, these chains can slide past each other, allowing the material to soften and be reshaped.
2
Thermosetting structure: Thermosetting polymers form permanent covalent cross-links between their polymer chains during the curing process. These cross-links lock the structure rigidly in place and cannot be broken by reheating — the material will char or burn rather than melt.
3
Recycling consequence: Thermoforming polymers CAN be recycled — they can be remelted, reformed, and reused. Thermosetting polymers CANNOT be recycled because the permanent cross-links prevent them from softening again.

🔄 Technical Term → Plain English
Ferrous metal→A metal containing iron — usually magnetic, usually rusts
Alloy→A designed mix of two or more metals with improved properties
Ductility→Can be pulled into wire without breaking
Malleability→Can be pressed/hammered into sheets in any direction
Thermoforming→Can be re-softened by heat and reshaped → recyclable
Thermosetting→Permanently set by heat → cannot recycle
Cross-linked→Polymer chains permanently bonded together — cannot slide
Biodegradable→Broken down naturally by bacteria — decomposes in soil
