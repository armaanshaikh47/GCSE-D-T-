Hardwood
Hardwoods
Timber from a deciduous tree — a tree with broad leaves that loses its leaves in winter (or from tropical trees with large leaves). Hardwood trees grow slowly (100+ years) and produce dense, durable timber. Generally more expensive than softwood.
"Hardwood such as oak is chosen for high-quality furniture because its density and grain make it durable and attractive."
💡 Confusingly, hardwood does NOT always mean hard! Balsa is a hardwood but it is extremely soft. The name refers to the TYPE of tree (deciduous/broad-leaved), not the hardness. Balsa is a hardwood because it comes from a broad-leaved tree.
Oak
Hardwood · European
A strong, hard, durable hardwood with a distinctive grain. It is resistant to moisture and insects, making it suitable for interior and exterior use. One of the most commercially important European hardwoods.
"Oak is commonly used for high-quality furniture, flooring, and wine barrels because of its strength, attractive grain, and natural resistance to moisture."
💡 Oak key facts: very strong and durable, distinctive grain, resistant to insects and moisture, heavy and expensive. Origin: European forests (also North American). Used for: furniture, flooring, doors, barrels.
Mahogany
Hardwood · Tropical
A reddish-brown tropical hardwood with a fine, straight grain. It is easy to work and takes a high finish. However, it comes from tropical rainforests (South America, Africa, Indonesia) and its use raises serious sustainability concerns.
"Mahogany was historically popular for fine furniture, but designers now use FSC-certified sources or alternatives to avoid contributing to rainforest deforestation."
💡 Mahogany key facts: reddish-brown, fine grain, easy to work, good finish. BUT — sustainability concern! It comes from tropical rainforests. Always mention FSC certification when discussing mahogany in an exam.
Beech
Hardwood · European
A hard, straight-grained hardwood with a pale pinkish-brown colour. It is tough and easy to work, making it popular for furniture and tool handles. It does not resist rot well outdoors.
"Beech is commonly used for school chairs and workbenches because it is hard, tough, and resists wear — ideal for products that experience heavy use."
💡 Beech key facts: pale pinkish-brown, hard, tough, good finish, NOT good outdoors (rots). Used for: chairs, workbenches, tool handles, toys. Origin: European forests.
Balsa
Hardwood · Tropical
The lightest commercially available timber — despite being classified as a hardwood. It is extremely soft, very easy to cut and shape, but not strong enough for structural uses. Grown in tropical South America.
"Balsa wood is ideal for model aircraft and prototype structures — its extremely low density makes it lightweight, allowing designers to test forms without adding significant weight."
💡 Balsa key exam trap: it is a HARDWOOD (broad-leaved tree) but feels SOFT. Its main property is being extremely LIGHT (low density). Uses: model making, insulation, packaging. NOT for structural or outdoor use.
Ash
Hardwood · European
A strong, tough, flexible hardwood with a pale brown colour and open grain. It is one of the best timbers for absorbing shock and can be bent when steamed, making it ideal for sports equipment and tool handles.
"Cricket bats and hockey sticks are made from ash because the timber is tough enough to absorb the repeated shock of impact without cracking."
💡 Ash key facts: strong, tough, FLEXIBLE (can absorb shock), open grain. Uses: tool handles, sports equipment (cricket bats, hockey sticks), ladders. Note: ash trees are threatened by ash dieback disease in Britain.
Softwood
Softwoods
Timber from a coniferous (evergreen) tree — a tree with needle-like leaves and seeds in a cone. Softwood trees grow quickly (30 years) and can be commercially farmed, making softwood much cheaper than hardwood.
"Most construction timber is softwood — pine and larch are used for roof structures and floor joists because they are strong enough for the purpose and much cheaper than hardwood."
💡 Softwood = conifer = needle leaves + cones = grows fast = cheaper. Most forests planted for timber are softwood forests. Majority of timber used in construction is softwood. Larch is unusual — it is a softwood that DOES lose its leaves in winter.
Pine
Softwood · Alpine
The most widely used softwood — light, easy to work, and relatively cheap. It has a pale yellow colour with distinct knots. It is not naturally durable outdoors but can be treated for external use.
"Pine is used for internal joinery (skirting boards, door frames) and furniture because it is affordable and easy to cut, shape, and paint."
💡 Pine key facts: pale yellow, knotty, cheap, easy to work, NOT naturally weather-resistant (must be treated for outdoor use). Most common softwood. Origin: Alpine/cold climate forests. Uses: construction, furniture, floorboards.
Larch
Softwood · Alpine
An unusual softwood that loses its leaves in winter (unlike other softwoods). It is tough, durable and naturally resistant to water — making it suitable for outdoor use without chemical treatment. More expensive than most softwoods.
"Exterior cladding on contemporary buildings is often made from larch — it weathers to an attractive silvery grey over time and does not rot outdoors, making it a sustainable finish."
💡 Larch key exam point: it is the ONLY common softwood that naturally resists water and can be used UNTREATED outdoors. It fades to silvery grey — this is not damage, it is natural weathering. Uses: exterior cladding, small boats, yachts.
Chipboard
Manufactured Board
Made from wood chips and sawdust mixed with resin glue and compressed into flat sheets. It uses waste materials, making it cheap to produce. However, it has poor structural strength (especially when damp) and must usually be surface-coated.
"Flat-pack kitchen units are typically made from chipboard coated with a plastic laminate — the low cost keeps furniture affordable, though it will not survive prolonged exposure to moisture."
💡 Chipboard = waste wood + glue → cheap, flat sheets. Key weakness: weak when damp — edges absorb water and swell. Surface is rough so usually needs laminate or veneer. Used for: flat-pack furniture, kitchen worktops, flooring base.
Seasoning
Properties
The process of drying timber to reduce its moisture content to 10–18% before use. Freshly cut timber is about 85% water. Unseasoned timber shrinks, warps, and cracks as it dries. Two methods: air seasoning (slow, years) and kiln drying (fast, weeks).
"The furniture manufacturer specified kiln-dried seasoned oak for the dining table — unseasoned timber would warp as it dried in the heated interior of a home."
💡 Why season? Wet timber = weak, warps, cracks, and is hard to work. Dry timber = stable, strong, and can be finished well. Kiln drying is faster and dries to 10% (for interior use). Air drying takes years and only reaches ~18%.
Grain
Properties
The fibres that run the length of a tree trunk, giving timber its strength and creating the distinctive pattern you see on the surface. Timber is stronger along the grain than across it. Fine grain = small, even fibres. Open/coarse grain = large, visible vessels.
"Oak has a distinctive open-grain pattern that is highly valued for its aesthetic appearance — the large vessels create a clearly visible texture that looks attractive when varnished."
💡 Grain = fibres running lengthways. Strong WITH grain (along it), weaker ACROSS grain. Open grain (oak, ash) = large vessels visible. Close grain (birch, beech) = small, tight fibres, smooth surface. Affects: strength, finish, and cutting direction.
Tensile Strength
Properties
The ability of a material to resist being pulled apart or stretched. Timber has moderate tensile strength along the grain but poor tensile strength across the grain. Ash and oak have particularly high tensile strength.
"The tensile strength of timber is 3–4 times greater than its compressive strength — this is why timber beams are excellent for spanning distances where bending forces create tension in the lower surface."
💡 Tensile = pulling/stretching force. Timber is stronger in TENSION than in COMPRESSION (3–4× more). This is why engineered timber beams (like I-beams) put most material at the top and bottom where bending creates maximum tension and compression.
Deforestation
Ecology
The large-scale cutting down of trees without replanting. A global problem — about 46,000–58,000 square miles of forest are lost each year (equivalent to 48 football fields every minute). Causes: soil erosion, habitat loss, increased CO₂, climate change.
"Deforestation in Nepal has caused serious landslide problems — removing trees means that rainwater can no longer be absorbed by roots, so it rushes down hillsides taking soil with it."
💡 De- = removal of. Deforestation = removal of forests. Consequences: habitat loss, soil erosion, more CO₂ in atmosphere (trees absorb CO₂ when living), climate change, loss of indigenous communities' homes. Solution: FSC-certified sustainable forestry.
FSC Certification
Ecology
The Forest Stewardship Council logo on timber products certifies that the wood comes from sustainably managed forests — where trees are replanted as they are cut down and the ecosystem is protected. Allows consumers to make ethical choices.
"By specifying FSC-certified oak, the architect ensured that the timber came from forests where replanting keeps the supply sustainable and habitat destruction is minimised."
💡 FSC = Forest Stewardship Council. Look for their green tree logo. It means: for every tree cut down, another is planted. Sustainable forestry = timber supply continues indefinitely without damaging ecosystems. Key exam use: always mention FSC when evaluating timber selection.
Built-in Obsolescence
Ecology
Deliberately designing products with a short lifespan so consumers must buy replacements sooner. In timber products, this is achieved by using cheap chipboard instead of solid hardwood — chipboard furniture fails faster, generating more waste and more sales.
"Flat-pack furniture made from chipboard exemplifies built-in obsolescence — it will delaminate or break within a few years, encouraging replacement, unlike solid oak furniture that can last generations."
💡 Obsolescence = becoming out of date or worn out. Built-IN = the manufacturer put it there on purpose. Environmentally harmful: creates more waste, uses more resources. Opposite: heirloom design (built to last).

🪵 Manufactured Boards (7.2.3)

Spec ref: 7.2.3 | Manufactured boards use timber in an engineered form to overcome the limitations of natural timber — limited width, directional strength, and knots.

Natural timber is only available in the width of the tree trunk, and its grain only runs in one direction (so it is weaker across the grain). Manufactured boards solve both problems — they come in large sheets and are strong in all directions.

⚙️ Physical Characteristics & Working Properties (7.2.5–7.2.6)

Spec ref: 7.2.5 and 7.2.6 | You must know what each property means AND be able to compare timbers using these properties.

Physical Characteristics of Timber

⚫

Knots

Where a branch grew out of the trunk. Swirling grain around a knot makes it harder to cut and plane. Knots can fall out of unseasoned timber, leaving holes. Knotty timber can be aesthetically appealing for rustic designs but is weaker at knot points.

🎨

Colour

Ranges from pale cream (balsa, pine) to rich dark brown (mahogany, walnut). Colour can vary even within a single species, and between the heartwood (darker centre) and sapwood (paler outer). Staining can change colour but cannot easily change texture.

〰️

Grain Structure

The fibres running lengthways through the trunk. Open grain (oak, ash): large vessels visible on the surface, coarser texture. Close grain (beech, birch): small tight fibres, smoother surface. Grain direction affects strength and how the surface is finished.

⚖️

Density

The weight per unit volume (g/cm³ or kg/m³). Balsa = 60 kg/m³ (very light). Oak = 750 kg/m³ (heavy). Higher density generally means harder, stronger timber. Wood has a high strength-to-weight ratio compared to metals because of its low density.

⚖️ Selection of Timber — 7.3 All Factors

Spec ref: 7.3.1–7.3.6 | When asked why a particular timber is chosen for a product, you must consider multiple factors. Exam questions often ask you to justify a timber choice using two or more selection criteria.

💡 Exam technique: Selection questions often give you a product context (e.g. "a children's outdoor bench") and ask you to choose and justify a timber. Always: (1) name the timber, (2) state the relevant property, (3) explain WHY that property is needed for that specific product. Never just say "it is strong" — say "it is durable and resistant to rot, which means it can be left outdoors without maintenance."
🎨 Aesthetic Factors
  • Form — the overall shape and visual structure. Timber's natural organic form is attractive for many products
  • Colour — ranges from pale pine to rich dark mahogany. Can be stained to change colour
  • Texture — grain patterns vary widely; some are valued for their visual appeal (oak, walnut)
🌿 Environmental Factors
  • Sustainability — use FSC-certified timber; prefer softwoods (faster growing)
  • Genetic engineering — allows faster-growing or disease-resistant trees (controversial)
  • Seasoning — must be correctly dried or it will warp in use
  • Upcycling — using old timber gives it a new purpose; more sustainable than new material
📦 Availability Factors
  • Stock sizes — designing to standard stock sizes saves waste and cost (e.g. 2440 × 1220 mm sheets)
  • Specialist materials — marine plywood, fire-retardant treated timber, tanalised (pressure-treated) timber
  • Hurricanes, storms, disease — can reduce availability and increase price suddenly (ash dieback disease)
💷 Cost Factors
  • Quality grades — constructional carcassing (hidden, for structure) is cheaper; joinery grade (visible, for appearance) is more expensive
  • Manufacturing scale — mass production uses standard stock sizes and cuts per-unit cost significantly
  • Treatments — fire-proofing and tanalising (Tanalith E) add cost but extend product life outdoors
👥 Social Factors
  • Social groups — bespoke hardwood furniture appeals to a wealthier market; chipboard flatpack appeals to the mass market
  • Trends and fashion — currently sustainable timber and reclaimed wood are fashionable for home interiors
  • Popularity — softwood from sustainable sources is increasingly used as concerns about tropical hardwoods grow
🤝 Cultural & Ethical Factors
  • Avoiding offence — symbols, colours or patterns carved into timber may have different meanings in different cultures
  • Consumer society — chipboard furniture designed to be replaced within a few years rather than lasting a lifetime
  • Mass production effects — automated sawmills reduce low-skilled jobs
  • Built-in obsolescence — using cheap chipboard instead of solid hardwood means products fail sooner

Activity 3 — True or False?

Test understanding
📝 Read each statement carefully. Several test common misconceptions that frequently appear in exam answers. Read the explanation after each question carefully.
1. Balsa is not a hardwood — it is classified as a softwood because it is very soft and easy to cut.
❌ False — this is one of the most common exam mistakes! Balsa IS a hardwood. The classification "hardwood" refers to the TYPE of tree it comes from (a deciduous, broad-leaved tree), NOT the hardness of the wood. Balsa comes from a broad-leaved tropical tree and is therefore classified as a hardwood — despite being the softest commercial timber available.
2. Larch is the only common softwood that can be used outdoors without chemical treatment because it is naturally tough, durable and water-resistant.
✅ True. Larch is unusual among softwoods — most softwoods like pine will rot if left outdoors untreated, but larch has a high natural oil content that makes it resistant to water and decay. It is used for exterior cladding on buildings and small boats. It also naturally weathers to an attractive silvery grey colour when left untreated outdoors — this is normal and not a sign of deterioration.
3. Plywood is stronger when cut to an even number of layers (e.g. 4 or 6 layers) because an even number creates perfect balance between the two surfaces.
❌ False. Plywood is always made with an ODD number of veneer layers (3, 5, 7...). An odd number ensures that the top and bottom surfaces always have their grain running in the SAME direction, which balances the internal stresses and prevents the board from warping. An even number would create unbalanced stresses between the two surfaces, causing the board to bow.
4. FSC certification guarantees that a timber product was made from tropical hardwood forests that are being responsibly replanted and managed.
❌ False — but only because of the word "tropical". FSC certification applies to ALL types of forests — not just tropical ones. It guarantees that the timber (whether softwood, European hardwood, or tropical hardwood) comes from a forest where trees are replanted as they are cut, biodiversity is protected, and the rights of local communities are respected. The certification is about sustainable management of any forest, not specifically tropical forests.
5. Seasoned timber is weaker than freshly cut "green" timber because drying removes the moisture that gives the wood its flexibility.
❌ False — this is the opposite of the truth! Seasoned (dried) timber is STRONGER, more stable, and easier to work than freshly cut green timber. Freshly cut timber contains up to 85% water. As this moisture evaporates after cutting, unseasoned timber shrinks, warps, twists, and cracks — making it unsuitable for furniture or joinery. Properly seasoned timber (10–18% moisture) is more dimensionally stable, stronger, and will not continue to move after the product is made.
6. MDF is preferable to chipboard for outdoor garden furniture because its smooth, uniform surface accepts paint and varnish better.
❌ False. Neither MDF nor chipboard is suitable for outdoor furniture. Both are manufactured boards that absorb moisture readily and will swell, delaminate, and lose their structural integrity when exposed to weather. MDF is particularly bad outdoors — it absorbs water rapidly at its edges and swells badly. For outdoor furniture, natural timbers with good durability (larch, oak, cedar) or treated softwood (tanalised pine) should be specified instead.

✍️ Sentence Frames for Timber Selection Questions

Justifying a timber choice (2–3 marks)
[Timber name] would be the most suitable choice because it has [specific property]. This is important for this product because [why that property is needed for this product]. Furthermore, [second property/reason].
"Oak would be the most suitable choice because it has excellent hardness and natural durability. This is important for a kitchen worktop because it will be subject to daily cutting, scratching, and exposure to moisture — properties that oak can withstand without deteriorating. Furthermore, oak has an attractive grain pattern that enhances the visual appeal of a kitchen."
Explaining an environmental consideration (2 marks)
When selecting [timber] for this product, an important environmental consideration is [issue]. To minimise this impact, the designer should [action].
"When selecting mahogany for this product, an important environmental consideration is deforestation — mahogany is a tropical hardwood and unsustainable logging has destroyed large areas of rainforest. To minimise this impact, the designer should specify FSC-certified mahogany from a sustainably managed source, or consider using an alternative European hardwood such as oak instead."
Comparing manufactured board vs solid timber (4 marks)
While [solid timber] would provide [advantage], using [manufactured board] would be more appropriate for this product because [reason]. However, a disadvantage of [manufactured board] is that [disadvantage].
"While solid pine would provide a natural aesthetic and good strength, using MDF would be more appropriate for this painted speaker cabinet because it has no grain direction, allowing it to be machined to precise dimensions, and its smooth surface accepts paint without needing a grain filler. However, a disadvantage of MDF is that it is heavy and will swell badly if exposed to moisture at its edges, making it unsuitable if the product will ever be used in damp environments."

Q1 · Timber Properties · 1 mark1 mark
Give one type of timber you would use to make a table top for a children's nursery.
💡 Think about what a nursery table needs: hard wearing for scratches, safe (no toxic finishes), attractive, affordable. One named timber is enough — 1 mark.
1
Beech. (Also acceptable: pine — with treatment; MDF — with non-toxic painted surface)
✏️ For 1 mark, just the timber name is enough. But if this were 2 marks, you would also need: "because it is hard, smooth-grained and takes a clean painted or varnished surface that is resistant to the scratching and knocks that a children's nursery table receives."
Q2 · Ecological Footprint · 4 marks4 marks
Explain two negative impacts of deforestation on the environment.
💡 Language hint: Two marks = two separate, developed points. Each point needs: what happens + why it is harmful. Use: "When forests are cleared... this causes... because..."
1
Habitat destruction: When large areas of forest are cleared, the animals that live there lose their homes and food sources. Animals such as tigers, gorillas, and orangutans are forced into smaller areas, reducing biodiversity and putting many species at risk of extinction.
2
Increased atmospheric CO₂: Living trees absorb carbon dioxide from the atmosphere. When forests are cut down and burned, the stored carbon is released as CO₂, contributing to the greenhouse effect and accelerating climate change. Global deforestation releases approximately 4.8 billion tonnes of CO₂ per year.
✏️ Also acceptable for a third point: soil erosion (tree roots hold soil together; without them, topsoil washes away, causing landslides and reducing land fertility for communities).
Q3 · Timber Selection · 6 marks6 marks
Evaluate the use of chipboard compared to solid oak for making a kitchen cabinet. Consider cost, durability, environmental impact, and aesthetics in your answer.
💡 Language hint: For 6 marks, give at least one advantage AND one disadvantage for each material, covering at least 3 of the 4 criteria listed. End with an overall recommendation. Use: "However... Furthermore... Overall..."
1
Cost: Chipboard is significantly cheaper than solid oak because it uses waste timber chips and can be mass-produced efficiently. This makes it the dominant material for budget kitchen cabinets and flat-pack furniture. However, solid oak cabinets represent a long-term investment — they will last for decades, while chipboard cabinets may need replacing within 5–10 years, particularly if exposed to steam or moisture in a kitchen.
2
Durability: This is chipboard's major weakness in a kitchen environment. The edges of chipboard absorb moisture rapidly, causing swelling and delamination — particularly around dishwashers, sinks, and steam. Solid oak is naturally moisture-resistant (when properly finished) and will withstand the humid kitchen environment for many years without structural failure.
3
Environmental impact: Paradoxically, chipboard can be more environmentally responsible — it uses waste wood chips that would otherwise be discarded. Solid oak requires the harvesting of slow-growing trees. However, chipboard cabinets fail sooner and go to landfill more quickly, while a well-maintained oak kitchen could last a lifetime.
4
Aesthetics: Solid oak has a beautiful natural grain that cannot be replicated by chipboard. However, chipboard with a realistic wood veneer or laminate surface is visually acceptable for most consumers and allows a wider range of colours and finishes. Solid oak is considered a premium aesthetic choice associated with quality craftsmanship.
Overall: For a budget kitchen, chipboard with a moisture-resistant laminate (melamine-faced chipboard) is a practical choice if properly sealed. For a premium kitchen designed to last 20+ years, solid oak is a more appropriate choice despite the higher initial cost — the long lifespan and natural beauty justify the investment, and the lower environmental impact over a longer product life makes it the more sustainable long-term option.
