🧱 Lesson 1: Material Properties

📌 ESL Strategy — Vocabulary Notebook: As you read the property words below, write each one in your notebook with: (1) the English word, (2) the Chinese translation, (3) one example product. E.g. Tough → 坚韧 → oak floorboards.
📝 The Property + Justify Sentence Frame

Most material property questions are worth 1 mark (name a property) or 2 marks (name it AND justify it). Learn both frames.

1-mark answer — just name the property

Property name
✅ Example: Tough  |  Flexible  |  Corrosion resistant

2-mark answer — property + justification (why does it help?)

material is property, therefore it what this means for the product.
✅ Example: Oak is tough, therefore it can withstand the impact of furniture being placed and moved on top of it without becoming damaged.
💡 Exam tip: The word "therefore" is your best friend. It forces you to link the property to a real function. If your answer does not say "therefore" (or "which means that" / "so that"), you may only get 1 mark even for a 2-mark question.
📖 Key Property Vocabulary

Learn these words. Group them by meaning to help you remember.

Mechanical Properties (how a material behaves under force)

Tough
Can absorb impact/shock without breaking. Resists sudden forces.
Hard
Resists scratching, denting, and abrasion on the surface.
Strong
Resists breaking when a force is applied (tension, compression, torsion).
Flexible / Malleable
Can bend or be shaped without breaking. Springs back (elastic) or stays bent (plastic/malleable).
Stiff / Rigid
Resists bending or deforming. Does NOT flex.
Durable / Hardwearing
Lasts a long time under normal use. Resists wear.

Thermal, Electrical & Chemical Properties

Thermal insulator
Does NOT conduct heat. Keeps things warm. ⚠️ Must say of heat — "insulator" alone is not accepted.
Electrical insulator
Does NOT conduct electricity. Prevents electric shocks.
Corrosion resistant
Will not rust or oxidise. Stays strong in wet/humid conditions.
Waterproof / Water resistant
Does not absorb water. Cannot be soaked through.
Non-toxic / Food safe
Safe for contact with food. Does not leach harmful chemicals.
Good fluidity / Casts well
Melts and flows easily into a mould when liquid.

Aesthetic & Other Properties

Foldable / Easily creased
Can be folded and holds its fold shape. Used for papers/boards.
Biodegradable
Will break down naturally. Better for the environment at end of life.
Lightweight / Low density
Does not weigh much. Easy to carry/move. ⚠️ Say "lightweight" not just "light".
Impact resistant
Can absorb sudden blows without cracking or shattering.

🔢 Lesson 2: D&T Maths Skills

📌 ESL Strategy — Read, Cover, Write, Check: For each worked example, read it slowly. Then cover it with paper and try to write the steps from memory. Uncover and check. Do this three times for each calculation type.
📐 Calculation Type 1 — Cost per Unit Area

These questions give you a total price and a total area, and ask you to find the cost per m² (or cost per cm²).

Cost per m² = Total Cost (£) ÷ Total Area (m²)
1
Identify the total cost (£) and total area (m² or cm²). Write them down.
2
Divide: Total Cost ÷ Total Area
3
Write your answer with the correct unit (£ per m²) and show your working.

WORKED EXAMPLE

A pack of pine floorboards costs £43.20 and covers an area of 1.20 m². Calculate the cost per m².

Step 1 — Write the formula:Cost per m² = Total Cost ÷ Total Area
Step 2 — Substitute values:= £43.20 ÷ 1.20
Step 3 — Calculate:= £36.00
Answer: £36.00 per m²

Practice Questions — try these yourself

(2 marks)
A sheet of beech veneer costs £27.00 and covers 0.9 m². Calculate the cost per m².
1✓
Working: £27.00 ÷ 0.9 = 30
1✓
Answer: £30.00 per m²
(2 marks)
A sheet of MDF costs £18.00 and has an area of 2.4 m². Calculate the cost per m².
1✓
Working: £18.00 ÷ 2.4 = 7.5
1✓
Answer: £7.50 per m²
♻️ Calculation Type 2 — Percentage Waste

These questions give you a starting material size and a used size. You must calculate the percentage that is wasted.

% Waste = (Waste Area ÷ Total Area) × 100
1
Calculate the total area of the starting material (length × width).
2
Calculate the useful area (the part you actually use).
3
Calculate the waste area: Total Area − Useful Area
4
Divide and multiply: (Waste ÷ Total) × 100. Round as instructed.

WORKED EXAMPLE

A rectangle of cardboard measures 30 cm × 20 cm. A circle with a diameter of 20 cm is cut from it. Calculate the percentage of waste cardboard. Give your answer to the nearest whole number.

Step 1 — Total area of rectangle:30 × 20 = 600 cm²
Step 2 — Area of circle (useful):π × 10² = 314.16 cm²
Step 3 — Waste area:600 − 314.16 = 285.84 cm²
Step 4 — Percentage:(285.84 ÷ 600) × 100 = 47.64%
Answer: 48% (rounded to nearest whole number)
💡 Common mistake: Students sometimes divide the useful area by the total, and call that the "waste." Always subtract first to find the actual waste, then divide by the total.

📋 Calculation Type 3 — Material Costing from a Parts Table

These questions give you a table of parts (with dimensions in mm), and a price per metre. You must calculate the total cost of all the material needed.

1
Convert mm to metres: divide length by 1000. This is the most common place for errors!
2
Multiply each part's length (in m) by the number required to get total length of that section.
3
Multiply total length by price per metre.
4
Repeat for each material type, then add all costs together.
Cost = (Length in mm ÷ 1000) × Number required × £ per metre

WORKED EXAMPLE

Pine (50×30) costs £3.50/m. Pine (80×15) costs £2.20/m.

PartQtyLength (mm)Section
A215050×30
B130050×30
C220080×15
Parts A+B (50×30): total length in metres(2×150 + 1×300) ÷ 1000 = 600 ÷ 1000 = 0.6 m
Cost of Parts A+B:0.6 × £3.50 = £2.10
Part C (80×15): total length in metres(2×200) ÷ 1000 = 400 ÷ 1000 = 0.4 m
Cost of Part C:0.4 × £2.20 = £0.88
Total cost:£2.10 + £0.88 = £2.98
Answer: £2.98
⚠️ Unit conversion warning: Dimensions in parts tables are ALWAYS in mm. You MUST divide by 1000 to get metres before multiplying by the £/metre price. Forgetting this gives an answer 1000× too large.

🔩 Methods of Assembly (Timber Products)

The exam often asks for semi-permanent methods specifically. Learn the difference between all three categories.

CategoryWhat it meansExamples
PermanentCannot be separated without damagePVA glue, nails, welding
Semi-permanentCan be taken apart, but requires a tool / deliberate actionScrews, dowels, KD fittings (knock-down), cam locks, barrel nuts & bolts
TemporaryCan be easily attached and detached without toolsHinges (with pin), magnetic catches, velcro

Key Semi-Permanent Methods — 2-mark answers

Screws
Inserted through one piece into another using a drill. Can be removed and re-inserted. Provides a very strong joint that can be dismantled.
Wooden Dowels
Round wooden pegs glued into drilled holes in both parts. Aligns pieces accurately. Common in flat-pack furniture.
KD (Knock-Down) Fittings
Plastic/metal blocks screwed into each piece. Bolts join the blocks. Fast to assemble/disassemble. Used in flat-pack.
Cam Lock (Barrel & Bolt)
A bolt screws into a cylindrical barrel. Rotating the cam lock pulls the pieces together. Quick and easy. Common in IKEA furniture.
📌 Sentence frame for assembly method answers:
"[Method name] — The [part A] and [part B] can be taken apart when the [screw is undone / cam is rotated / bolt is removed], because [explain the mechanism]. This means the product can be [reason why this is useful — e.g. flat-packed for transport / repaired if broken / disassembled at end of life for recycling]."

🌍 Ecological Footprint & Sustainability

These questions ask about the environmental impact of using timber, especially tropical hardwoods like mahogany.

Why is mahogany problematic? (3-mark answer structure)

The demand for mahogany results in problem 1 (1), which results in consequence 1 (1), therefore leading to final impact (1).
✅ Example: "The demand for mahogany results in large-scale deforestation in tropical rainforests, which results in loss of natural habitat for many animal species, therefore leading to a decline in biodiversity and potential extinction of species."
IssueCauseConsequence
DeforestationTrees cut down faster than they can grow backLoss of habitat, soil erosion, flooding, loss of biodiversity
Carbon releaseTrees store CO₂; cutting releases itIncreased greenhouse gases → climate change
Transport footprintTropical hardwood shipped thousands of km to UKLarge CO₂ emissions from shipping / fuel burning
Indigenous communitiesForests are home to local peopleDisplacement, loss of livelihood

Sustainable alternatives — exam credit points

  • FSC-certified timber (Forest Stewardship Council) — guarantees replanting
  • Using manufactured boards (MDF, plywood) — made from recycled wood waste
  • Using local hardwoods (e.g. European ash or oak) — shorter transport distance
  • Recycled / reclaimed timber — no new trees felled
🪵 The Veneering Process (notes + sketches question)

This is often a 4-mark "notes and sketches" question. You must include BOTH notes AND a sketch — max 3 marks for notes only or sketches only.

1
Prepare the surface: Sand/rub down the MDF. Remove all dust.
2
Apply adhesive: Brush PVA glue onto the MDF surface OR peel self-adhesive backing from veneer.
3
Position the veneer: Lay the veneer flat onto the surface. Align carefully.
4
Apply pressure: Use a bag press, bench press, or clamps (and iron if heat-activated). Apply even pressure across the whole surface.
5
Leave to dry: Leave clamped until the adhesive is fully cured/dry.
6
Trim edges: Once dry, trim the overhanging veneer with a craft knife or plane.

✍️ Extended Writing Skills

📌 ESL Strategy — Model Then Modify: Read the model answer carefully. Then close it and write your own version about a DIFFERENT product. Do not copy — use the structure only. This practises the thinking, not just the writing.
📋 Command Words — What the Examiner Wants
State / Give / Name
1 mark · no explanation needed
Just write the answer. One word or short phrase. Do NOT waste time explaining.
Describe
1–2 marks · say what it looks like / how it works
Explain what something IS or what it DOES. No need to evaluate.
Explain
2 marks · property + why/therefore
Name a point AND give a reason. Use "therefore" / "which means that" / "because".
Discuss
4–6 marks · make points + link reasoning
Give multiple linked points with reasons. Show both sides or different angles. Use connectives.
Evaluate / Analyse
6–9 marks · reasons + judgement + conclusion
Make detailed points AND weigh them up (what are the trade-offs?). Give a supported conclusion.
Calculate
2–5 marks · SHOW all working
Always write the formula first. Show each step. Write units in the answer. Use ECF — never leave blank.
📝 Answer Sentence Frames by Mark Level

2-mark "Explain one..." answer

Material/feature is property/characteristic (✓1), therefore specific benefit for this product/context (✓2).
✅ "MDF has no grain (✓1), therefore it does not split or give rise to splinters when the shelf slots are machined with a router (✓2)."

4-mark "Explain two..." answer

Reason 1 is point (✓1), therefore justification (✓2).
Additionally, Reason 2 is point (✓3), which means that justification (✓4).
✅ "Pine stock materials are readily available (✓1), therefore the manufacturer does not need to hold large volumes of different-sized materials in stock, reducing storage costs (✓2). Additionally, using standard stock sizes means that machines do not need to be reset each time (✓3), which speeds up the manufacturing process and reduces the chance of errors (✓4)."

6-mark "Discuss why..." answer — use PEEL × 3

Point: make a clear point.
Evidence: what happens / what this allows.
Explain: why this is important or useful.
Link: connect back to the question.
✅ "When a designer takes a product apart (P), they can see each individual component in isolation (E), which allows them to measure parts, understand how they fit together, and identify potential improvements to the design (E). This is useful because it means the designer does not have to start from nothing — they can build on the work of others (L)." — write 3 PEELs for full marks.

9-mark "Analyse and evaluate..." answer — structure guide

Introduction: briefly introduce the product and the factors you will discuss.

Body paragraph 1: discuss first factor with linked reasoning.
Body paragraph 2: discuss second factor — can be a contrasting/related point.
Body paragraph 3: discuss third factor — try to be specific about costs, trade-offs, or context.

Conclusion: weigh up the factors — what is the overall judgement? What matters most?
🔗 Academic Connectives for Extended Writing
This means that... Therefore... As a result... However... Furthermore... In addition... In contrast... For example... Overall... This would mean that... Which means that... On the other hand...

⚠️ Common ESL Mistakes in D&T Exams
❌ What students often write✅ What you should writeWhy this matters
"It is an insulator.""It is a good thermal insulator of heat." / "It is an electrical insulator."Examiner needs to know WHAT type of insulation. "Insulator" alone = 0 marks.
"It is durable.""It is durable / hardwearing, therefore it will last for many years under normal use.""Durable" alone is often not accepted unless linked to a benefit.
"It is light.""It is lightweight / has a low density.""Light" is ambiguous (could mean light in colour). Be specific.
"Wood is good for the environment because it is natural.""Certified timber (FSC) is sustainable because it comes from managed forests where trees are replanted after felling."Vague answers like "it's natural" score 0. Be specific about WHY.
"The varnish makes it look better.""The varnish provides a tough, waterproof surface finish, which means the timber is protected from moisture absorption and the natural grain is enhanced."2-mark questions need a point AND a reason. Beauty alone is incomplete.
Not showing working in maths questionsAlways write formula → substitute → calculate, even if you use a calculatorYou can get method marks (ECF) even if your final answer is wrong, but ONLY if working is shown.
