Key Vocabulary

Learn the meaning of each word, read the example sentence, and use the memory tip to help you remember it. Filter by topic area using the buttons below.

📌 ESL Tip: For each word, practise saying it out loud, then cover the definition and test yourself. Try to use the word in your own sentence. The orange highlighted words in examples are the key vocabulary in context.
Filter:
Unemployment
Industry
Simple Definition
When people do not have a job. In technology, this happens when machines replace workers.
"New robotic machines increased unemployment in the car factory because robots replaced 200 workers."
💡 un- = not. Unemployed = not employed (not working).
Workforce Skill Set
Industry
Simple Definition
All the skills and abilities that workers need to do their job well. Technology creates a need for new skills like coding and data analysis.
"Companies invest in training to improve their employees' workforce skill set, so workers can use the latest technology."
💡 A skill is something you can do well. A set is a group. So a skill set = a group of skills.
Demographic Movement
Industry
Simple Definition
When groups of people move from one place to another, often because technology creates jobs in certain areas. This changes the population.
"Technology companies in Silicon Valley caused demographic movement as thousands of workers moved there to find jobs."
💡 Demo- = people (like "democracy"). Demographic = about population/people groups.
Science & Technology Parks
Industry
Simple Definition
Special areas where technology companies and universities work together to develop new ideas. They help the local economy grow.
"Stanford University's science and technology park helped turn Silicon Valley into a global centre for innovation."
💡 Like a shopping park (many shops together), a science park = many technology companies together in one place.
Enterprise
Enterprise
Simple Definition
A business, especially one that a person starts by taking a risk with their own money or ideas. It requires initiative — taking action without being told.
"Starting a new enterprise using 3D printing technology was a big risk, but it became very successful."
💡 Entrepreneurship = the skill of running an enterprise. An entrepreneur = a person who starts a business.
Crowd Funding
Enterprise
Simple Definition
A way to raise money for a new product or business by asking many people (a "crowd") to each give a small amount of money online.
"The designer used crowd funding on Kickstarter to raise £50,000 for her new smart watch design."
💡 Crowd = a large group of people. Funding = money. Crowd + funding = money from many people.
Not-for-Profit Organisation
Enterprise
Simple Definition
An organisation that uses all its money to support a cause (like health or education), not to make profit for the owners. Also called a charity or NGO.
"Charity: Water is a not-for-profit organisation that uses remote sensors to manage water projects globally."
💡 Profit = money a company keeps after paying costs. Not-for-profit = money goes back into the cause, not the owners' pockets.
Sustainability
Sustainability
Simple Definition
Using resources carefully today so that future generations can also use them. A sustainable design does not damage the environment or use up all natural resources.
"The company's sustainable packaging uses recycled cardboard so it does not harm the environment."
💡 Sustain = to keep going. Sustainable = able to keep going into the future without running out.
Pollution
Sustainability
Simple Definition
Harmful substances that are released into the environment (air, water, land) during manufacturing, transport, or using a product. It damages nature and health.
"The factory was fined because its pollution — toxic chemicals in the river — killed fish and endangered wildlife."
💡 Pollute = the verb (to dirty the environment). Pollution = the noun (the dirty substance).
Natural Resources
Sustainability
Simple Definition
Materials found in nature that humans use, such as wood, oil, coal, copper, and iron ore. Many are finite — they will run out one day.
"Coal and oil are finite natural resources — once we use them all, they cannot be replaced."
💡 Natural = from nature (not made by humans). Resources = things we can use. Key word: finite = limited / will run out.
The 4 Rs of Waste
Sustainability
Simple Definition
Reduce — make less waste. Reuse — use the same material again. Recycle — turn waste into new products. Recover — get energy from waste (e.g. heat from burning).
"The factory used the 4 Rs: it reduced off-cuts, reused packaging, recycled metals, and recovered heat energy."
💡 The order matters! Reduce is best (make less waste). Recover is last (least sustainable).
Automation
People
Simple Definition
Using machines, computers, or robots to do tasks that humans used to do — automatically, without human effort. This can increase efficiency but reduces jobs.
"Automation in car manufacturing means robots now weld car bodies, a task that once required hundreds of workers."
💡 Auto = self/automatic (like automatic). Automation = systems that work by themselves.
Apprenticeship
People
Simple Definition
A way of learning a job by working in a company AND studying, usually at the same time. You earn money while training and gain an official qualification.
"She completed an apprenticeship at an engineering firm, gaining both work experience and a nationally recognised qualification."
💡 An apprentice = a person learning on the job. Compare with: intern (usually unpaid), student (studying only).
Assistive Technology
People
Simple Definition
Technology designed to help people with disabilities — from small tools like pencil grips to complex devices like prosthetic limbs or voice recognition software.
"Assistive technology such as screen readers and eye-tracking devices helps people with visual impairments use computers."
💡 Assist = to help. Assistive = helpful. This technology assists people who need extra support.
Internet of Things (IoT)
Society
Simple Definition
Everyday objects (fridges, alarms, cars) that are connected to the internet and can communicate with each other or be controlled remotely using a phone or computer.
"Thanks to the Internet of Things, her smart fridge automatically orders milk when it detects the supply is low."
💡 Not just computers — things (objects like fridges and cars) are connected to the internet. The key idea is: objects + internet = IoT.
Remote Working
Society
Simple Definition
Working from a location other than the main office — for example, from home, a café, or another country. Technology makes this possible.
"During the pandemic, millions of people switched to remote working, using video calls and cloud software to stay connected."
💡 Remote = far away (from the office). Remote working = working from far away. Synonym: working from home (WFH).
Culture
Society
Simple Definition
The way of life of a group of people — including their behaviour, traditions, language, food, religion, and art. Technology can change or support culture.
"New technology like social media has changed culture by making it easier for people in different countries to share ideas and connect."
💡 Culture is not just art or music. It includes everything about how a group of people live together.
Critical Evaluation
Evaluation
Simple Definition
Carefully examining something to identify both the positives (advantages) AND the negatives (disadvantages) — not just saying "it is good" or "it is bad".
"A critical evaluation of 3D printing technology considers both its ability to reduce waste AND its high initial cost."
💡 Critical here does NOT mean "negative". It means "careful and balanced". You must look at BOTH sides.
Ethics
Evaluation
Simple Definition
Deciding what is morally right and fair. In design and technology, this includes asking: Who made it? Were workers treated fairly? Who benefits?
"Ethical companies do not hire child labour or underpay workers in developing countries to reduce costs."
💡 Ethics = moral rules. Ethical = following moral rules (the adjective). Ask: Is this fair? Is this right?
Carbon Footprint
Evaluation
Simple Definition
The total amount of carbon dioxide (CO₂) produced — directly or indirectly — by a person, product, or company. A larger footprint = more damage to the environment.
"Shipping products by air increases a company's carbon footprint because aeroplanes burn large amounts of fossil fuels."
💡 Like a physical footprint shows where you walked, a carbon footprint shows the environmental "mark" you leave behind.
Life-Cycle Analysis (LCA)
Evaluation
Simple Definition
A detailed study of the environmental impact of a product at every stage of its life: from raw material extraction → manufacturing → transport → use → disposal.
"An LCA of a plastic bottle shows that most environmental damage happens during oil extraction, not when you throw it away."
💡 LCA = Life-Cycle Analysis. Think of a product's full "life story" from birth (raw materials) to death (disposal).
Global Warming
Evaluation
Simple Definition
The gradual increase in Earth's average temperature, mainly caused by greenhouse gases (like CO₂) that trap heat in the atmosphere. Technology can cause OR reduce global warming.
"Electric vehicles help reduce global warming because they produce less CO₂ than petrol cars during use."
💡 Global = worldwide. Warming = getting hotter. Caused by burning fossil fuels → CO₂ → greenhouse effect → temperature rises.
Just-in-Time (JIT)
Production
Simple Definition
A production method where materials are ordered and delivered only when they are needed — not stored beforehand. This reduces storage costs and waste.
"Toyota uses just-in-time production: car parts arrive at the factory the same day they are needed on the assembly line."
💡 "Just in time" = arriving exactly when needed, not too early and not too late. The opposite is bulk-buying and storing materials.
Lean Manufacturing
Production
Simple Definition
A production system that tries to eliminate waste at every stage — wasted time, wasted materials, and unnecessary steps in the process.
"By adopting lean manufacturing, the company reduced material waste by 30% and made production faster."
💡 Lean = thin (not fat = no waste). A lean body has no extra fat; lean manufacturing has no extra waste or unnecessary steps.
Mass Production
Production
Simple Definition
Making very large numbers of identical products using automated machines and assembly lines. This makes each product cheap to produce but allows little variety.
"Toy manufacturers use mass production to make millions of identical action figures at a very low cost per unit."
💡 Mass = a very large amount. Mass production = making a very large amount of identical products. Think: fast, cheap, identical.
One-Off Production
Production
Simple Definition
Making a single, unique product that is custom-made for one specific customer. It requires skilled workers and is expensive, but produces high-quality results.
"A handmade wedding dress is an example of one-off production — it is made only once, to fit one specific person."
💡 "One-off" is a common English phrase meaning: done only once, not repeated. One-off production = made once, for one person.

🏭 Industry — How technology changes work

Spec reference: 1.1.1 | Key question: How does new technology affect industry and employment?

📉
Unemployment
Robots and computers replace workers in factories and offices, especially in low-skilled, repetitive jobs. This causes job losses in some areas.
🎓
Workforce Skill Set
Workers need new digital skills to use modern technology. Companies must invest in training. Workers must keep learning throughout their careers.
✈️
Demographic Movement
Technology hubs attract workers, creating "brain drain" — younger, skilled people leave their home region/country to find better-paid tech jobs elsewhere.
🏢
Science & Tech Parks
Areas where businesses and universities collaborate. They boost local economies and transfer knowledge. E.g. Silicon Valley (USA), Cambridge Science Park (UK).
✓ Advantages of new technology in industry✗ Disadvantages of new technology in industry
Cuts manufacturing costs → cheaper products for consumersCreates unemployment in low-skilled jobs
Products reach the market fasterWorkers need expensive retraining programmes
Reduces human error → higher quality productsWealth becomes concentrated in tech areas → inequality
Robots can work in dangerous environments safelyDemographic movement causes social problems in communities that lose workers

💼 Enterprise — Starting & funding businesses

Spec reference: 1.1.2 | Key question: How can new technology businesses be funded?

👤
Privately-Owned Business
Owned by an individual or small group. Flexible and quick to adopt new technology, but may lack funds for expensive equipment.
💻
Crowd Funding
Raise money from thousands of small donors online (e.g. Kickstarter). Good for creative or tech products. Success depends on capturing public interest.
🏛️
Government Funding
Loans or grants from the government to help start-ups that will benefit the economy. Over 40,000 UK businesses have used start-up loans since 2012.
❤️
Not-for-Profit
Charities and NGOs that use technology for social good. All income is reinvested into their cause. E.g. Charity: Water uses sensor technology to manage water projects.
✓ Advantages of crowd funding✗ Disadvantages of crowd funding
No need to repay a bank loanProduct idea becomes public — competitors may copy it
Builds a community of early supporters/customersNo guarantee you will reach your funding target
Proves that there is market demand for the productRequires significant marketing effort to attract donors

🌱 Sustainability — Protecting the environment

Spec reference: 1.1.3 | Key question: How can companies reduce their environmental impact?

🚚
Transportation Costs
Moving products burns fossil fuels → CO₂ emissions. Solutions: local production, electric vehicles, lighter/compact packaging, efficient routes.
☁️
Pollution
Manufacturing releases harmful gases, chemicals, and waste. Companies use clean technology, carbon taxes, and renewable energy to reduce pollution.
⛏️
Natural Resources
Coal, oil, copper, and iron ore are finite — they will run out. Companies develop sustainable alternatives like biodegradable plastics.
♻️
The 4 Rs
Reduce waste produced → Reuse materials → Recycle into new products → Recover energy from waste. In that order of preference.

🔑 Key sustainability strategies companies use:

Reduce packaging
Design smaller, lighter packaging so more products fit per delivery vehicle → fewer journeys → less CO₂.
Use renewable energy
Switch from coal/gas (finite, polluting) to solar, wind, or hydroelectric power (renewable, clean).
Materials separation
Automated machines sort materials (metals, plastics, paper) so they can be recycled rather than sent to landfill.
Local production
Making products close to where they are sold reduces transport distances and transportation costs.

👥 People — How technology affects society

Spec reference: 1.1.4 | Key question: Who is affected by new technology, and how?

👔
Workforce
Technology means workers need to regularly update their skills throughout their careers. This creates demand for online learning and professional development.
🛒
Consumers
People are more aware of new technologies, increasing demand. Some technologies (3D printing) even allow consumers to design their own products.
👧
Children
Technology creates rich learning opportunities (e.g. educational apps, Minecraft) but raises concerns about screen time, social skills, and physical activity.
♿
People with Disabilities
Assistive technology (screen readers, voice recognition, prosthetic limbs using electronics) significantly improves independence and quality of life.
💰
Wage Levels
High-skilled tech workers earn more. However, automation reduces the number of low-skilled jobs → wage inequality may increase.
🤖
Automation
Repetitive, routine tasks are increasingly done by machines. This is efficient but displaces workers who do not have advanced technical skills.
✓ Benefits of technology for people✗ Problems caused by technology for people
Assists people with disabilities — increases independenceAutomation reduces low-skilled jobs → unemployment
Creates new high-skilled, well-paid jobsWorkers must constantly retrain — this is expensive and stressful
Children gain access to interactive, creative learning toolsChildren may spend too much time on screens → health concerns
Consumers can customise products (e.g. via 3D printing)Digital divide — those without access to technology are left behind

🌐 Society — Technology & how we live

Spec reference: 1.1 (Society & Culture) | Key question: How has technology changed the way society functions?

📱
Internet of Things (IoT)
Smart devices (fridges, thermostats, cars) connected to the internet. Benefits: automation, efficiency, remote control. Risks: security breaches, over-reliance on technology.
🏠
Remote Working
Technology allows employees to work from home. Reduces commuting time and costs, but can blur work-life balance and reduce face-to-face collaboration.
📹
Video Conferencing
Tools like Zoom allow meetings across the world for free. Reduces travel costs but lacks the benefits of physical meetings (body language, networking).
🏘️
Social Segregation
Technology can divide communities. Those without digital access or skills are excluded from opportunities in education, jobs, and services.
✓ Advantages of remote working✗ Disadvantages of remote working
Flexible work schedule — better work-life balanceLess workplace social interaction
Less time and money spent commutingBlurs the boundary between work and home life
Wider talent pool — hire workers from anywherePotential security breaches when working on public networks
Technological advances make more work possible remotelyLess IT support available → can reduce productivity

🔍 Evaluating New & Emerging Technologies

Spec reference: 1.2 | Key question: How do we critically evaluate whether to use a new technology?

When evaluating a new technology, always consider these 5 areas (BTMWM):

💷
Budget Constraints
How much does the new technology cost? Can the company afford it? Will it save money in the long term?
⏰
Timescale
How long will it take to develop or implement? Will it speed up or slow down production?
👤
Who the Product is For
Does the technology meet the needs of the target user? Has the target market been researched?
🧱
Materials Used
Are the materials sustainable? Have they been tested? Will the improvements justify the extra cost?
🏗️
Manufacturing Capabilities
Can current machines/equipment be used? Will automated assembly lines produce better quality products?

Environmental perspectives (LCA & Carbon Footprint):

👣
Carbon Footprint
Reduce CO₂ by: using renewable energy, recycling materials, reducing transportation, designing energy-efficient products.
🔄
Life-Cycle Analysis
Examine environmental impact at every stage: extraction → manufacture → transport → use → disposal. Required by governments in many industries.
🤝
Ethical Perspectives
Where was it made? Were workers paid fairly? Is it Fairtrade? Does it exploit workers or damage communities?
🌡️
Global Warming
Technology can cause more emissions OR provide solutions (solar power, electric vehicles, smart energy management).

⚙️ Production Techniques & Systems

Spec reference: 1.1 (Production) | Key question: What are the different ways products can be manufactured?

TechniqueWhat it meansExampleKey advantageKey disadvantage
Standardised Design The same components are used across many different products Standard screws, resistors, USB ports Consistent quality; easy to replace parts Difficult to customise; products look similar
Just-in-Time (JIT) Materials are delivered exactly when needed — no storage Car manufacturing assembly lines Reduces storage costs and waste Any delay in delivery stops the whole production line
Lean Manufacturing Eliminate all forms of waste (time, materials, movement) Eliminating overproduction, reducing defects Multi-skilled teams improve efficiency Requires time-consuming data analysis to implement

Exam Language & Practice

This section helps you understand what exam questions are asking you to do, and gives you sentence frames to structure your answers correctly.

📌 ESL Tip: In exams, the command word (e.g. "Explain", "Evaluate", "Describe") tells you WHAT to do. Always read it carefully. A common mistake is to "describe" when the question asks you to "explain" — these are very different!

Command Words — What do they mean?

Describe
Say what something is or what it looks like. Give features and characteristics. No explanation needed.
Usually 1–2 marks
Explain
Say what something is AND why it happens or how it works. You must give a reason using: because, therefore, this means that...
Usually 2–4 marks
Evaluate
Discuss the advantages AND disadvantages, then make a judgement. You must consider both sides.
Usually 6–9 marks
Analyse
Break something down into parts and examine each part carefully. Look at causes, effects, and connections.
Usually 4–6 marks
Discuss
Write about a topic from different points of view. Give arguments for AND against.
Usually 4–6 marks
State / Give
Write a short, direct answer. One or two words or a sentence may be enough.
Usually 1 mark

✍️ Sentence Frames for Exam Answers

Use these structures to write well-organised answers. The orange boxes show where you add your own content.

For EXPLAIN questions (2 marks)
One [advantage / disadvantage] of [topic] is that [main point]. This means that [consequence / explanation].
"One advantage of automation is that it reduces human error in manufacturing. This means that products are more consistent in quality, which reduces waste and customer returns."
For EXPLAIN questions — cause and effect (2 marks)
When [cause], this leads to [effect], because [reason].
"When robots replace factory workers, this leads to unemployment, because the machines can do the same tasks faster and more cheaply without needing wages or breaks."
For EVALUATE questions — advantage + disadvantage (4–6 marks)
One benefit of [technology/idea] is [advantage]. However, a disadvantage is that [disadvantage]. Overall, [judgement].
"One benefit of crowd funding is that it allows designers to raise money without taking out a bank loan. However, a disadvantage is that the product idea becomes public, which means competitors could copy it. Overall, crowd funding is most useful for creative or unique products that generate public interest."
For sustainability / environmental questions
One way [company/product] could reduce its environmental impact is by [action]. This would [positive outcome].
"One way a shampoo manufacturer could reduce its environmental impact is by using smaller, lighter packaging made from recycled cardboard. This would reduce transportation costs and CO₂ emissions because more bottles could fit in each delivery vehicle."
Connective phrases to link your ideas
  • Furthermore, ... adds another point in the same direction
  • However, ... introduces the opposite point / a disadvantage
  • Therefore, ... gives the result or consequence
  • As a result, ... gives the outcome of something
  • In contrast, ... compares two different things
  • This means that ... explains the significance of your point
  • For example, ... gives a specific example to support your point
  • Overall, ... gives your final judgement / conclusion

📝 Practice Exam Questions

📌 ESL Tip: Try to answer each question yourself FIRST before pressing "Show Model Answer". Use the sentence frames above to help structure your response.
Question 1 · Unemployment2 marks
Explain one reason why unemployment in the UK may rise as new and emerging technologies develop.
💡 Language hint: This question says "Explain" — you need to give ONE reason AND explain WHY it causes unemployment. Use: "...this is because..." / "...which means that..."
1
New technologies such as robotics and automation allow factories to replace human workers with machines that can perform tasks continuously without breaks or wages.
2
This leads to unemployment because workers in low-skilled, repetitive jobs are no longer needed, and they may not have the new skills required to operate the technology that replaced them.
✏️ Key words to include: automation, low-skilled, repetitive, replaced, workforce skill set
Question 2 · Crowd Funding2 marks
Explain one benefit of crowd funding that can help to promote new and emerging technologies.
💡 Language hint: State the benefit clearly, then explain the consequence: "One benefit is... This means that..."
1
One benefit of crowd funding is that it allows designers and entrepreneurs to raise money from a large number of people online without needing to borrow from a bank.
2
This means that new technology companies can access startup funding even if they do not have the financial history needed to secure a traditional bank loan, making it easier for innovative ideas to reach the market.
✏️ Key words to include: crowd funding, raise money, startup, innovative, online platforms
Question 3 · Transportation & Packaging4 marks
Explain two ways in which a shampoo manufacturer could change its packaging to reduce transportation costs.
💡 Language hint: Give TWO separate and different ways. Use the structure: "One way... Another way..." Remember: each way needs an explanation — how does it reduce transportation costs?
1
Way 1: Use lighter packaging materials such as thin recyclable plastic instead of heavy glass. This reduces the total weight per shipment, which means vehicles use less fuel, cutting both financial and environmental transportation costs.
2
Way 2: Redesign bottles to have a more compact, stackable shape. This means more bottles can fit inside each delivery vehicle per journey, reducing the number of trips required and therefore lowering costs and CO₂ emissions.
✏️ Key words: lightweight, compact packaging, CO₂ emissions, fewer journeys, transportation costs
Question 4 · LCA Steps4 marks
Explain the steps involved in carrying out a Life-Cycle Analysis (LCA).
💡 Language hint: LCA has 5 stages. Present them in order using: "First... Then... Next... After that... Finally..."
1
Stage 1 — Raw material extraction: Examine the environmental impact of extracting the raw materials needed (e.g. mining metals, cutting timber).
2
Stage 2 — Manufacturing: Consider the energy, water, and emissions used during the manufacture and assembly of the product.
3
Stage 3 — Transportation: Analyse the CO₂ produced by transporting the product from factory to retailer to consumer.
4
Stage 4 — Consumer use: Consider energy and resources used by the consumer while using the product.
Stage 5 — End of life / Disposal: Examine whether the product can be recycled, reused, or if it goes to landfill.
💡 An LCA identifies where most environmental damage occurs so companies can make targeted improvements.

🔗 Connectives for Exam Answers
  • This means that... Use to explain the consequence of your point
  • As a result,... Shows the effect / outcome
  • However,... Introduces the opposite view
  • Furthermore,... Adds another supporting point
  • In contrast,... Compares two different things
  • For example,... Gives a specific example
  • Although... Acknowledges a counter-argument
  • Therefore,... Gives your conclusion / judgement
  • On the other hand,... Shows a balanced evaluation
💬 Describing Advantages & Disadvantages

Avoid always writing "good" and "bad". Use these more academic alternatives:

✓ Advantages
One benefit of... / A key advantage is... / This results in...
This improves... / This enables... / This allows...
This is effective because... / This is beneficial as...
✗ Disadvantages
A drawback of... / One limitation is... / However,...
This can lead to... / This may cause... / This results in...
This is problematic because... / This is a concern as...
🚨 Common ESL Mistakes — and how to fix them
❌ Avoid this✅ Write this insteadWhy?
"Technology is very good""New technology increases efficiency and reduces manufacturing costs"Be specific — give D&T reasons
"It is bad for the environment""It increases CO₂ emissions, which contributes to global warming"Explain the cause AND effect
"Unemployment means no job""Automation leads to unemployment as machines replace workers in low-skilled roles"Use subject-specific language
"The company should use recycle""The company should recycle its waste materials"Recycle is a verb — use it correctly
"It has many advantages and disadvantages"Name the specific advantages and disadvantages!Avoid vague, empty sentences
"Critical evaluation means criticising""Critical evaluation means examining both advantages and disadvantages to make a balanced judgement"Critical ≠ negative in academic English
