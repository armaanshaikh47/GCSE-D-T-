Critical Evaluation
Evaluation
A process that identifies both the positives (advantages) AND the negatives (disadvantages) of a technology, material, or design decision — from a range of different perspectives. It is a balanced judgement, not a one-sided opinion.
"A critical evaluation of 3D printing for dental implants considers both the ability to create patient-specific shapes AND the high equipment cost and long testing time required."
💡 Critical in academic English does NOT mean "negative". It means "careful and balanced". You must always look at BOTH sides. A good evaluation never says something is purely good or purely bad.
Budget Constraints
Evaluation
The financial limits that affect design decisions — how much the customer is willing to pay, how new technology affects material selection, and whether cost savings are possible through the new technology.
"Budget constraints meant the designer had to choose injection moulding over 3D printing — despite the longer setup time, the lower cost per unit justified the choice."
💡 Budget = the money available. Constraint = a limit or restriction. Budget constraints = the money restrictions that limit what you can do. Key question to ask: How much are customers prepared to pay?
Timescale
Evaluation
The amount of time needed to develop, manufacture, or implement a new technology. This includes lead time for customers and whether staff need training before the technology can be used.
"The timescale for introducing robotic assembly was 18 months — 6 months for installation and 12 months of staff retraining before full production could begin."
💡 Time-scale = how long something takes, shown on a time scale. Key questions: Will production times be cut? Does staff need training (which takes time)? What lead time do customers expect?
Manufacturing Capabilities
Evaluation
Whether the existing equipment, skills, and processes in a factory can support a new technology. This includes whether automated assembly lines can produce the product and whether flexible production systems are possible.
"The factory's current manufacturing capabilities did not include CNC laser cutting, so the company had to invest in new machinery before the product could be made."
💡 Manufacture = to make products. Capability = ability or capacity. Ask: Will new technologies influence how products are made? Can existing equipment handle it?
Target Market
Evaluation
The specific group of people a product is designed for. Understanding the target market means researching their needs, habits, budget, and how the technology will improve their experience.
"Before committing to the new material, the designer researched the target market — elderly users who needed a lightweight product they could carry independently."
💡 Target = the aim or goal. Market = the group of potential buyers. Target market = the specific audience for a product. Always ask: Has the target market been researched?
Natural Disaster
Scenarios
Extreme weather events or geological events (floods, hurricanes, earthquakes) that are increasingly linked to human activity and climate change. Technology can help predict, prevent, and build resilience against them.
"Technology helps reduce the impact of natural disasters — seismic sensors predict earthquakes, satellite data tracks hurricanes, and new construction materials create earthquake-resistant buildings."
💡 Natural = from nature. Disaster = a catastrophic event. The key exam point: many natural disasters are now linked to human-caused climate change. Technology can both cause AND reduce them.
Global Warming
Scenarios
The gradual increase in Earth's average temperature, primarily caused by greenhouse gas emissions (especially CO₂) from burning fossil fuels. Solutions include low-carbon and zero-carbon technologies.
"Global warming is probably caused by greenhouse gas emissions. Technology offers solutions such as solar panels, electric vehicles, and smart energy management systems."
💡 Global = worldwide. Warming = getting hotter. Caused by: burning fossil fuels → CO₂ → greenhouse effect → temperature rises. Key word pair: low-carbon technology (the solution).
Medical Advances
Scenarios
New technologies in healthcare — including biotechnology, artificial organs, implants, prosthetic limbs, and medical imaging (MRI, CT scans) — that improve health outcomes but also raise questions about access, cost, and population impact.
"Medical advances such as 3D-printed prosthetic limbs improve patient independence, but the impact on the wider population (e.g. increased elderly population needing care) must be evaluated."
💡 Medical = relating to medicine/health. Advances = improvements. Key exam point: benefits must be evaluated AND the wider population impact considered (longer lives → more demand for elderly care).
Communication Technology
Scenarios
The internet and digital tools that make global communication cheap, instant, and widely available. However, designers must not assume all users have access to hardware, software, internet infrastructure, or reliable power.
"Although communication technology now allows global collaboration, the designer should not assume the target market in rural areas has reliable internet access or modern devices."
💡 Key exam warning: "Communication is now cheap and global" — TRUE. But designers CANNOT assume everyone has access. Always consider the digital divide when evaluating communication-based products.
Ethics
Ethics
The moral principles that guide decisions about what is fair, honest, and right. In design and technology, ethics means considering whether products are made fairly — not exploiting workers, suppliers, or damaging communities.
"Companies that trade ethically consider their products' wider implications — they do not exploit workers or suppliers, and they do not damage the environment."
💡 Ethics = moral rules about what is right. Ethical = following these rules (adjective). Unethical = breaking them. Key questions: Is this fair? Is this honest? Does this harm anyone?
Fairtrade
Ethics
An international certification system ensuring that producers in developing countries receive a fair price for their goods and work in safe, ethical conditions. Companies producing Fairtrade products follow specific environmental and social standards.
"By choosing Fairtrade certified cotton, the clothing company ensured that farmers in developing countries received fair wages, improving their livelihoods and living standards."
💡 Fair = just and equal. Trade = buying and selling. Fairtrade = a system that makes trade more equal between rich and poor countries. Look for the Fairtrade Mark on products.
Exploitation
Ethics
Using people unfairly for profit — such as paying very low wages, using child labour, or providing unsafe working conditions — especially in developing countries where regulations may be weaker.
"Manufacturing in countries with low wages can lead to exploitation — paying workers less than a living wage and ignoring health and safety standards to reduce costs."
💡 Exploit = to use unfairly for your own advantage. Exploitation = the act of exploiting someone. Related: child labour, sweatshops, unsafe factories, poverty wages.
Life-Cycle Analysis (LCA)
Environment
A systematic study of the environmental impact of a product at every stage of its life — from extracting raw materials through manufacturing, transportation, consumer use, and finally disposal or recovery. Increasingly required by governments.
"An LCA of a plastic bottle reveals that most environmental damage occurs during oil extraction, not during the consumer use stage — a finding that changes where the company focuses its sustainability efforts."
💡 LCA = Life-Cycle Analysis. Think of the complete "life story" of a product: birth (raw materials) → childhood (manufacturing) → adult life (use) → death (disposal). Each stage has environmental costs.
Carbon Footprint
Environment
The total amount of CO₂ and greenhouse gases produced — directly and indirectly — by a person, product, or organisation. A larger footprint = greater environmental damage. Companies reduce it by using renewable energy, reducing transport, and recycling.
"Air-freighting products across the world creates a huge carbon footprint. The company switched to sea freight and reduced its CO₂ emissions by 70%."
💡 A physical footprint shows where you walked. A carbon footprint shows the environmental "marks" you leave behind. Reduction strategies: renewable energy, local sourcing, efficient transport, recycled materials.
Sustainable Design
Environment
Designing products that use fewer materials, use recyclable or renewable materials, reduce energy consumption, and minimise environmental damage throughout their life cycle — so that future generations can meet their own needs.
"The company adopted sustainable design principles — using recycled aluminium, reducing packaging by 40%, and designing the product for easy disassembly at end of life."
💡 Sustain = to keep going. Sustainable = able to continue without running out or causing permanent harm. Three pillars: environmental, social, and economic sustainability.
Energy Consumption
Environment
The amount of energy used during manufacturing and transportation of a product. Fossil fuel energy harms the environment; renewable energy reduces the product's carbon footprint. No energy source is completely clean.
"Switching the factory's energy consumption from coal-fired electricity to wind power reduced the product's overall carbon footprint by 45% without changing the manufacturing process."
💡 Consume = to use up. Consumption = the act of using. Energy consumption = how much energy is used. Key exam point: even "clean" renewable energy has some environmental impact during manufacture of turbines/panels.
Materials Recycling
Environment
Converting waste materials back into usable raw materials, reducing the demand for new natural resources and the amount of waste sent to landfill. Designers should select fewer materials and ensure they are recyclable and lighter in weight.
"The designer specified recycled aluminium for the casing — it requires 95% less energy to produce from recycled material than from raw ore, significantly reducing the product's LCA."
💡 Recycle = convert waste into new material. Key design rule: use FEWER materials (easier to separate and recycle), make them LIGHTER (less transport energy), make them RECYCLABLE, and make them less TOXIC.
End of Life / Disposal
Environment
The final stage of a product's life — what happens when the consumer no longer needs it. Options include: landfill (worst), recycling (good), reuse (very good), energy recovery (intermediate). Designers should plan for this from the start.
"By designing the product for easy disassembly, the company ensured that 90% of its components could be separated and recycled at end of life, avoiding landfill."
💡 "End of life" = when the product is thrown away or finished with. The best designs are planned for end of life BEFORE they are made. Design for disassembly = making it easy to separate materials for recycling.

⚖️ The BTMWM Critical Evaluation Framework

Spec ref: 1.2.1 | Key question: What five criteria must a designer evaluate when considering any new technology?

When evaluating a new or emerging technology for use in a product, designers must consider five key areas. Use the acronym B · T · M · W · M to remember them all.

📸 Figure 1.2.1 — The five critical evaluation criteria (textbook p.11)

B
Budget Constraints
Key questions to ask:
• How much are customers prepared to pay?
• Will new technology reduce or increase material costs?
• Will it improve profit margins?
• Can cost savings be made through process redesign?
e.g. Is 3D printing too expensive for mass market, or does it save tooling costs for small runs?
T
Timescale
Key questions to ask:
• Will production times be cut?
• Is staff retraining required (takes time)?
• What lead time do customers expect?
• How long does product development take?
e.g. Robotic assembly is fast once installed, but setup and training takes 12–18 months.
M
Materials Used
Key questions to ask:
• Have the materials been tested for the required use?
• Will improvements outweigh increased costs?
• Are the materials sustainable and recyclable?
• Are they lighter/less toxic than alternatives?
e.g. Carbon fibre is lighter and stronger but costs 10× more than steel.
W
Who the Product is For
Key questions to ask:
• How does the technology help fulfil customer needs?
• Has the target market been researched?
• Is the market budget or premium?
• What is the design's expected lifespan?
e.g. A medical implant must meet patient safety needs regardless of cost — the budget cannot override this.
M
Manufacturing Capabilities
Key questions to ask:
• Will the new technology influence production methods?
• Will automated lines produce high quality, customised products?
• Will it allow flexible reaction to demand?
• Does the factory have the required equipment?
e.g. The factory may need CNC machines to produce parts — if they don't have them, investment is needed first.

🌍 Contemporary & Future Scenarios

Spec ref: 1.2.2 | Key question: How do designers need to consider current and future scenarios when making design decisions?

When making design decisions, the outcome of using a new technology can be positive, negative, or both. Designers must examine five key contemporary and future scenarios and consider how technology can create problems AND provide solutions for each.

📸 Satellite imagery showing a flood event — technology helps predict and respond to natural disasters

🌊 Natural Disasters

Floods, hurricanes, and earthquakes are increasingly linked to human-caused climate change. Technology both reduces and predicts natural disasters.

Technology can help by: Seismic sensors predict earthquakes; satellite data tracks hurricanes; 3D printing produces emergency shelter components quickly; earthquake-resistant building materials and designs.
🏥 Medical Advances

Biotechnology, implants, prosthetics, and MRI scanners improve health outcomes. However, the population impact must be evaluated — longer lives create greater care needs.

Considerations: 3D printed implants improve recovery; longer life expectancy increases elderly care demand; medical advances can also create ethical questions about access and cost.
✈️ Travel

Travel has not become significantly faster in 50 years, but technology has improved comfort, efficiency, and safety. Environmental impact is still a serious concern.

Considerations: Electric vehicles and hydrogen-powered aircraft reduce emissions; lighter composite materials reduce fuel consumption; autonomous vehicles improve safety; but transport remains one of the biggest CO₂ contributors.
🌡️ Global Warming

Probably caused by greenhouse gas emissions from burning fossil fuels. New low-carbon and zero-carbon technologies are the key solutions.

Technology solutions include: Solar and wind power generation; electric vehicles; smart energy management in buildings; carbon capture technology; sustainable manufacturing processes.
📱 Communication

The internet has made global communication cheap, quick, and instant. But designers must NOT assume that all target users have access to technology.

Important caution: Designers must consider whether the target audience has access to hardware, software, internet infrastructure, and reliable power — especially in rural or developing regions.
💡 Exam insight: For scenario questions, always give BOTH a positive outcome AND a negative outcome or caution. For example: "Communication technology allows designers to reach global markets (positive). However, designers should not assume that all target users have reliable internet access or modern devices (caution/negative)." This balanced approach is what earns top marks.

🤝 Ethical Perspectives

Spec ref: 1.2.3 | Key question: What four ethical questions must a designer ask when evaluating a new technology?

Companies that trade ethically are not totally driven by profit. They consider their products' wider implications — acting fairly, honestly, and not exploiting workers, suppliers, or the environment. There are four key ethical questions to always consider.

📸 Fairtrade cocoa farmer — ethical manufacturing means fair wages and safe conditions for producers

📍 1. Where was it made?
  • Cheap labour in developing countries may save costs but can exploit workers
  • New technologies may produce less pollution and waste in some locations
  • Consider whether factory location affects environmental regulations
  • Is the country's environmental and labour law adequate?
👷 2. Who was it made by?
  • Hiring low-paid workers in developing countries has led to exploitation
  • Child labour — the rights of workers must be a high priority
  • Check health and safety rules and building regulations in the factory location
  • Were workers paid a living wage? Did they work in safe conditions?
👥 3. Who will it benefit?
  • New technologies can create cheaper, widely available, higher quality products for all consumers
  • Technology could benefit the consumer by making their life easier
  • Manufacturing new products can create jobs in local communities
  • Does the product reach those who need it most, or only wealthy users?
🌿 4. Fairtrade Products
  • Use raw materials from sustainably managed sources
  • Try to buy materials locally to reduce transport emissions
  • Seek to reduce energy consumption (e.g. via renewable energy)
  • Minimise the impact of waste stream on the environment
💡 Exam tip: Ethics questions often ask you to "explain two methods a company could use to relocate its manufacturing to a developing country." Remember — the question asks about methods, not whether they should relocate. Give two genuinely different methods (e.g. partnering with a local factory vs. setting up a subsidiary company) and explain the ethical considerations of each. Do NOT repeat yourself — the exam tip in the textbook says "you must give two different reasons."

🌿 Environmental Perspectives & LCA

Spec ref: 1.2.4 | Key question: How do designers evaluate the environmental impact of new technologies, and what is a life-cycle analysis?

🧱
Use of Materials
Designers should select fewer materials, ensure they are recyclable, choose lighter options, and avoid toxic substances. Metal ore extraction and oil-based plastics are particularly damaging.
👣
Carbon Footprint
Companies should reduce CO₂ by maximising energy efficiency, analysing supply chains, using renewable energy, recycling, and identifying carbon offsetting methods.
🏭
Energy in Manufacture
Fossil fuel extraction, transportation, and emissions from manufacturing harm the environment. Even renewables impact habitats. Energy-efficient vehicles for distribution reduce the footprint.
🔄
Life-Cycle Analysis
A systematic inventory of environmental impacts at every stage of a product's life. Increasingly required by governments. Identifies where improvements will have the greatest impact.

The 5 Stages of a Life-Cycle Analysis (LCA)

📸 Life Cycle Analysis — all stages from raw material extraction to end of life

1
Raw Material Extraction & Processing
Mining metals, drilling for oil, harvesting timber — the environmental damage at this stage is often the largest but least visible to consumers. Includes habitat destruction, energy use, and transportation of raw materials to processing facilities.
↓
2
Product / Part Manufacture & Assembly
The energy, water, and emissions used in the factory to transform raw materials into finished parts and assemble them into the final product. Includes machining, moulding, welding, and painting processes.
↓
3
Product / Part Transportation & Distribution
The CO₂ produced by moving products from factory to distributor to retailer to consumer. Includes packaging at each stage and the fuel consumed by all modes of transport used.
↓
4
Product / Consumer Use
Energy and resources used by the consumer while using the product over its lifetime. For electronics, this can be the largest stage. For single-use products, this stage is minimal.
↓
5
Product Disposal or Recovery at End of Life
What happens when the product is no longer needed: landfill (worst), energy recovery (intermediate), recycling (good), or reuse (best). Designing for disassembly makes this stage more sustainable.
💡 Key LCA exam insight: LCA questions often ask you to "explain the steps involved." Use the order: extraction → manufacture → transport → use → disposal. The most important thing to add is why LCA is useful — it identifies which stage causes the most damage, so companies can target their sustainability improvements most effectively. Governments are increasingly requiring LCAs from businesses.

Activity 3 — True or False?

Test understanding
📝 Read each statement carefully. This topic is often misunderstood — at least 3 of these statements test common exam misconceptions. Press True or False, then read the explanation carefully.
1. "Critical" in "critical evaluation" means the evaluation is negative — you are looking for problems and disadvantages only.
❌ False — and this is one of the most common exam misconceptions. In academic English, "critical" does NOT mean negative. A critical evaluation means a careful, balanced, thoughtful evaluation that considers BOTH positives AND negatives from multiple perspectives. If you only write disadvantages in an "evaluate" exam question, you will lose significant marks.
2. A life-cycle analysis (LCA) only examines the environmental impact of the manufacturing stage — where the product is physically made in a factory.
❌ False. An LCA examines ALL stages of a product's life — not just manufacturing. The 5 stages are: (1) raw material extraction, (2) manufacture and assembly, (3) transportation and distribution, (4) consumer use, (5) disposal/recovery at end of life. The manufacturing stage is often NOT the most damaging — raw material extraction frequently causes the greatest environmental harm.
3. Because the internet now makes communication cheap and global, designers can safely assume their target market has access to hardware, software, and reliable internet.
❌ False — this is explicitly warned against in the textbook. Although communication is now cheap and global for many people, designers should NOT assume that the target audience has access to hardware, software, internet infrastructure, and reliable power sources. This is especially important in rural areas and developing countries. The digital divide is a real and significant barrier.
4. A Fairtrade product is one that organisations have certified to use sustainably managed raw materials, reduce energy consumption, and minimise environmental waste.
✅ True. Fairtrade certified organisations must: use raw materials from sustainably managed sources, try to buy materials locally, seek to reduce energy consumption (e.g. via renewable energy), and minimise the impact of their waste on the environment. Fairtrade is not just about fair wages — it has environmental standards too.
5. When evaluating a new technology using the BTMWM framework, the Budget (B) criterion is always the most important factor that outweighs all others.
❌ False. No single criterion always outweighs the others — the relative importance of each depends entirely on the context. For a medical implant, "Who the product is for" (patient safety) takes priority over budget. For a fast-moving consumer good, budget may be the primary driver. A good evaluation considers ALL five BTMWM criteria and reaches a conclusion about which is most significant for that specific scenario.
6. Medical advances such as longer life expectancy through better healthcare are entirely positive — they improve quality of life with no negative consequences to consider.
❌ False. Medical advances have both positive and negative consequences. While longer life expectancy and better health outcomes are clearly beneficial, the impact on the wider population must also be evaluated. For example: a growing elderly population creates greater demand for care services, housing, and medical support. Technology that allows people to live longer also means society must plan for supporting more elderly people — a significant social and economic challenge.

✍️ Sentence Frames for Evaluation Questions

For BTMWM evaluation — one criterion (2 marks)
One consideration when evaluating [new technology] for use in [product] is [BTMWM criterion]. [Specific question from that criterion], which means that [consequence for the design decision].
"One consideration when evaluating 3D printing for use in dental implants is budget constraints. The high cost of industrial 3D printing equipment may increase production costs per unit, which means that the final product may only be accessible to patients who can afford premium healthcare, limiting its impact on public health."
For full EVALUATE questions — advantage + disadvantage + conclusion (6–9 marks)
One benefit of using [technology] is [advantage + explanation]. However, a significant consideration is [BTMWM criterion] — [specific concern]. Furthermore, from an [ethical/environmental] perspective, [additional point]. Overall, [balanced conclusion].
"One benefit of using automated robotic assembly is that it reduces human error and produces more consistent product quality, which reduces waste and returns. However, a significant consideration is timescale — the factory would require 12–18 months of installation and staff retraining before full production could begin. Furthermore, from an ethical perspective, automation may displace workers in the factory, creating unemployment in the local community. Overall, robotic assembly is most appropriate for high-volume, standardised products where consistent quality outweighs the transition costs, but a phased introduction with retraining support would address the ethical concern."
For LCA explanation questions (4 marks)
A life-cycle analysis examines the environmental impact at every stage. First, [Stage 1]. Then, [Stage 2]. Next, [Stage 3]. After that, [Stage 4]. Finally, [Stage 5]. This allows companies to identify [purpose of LCA].
"A life-cycle analysis examines the environmental impact at every stage. First, raw material extraction — including the energy and habitat disruption from mining or farming. Then, manufacture and assembly of components in the factory. Next, transportation and distribution from factory to retailer to consumer. After that, consumer use — the energy consumed while the product is in use. Finally, disposal or recovery at end of life — whether the product is recycled, reused, or sent to landfill. This allows companies to identify which stage causes the greatest environmental damage and target their sustainability improvements most effectively."
For ethical perspective questions
From an ethical perspective, a key consideration is [who made it / where / who benefits]. Specifically, [specific ethical concern]. To trade ethically, the company should [ethical action], which would [positive outcome].
"From an ethical perspective, a key consideration is who made the product. Specifically, if manufacturing is moved to a developing country to reduce costs, there is a risk of worker exploitation — low wages, poor safety conditions, or child labour. To trade ethically, the company should require Fairtrade certification from its suppliers and conduct regular factory inspections, which would ensure workers receive fair wages and safe working conditions, reducing the risk of exploitation."
Connective phrases — essential for evaluation answers
  • However,... introduces the opposite side / disadvantage
  • Furthermore,... adds another supporting point in the same direction
  • From an ethical perspective,... introduces the ethics angle
  • From an environmental perspective,... introduces the LCA/sustainability angle
  • This means that... explains the consequence of your point
  • In contrast,... compares with an alternative approach
  • Overall, the most significant factor is... signals a conclusion
  • This is because... gives the reason — essential for "explain" questions
  • Despite [disadvantage], [technology] is suitable because... for justified conclusions

📝 Practice Exam Questions

Q1 · LCA · 4 marks4 marks
Explain the steps involved in carrying out a Life-Cycle Analysis (LCA).
💡 Language hint: Use the 5 stages in order. For each stage, name it AND briefly explain what it involves. Use sequence language: "First... Then... Next... After that... Finally..."
1
Stage 1 — Raw material extraction: Examine the environmental impact of obtaining raw materials — including energy used in mining, drilling, or farming, and the habitat disruption caused.
2
Stage 2 — Manufacture and assembly: Assess the energy, water, and emissions produced during manufacturing, machining, and assembling components into the final product.
3
Stage 3 — Transportation and distribution: Calculate the CO₂ produced by transporting the product from factory to warehouse to retailer to consumer at every stage of the supply chain.
4
Stages 4–5 — Consumer use & End of life: Evaluate the energy used by the consumer during the product's useful life, and then assess the disposal method — whether it is recycled, reused, or sent to landfill.
✏️ Key vocabulary: systematic, environmental impact, extraction, manufacture, transportation, consumer use, disposal, end of life, identify opportunities for improvement.
Q2 · Ethics · 6 marks6 marks
Explain two methods that a company could use to relocate its manufacturing operation to a developing country.
💡 Language hint: Give TWO genuinely different methods (not just two versions of the same idea). For each one: name the method, explain how it works, and explain the ethical consideration. Use: "One method is... Another method is..."
1
Method 1 — Outsourcing to a local manufacturer: The company could contract an existing factory in the developing country to manufacture its products. This avoids the cost of building a new facility and can be implemented more quickly. However, the company must rigorously audit the factory's working conditions to ensure ethical standards — including fair wages, safe working environments, and no child labour — are met and maintained.
2
Method 2 — Establishing a subsidiary company: The company could set up and own its own factory in the developing country, directly employing local workers. This gives greater control over quality and ethical standards. However, the company must invest significantly in training local staff and ensuring health and safety regulations match international standards, even if local law permits lower standards.
✏️ Key vocabulary: outsourcing, subsidiary, audit, working conditions, ethical standards, child labour, fair wages, health and safety.
Q3 · Full Evaluate · 9 marks9 marks
Evaluate the considerations for using new and emerging technologies in the development of a new bicycle for competitive cyclists.
💡 Language hint: This is a 9-mark evaluate question — use at least 3 BTMWM criteria, give an advantage AND disadvantage for each, add an ethical or environmental perspective, and write an overall conclusion. Aim for at least 4 developed paragraphs.
1
Materials (M): Using carbon fibre reinforced plastic (CFRP) would significantly reduce the bicycle's weight while maintaining exceptional strength, which directly improves competitive performance. However, CFRP is expensive and requires specialist manufacturing equipment, which may substantially increase the unit cost and limit access to professional teams with large budgets.
2
Manufacturing Capabilities (M): Advanced CNC machining and automated layup processes for CFRP frames can produce highly consistent, precision components. However, the factory would require specialised equipment and highly skilled technicians, which represents a significant capital investment and may require a long timescale to implement before production can begin.
3
Who the Product is For (W): Competitive cyclists have specific and demanding performance requirements — weight, stiffness, aerodynamics — which justify the use of expensive materials and advanced manufacturing. The target market is likely to be professional cyclists or serious amateurs with a premium budget, meaning that higher costs can be absorbed.
4
Environmental Perspective: Carbon fibre is not recyclable at end of life and its manufacture requires significant energy. A life-cycle analysis would likely show a high carbon footprint at the manufacturing stage. The company should consider using recycled carbon fibre where possible and designing the frame for maximum longevity to reduce environmental impact.
Overall conclusion: New technologies such as CFRP and precision CNC manufacturing are highly appropriate for a competitive cycling bicycle, where performance and weight savings justify the premium cost and complex manufacturing. However, the environmental impact should be addressed through a full LCA and responsible material sourcing, and the long setup timescale must be planned carefully to meet launch deadlines.
