⚙️ 1.8 — Ferrous & Non-Ferrous Metals

Spec ref: 1.8.1 | Textbook pp. 37–39 | Key distinction: ferrous metals contain iron; non-ferrous do not.

📌 Key rule: Ferrous = contains iron (ferrite) → mostly magnetic → vulnerable to rust (except stainless steel). Non-ferrous = no iron → higher corrosion resistance → NOT magnetic → more malleable. 💡 "Ferrous = iron" (ferrum = Latin for iron).
⚙️ Ferrous Metals — contain iron

Stainless Steel Carbon steel + 10.5–18% chromium + 8% nickel + 8% manganese (alloy) Corrosion resistant, hard, tough, sometimes magnetic, resists wear, difficult to cut Kitchenware, sinks, cutlery, medical equipment 1400°C
Cast Iron Iron + 2–6% carbon Hard skin, brittle, soft core, good in compression, self-lubricating, magnetic Machine parts, vices, brake discs, manhole covers 1200°C
⚙️ Non-Ferrous Metals — no iron, no magnetic properties, better corrosion resistance

🔑 Key Terms — Metals
Alloy
A mixture of two or more metals (or a metal and another element) which has improved properties compared to the pure metals alone. Stainless steel and brass are both alloys.
💡 Alloy = mixed metal. Pure metals have set properties; alloying allows engineers to customise properties. Always state the composition when naming an alloy in an exam — e.g. "brass is 65% copper and 35% zinc."
Ductility
The ability of a material to deform by bending, twisting, or stretching — and to be drawn out into a wire — without breaking. Ductility increases with temperature. All ductile materials are malleable, but NOT all malleable materials are ductile.
💡 Ductile = can be drawn into a wire (think of copper electrical wire). Copper and aluminium are both highly ductile. Key exam link: copper wire, drawn steel cables.
Malleability
The ability of a material to be permanently deformed in ALL directions without fracture. Increases with temperature. Copper is highly malleable — it can be shaped by hammering.
💡 Malleable = can be hammered into shape. Think of a blacksmith hammering metal. Copper is so malleable it can be beaten into very thin sheets (copper pots, roofing). Compare: ductile = stretched into wire; malleable = beaten into sheets.
Hardness
The ability of a material to resist deformation, indentation, or penetration. Hard materials can resist abrasion, drilling, impact, scratching, and wear and tear. Hard materials are often brittle (e.g. cast iron).
💡 Hardness ≠ toughness. Hard = resists scratching/denting. Tough = resists breaking/cracking under impact. Cast iron is HARD (resists wear) but BRITTLE (shatters on impact). Mild steel is TOUGH (absorbs impact) but less hard.
Ferrous / Non-Ferrous
Ferrous metals contain iron (ferrum = Latin for iron) and are generally magnetic and prone to rust. Non-ferrous metals contain no iron, are not magnetic, and generally have better corrosion resistance.
💡 Memory trick: FERrous = FERrum = IRON. If you see iron in the composition, it's ferrous. Non-ferrous = no iron = no rust (usually). Exception: stainless steel IS ferrous but does NOT rust due to its chromium content.
Strength-to-Weight Ratio
How strong a material is compared to how heavy it is. Aluminium has an excellent strength-to-weight ratio — it is strong enough for structural use but much lighter than steel, making it ideal for aircraft and vehicles.
💡 High strength-to-weight = strong but light. Aluminium = key exam example. Always mention this when explaining why aluminium is used for aircraft, drinks cans, or bicycle frames — strength without excessive weight.
📝 Most-Examined Questions — 1.8 Metals
Q1 · Most examined · 4 marks4 marks
Explain two reasons why stainless steel would be chosen over mild steel for the bolts on a bicycle.
💡 Each reason = name the property + explain why it matters for THIS product (bicycle bolts). Don't just say "it is stronger" — say WHY stronger matters for bolts on a bicycle.
1
Corrosion resistance: Bicycle bolts are exposed to rain, mud and sweat. Stainless steel contains chromium, which forms a protective oxide layer that prevents rust. Mild steel would corrode quickly outdoors, weakening the bolt and causing it to seize or fail.
2
Hardness and wear resistance: Stainless steel is harder than mild steel, meaning it resists the wear and abrasion caused by repeated tightening and loosening of bolts. Mild steel threads would strip or wear more easily over time.
Q2 · Most examined · 4 marks4 marks
Explain two reasons why aluminium would be used for drinks cans in preference to mild steel.
💡 Classic exam question — think about what matters for a drinks can: it must be light, resist the liquid inside (corrosion), be safe to use, and be recyclable. Two specific reasons.
1
Corrosion resistance: Aluminium does not rust when in contact with liquids, making it safe to store drinks without the metal contaminating the contents. Mild steel would corrode when exposed to the acidic liquid inside a drinks can.
2
Excellent strength-to-weight ratio: Aluminium is strong enough to withstand the internal pressure of a carbonated drink while being very lightweight. Mild steel cans would be much heavier, increasing transport costs and making them less convenient for consumers to carry.

💡 Definition + example. Always give a specific named example of a malleable metal and application.
1
Malleability is the ability of a material to be permanently deformed in all directions without fracture.
2
For example, copper is highly malleable — it can be beaten into thin sheets or hammered into complex curved shapes such as copper roofing or cookware without cracking.
Q4 · Application · 2 marks2 marks
Explain one reason why copper is a suitable material for use in electrical wiring.
💡 One reason, fully explained. Name the property + link it to what electrical wiring needs to do.
1
Copper is an excellent electrical conductor, meaning it allows electrical current to flow through it with minimal resistance. This makes it ideal for wiring because it ensures efficient transfer of electricity without significant energy loss as heat.
✏️ Bonus: copper is also ductile (can be drawn into thin wire without breaking) and malleable (can be bent around corners during installation). Mention these if asked for a second reason.
✏️ Quick Activity — True or False?

Metals — True or False?

1. Brass is a pure metal made entirely of copper.
❌ False. Brass is an alloy of 65% copper and 35% zinc. It is not a pure metal — the addition of zinc makes it harder and easier to machine than pure copper.
2. All ferrous metals rust easily, including stainless steel.
❌ False. Most ferrous metals rust, but stainless steel is corrosion resistant because its chromium content (10.5–18%) forms a protective oxide layer that prevents rust. Wrought iron is also an exception.
3. Aluminium has a lower melting point than copper.
✅ True. Aluminium melts at 660°C while copper melts at 1100°C. Aluminium's lower melting point makes it easier and cheaper to process and recycle — a fact that contributes to its widespread use in packaging (drinks cans).
4. Cast iron is tough and resists breaking when dropped or hit sharply.
❌ False. Cast iron is brittle — it has a hard skin but a soft core, and it will shatter under a sharp impact rather than deform. This is why cast iron is used for applications in compression (vices, brake discs) but NOT where it will be hit or dropped.

📄 1.9 — Papers & Boards

Spec ref: 1.9.1 | Textbook pp. 40–42 | Key distinction: paper = under 220 gsm; board = over 220 gsm.

📌 Key rule: gsm = grams per square metre. The higher the gsm, the heavier and thicker the paper or board. Paper = under 220 gsm. Board = over 220 gsm (thickness measured in microns, where 1 micron = 1/1000 of a millimetre).

🔑 Key Working Properties — Papers & Boards

Flexibility

Flexural stiffness = resistance to an external bending force. Handling stiffness = ability to support its own weight. Determined by thickness and weight of the material.

Printability

The ability to accept a printed image onto its surface. Affected by smoothness, finish, and structural properties such as bulk or thickness. Different from print quality which depends on the printing machine.

Biodegradability

The ability to be broken down by bacteria or biological means. Most uncoated paper products are biodegradable. Compostable means it can biodegrade in under 12 weeks.

🔑 Key Terms — Papers & Boards
gsm (grams per square metre)
The standard unit for measuring the weight of paper and board. The number of grams a sheet measuring 1m × 1m would weigh. Higher gsm = heavier, thicker, more opaque.
💡 Copier paper = 80 gsm. Cartridge = 120–150 gsm. The boundary between paper and board is 220 gsm. Below 220 = paper. Above 220 = board.
Translucent
Allows light to pass through but is not completely transparent — you can see shapes through it but not sharp detail. Tracing paper is translucent. Different from transparent (completely clear) or opaque (no light passes through).
💡 Trans = through. Translucent = light passes through partly. Translucent ≠ transparent. Tracing paper is translucent (you can trace through it). Greaseproof paper is also translucent.
Corrugated / Fluted
Corrugated = having parallel ridges and furrows (a wavy structure). The fluted inner layer of corrugated board acts like a series of arches, giving it strength to resist crushing forces despite being mostly air.
💡 The fluted inner layer is what gives corrugated board its impact resistance — the air pockets act as cushioning. This is why it is used for protective packaging of fragile items like electronics.

🧴 1.10 — Thermoforming & Thermosetting Polymers

Spec ref: 1.10.1 | Textbook pp. 43–46 | Key distinction: thermoforming CAN be recycled; thermosetting CANNOT.

📌 The most important fact: Thermoforming polymers CAN be reshaped by heat and CAN be recycled. Thermosetting polymers CANNOT be reshaped once set and CANNOT be recycled — because cross-links form between molecule chains that prevent the chains from moving. This single distinction is worth marks in virtually every polymer exam question.

🔑 Key Properties of Polymers

Insulator of Heat

Most polymers prevent heat from passing through. Used for: expanded polystyrene cups, handles on saucepans/kettles/hairdryers, oven doors. Keeps hot things hot and cold things cold safely.

Insulator of Electricity

Most polymers prevent electrical current from flowing. PVC cables shield electrical wires. Urea formaldehyde is used for plugs and sockets. Prevents electrocution and short-circuits.

Toughness

Most polymers withstand rough handling — wheelie bins, buckets, classroom chairs, car bumpers, watering cans. They absorb impact without fracturing. Some will fatigue (weaken) with repeated stress over time.

Cross-linking (Thermosetting)

When thermosetting polymers are heated, cross-links form between molecule chains. These locks prevent the chains from moving again — so the polymer CANNOT be re-softened, re-shaped, or recycled.

🔑 Key Terms — Polymers
Thermoforming Polymer
A polymer that can be re-softened by applying heat, reshaped, and then cooled into a new shape. The molecule chains are NOT cross-linked, so they can slide past each other when heated. CAN be recycled.
💡 Thermo = heat. Thermoforming = formed by heat again and again. Examples: acrylic, HIPS, Biopol®, PET, HDPE, PVC. Key test: thermoforming polymers are usually in the recycling bin — they have a recycling triangle with a number on them.
Thermosetting Polymer
A polymer where cross-links form between molecule chains when first heated, locking the shape permanently. CANNOT be re-softened or recycled. Used where heat resistance is needed.
💡 Thermosetting = SET by heat (permanently). Think of baking a cake — once baked (set), you cannot un-bake it. Cross-links = chemical bonds between chains that prevent movement. Examples: polyester resin, urea formaldehyde, epoxy resin.
GRP (Glass Reinforced Plastic)
A composite material made from polyester resin reinforced with glass fibres. The glass fibres provide tensile strength while the resin binds them and provides rigidity. Very strong and lightweight.
💡 GRP = polyester resin + glass fibres. Used for: boat hulls, sports car bodies (e.g. Lotus Elise), wind turbine blades. The glass fibres stop the brittle resin from cracking by absorbing tension forces.
Monomer / Polymer / Polymerisation
A monomer is a small molecule. Polymerisation is the process of joining many monomers together in a long chain to form a polymer. "Poly" = many. Most synthetic polymers are made from oil-based petrochemicals.
💡 Mono = one (one unit). Poly = many (many units joined). Polymerisation = joining many small molecules into one long chain. Example: many ethylene monomers → polyethylene (polythene) polymer chain.
📝 Most-Examined Questions — 1.10 Polymers
Q1 · Most examined · 2 marks2 marks
Explain the difference in structure between thermoforming and thermosetting polymers.
💡 Structure = molecule chains. Focus on cross-linking. This is a 2-mark definition question — one mark for thermoforming, one mark for thermosetting. Always mention cross-linking.
1
In thermoforming polymers, the molecule chains are NOT cross-linked — they can slide past each other when heated, allowing the polymer to be softened, reshaped, and recycled repeatedly.
2
In thermosetting polymers, cross-links form between the molecule chains when first heated and cooled. These locks prevent the chains from moving again — so the polymer cannot be re-softened, reshaped, or recycled.
Q2 · Application · 2 marks2 marks
Explain one reason why children's toys are made from high-impact polystyrene (HIPS).
💡 Name the property of HIPS + explain WHY it is needed for a children's toy. Think about how children use toys.
1
HIPS is lightweight and impact resistant, meaning it can absorb the knocks and drops that toys inevitably receive during play without breaking. This makes toys safer for children and more durable.
✏️ Also acceptable: HIPS can be formed in a wide range of colours (no painting required), which is important for children's products. It is also widely available and inexpensive to mass-produce using injection moulding.
Q3 · Most examined · 4 marks4 marks
Explain two reasons why urea formaldehyde is a suitable material for electrical plug casings.
💡 Urea formaldehyde is a thermosetting polymer. Think about what a plug casing needs to do: it must handle electricity safely AND handle heat from the electrical current. Two reasons, each with an explanation of why it matters.
1
Excellent electrical insulation: Urea formaldehyde prevents electrical current from passing through its structure, protecting the user from electric shock when handling the plug. This is essential for any product that carries mains electricity.
2
Heat resistant: As a thermosetting polymer, urea formaldehyde cannot be re-softened by heat. This is critical for a plug casing because electrical components generate heat during use — a thermoforming polymer would soften and deform, creating a safety hazard.
✏️ Quick Activity — True or False?

Polymers — True or False?

1. Thermoforming polymers can be recycled because their molecule chains are not permanently cross-linked.
✅ True. This is THE key fact about thermoforming polymers. No cross-links = chains can slide when re-heated = can be reshaped and recycled. Examples: acrylic, HIPS, PET bottles.
2. HIPS (high-impact polystyrene) is the most common thermosetting polymer used in school design technology.
❌ False. HIPS is a thermoforming (thermoplastic) polymer — not thermosetting. It is the most common material used for vacuum forming in school DT because of its low melting point and ease of forming. Urea formaldehyde and polyester resin are thermosetting.
3. Polyester resin can be recycled because it is a polymer made from petrochemicals.
❌ False. Polyester resin is a thermosetting polymer — it CANNOT be recycled. Once cured (set with a catalyst), the cross-links between molecule chains are permanent. Being made from petrochemicals does not determine recyclability — the polymer type does.
4. Acrylic is both food safe and easy to clean, making it suitable for use in food preparation areas.
✅ True. Acrylic (PMMA) is food safe, easily cleaned, and does not harbour bacteria in its surface — making it suitable for menu holders, serving platters, and food containers. However, it scratches easily and breaks if dropped, so care is needed.

🧵 1.11 — Categorisation of Fibres & Textiles

Spec ref: 1.11.1 | Textbook pp. 47–48 | Key distinction: natural fibres (plant or animal sources) vs synthetic fibres (made from oil/coal/petrochemicals).

📌 Structure: Fibres → Yarns → Fabrics. Fibres are fine hair-like structures. They are twisted/spun into yarns, which are then woven, knitted, or felted into fabrics. Natural fibres come from plants (cotton, flax, hemp) or animals (wool, silk). Synthetic fibres are manufactured from petrochemicals (polyester, acrylic, nylon).

🔑 Key Terms — Textiles
Fibre
Thread-like elements that can be formed into yarns and fabrics. Natural fibres come from plants (cellulose) or animals (protein). Synthetic fibres are manufactured from petrochemicals.
💡 Fibres → yarn → fabric. All textiles start as fibres. The type of fibre determines the properties of the final fabric. Knowing the source of a fibre helps predict its properties (plant fibres = breathable/absorbent; synthetic = durable/quick-dry).
Staple
The length of a single fibre. Wool has a short staple with a crimp (kink). Long staple fibres generally produce smoother, stronger yarn. Short staple fibres (like wool) trap more air and provide better insulation.
💡 Staple = the individual fibre length. Long staple = smoother (silk, fine cotton). Short staple = fluffier, warmer (wool). The crimp/kink in wool staple is what traps air pockets — air is a poor conductor of heat, so it insulates the body.
Monomer / Polymerisation
A monomer is a small molecule. Polymerisation joins many monomers into a long polymer chain. Polyester and acrylic fibres are created by joining monomers through polymerisation, then spinning the chains into fibre.
💡 Same term as in 1.10 polymers. Polyester as a FABRIC (1.11) and polyester as a PLASTIC (1.10) are made by the same process — polymerisation of monomers — but processed differently: fabric = spun into fibre; plastic = moulded into shape.
Natural vs Synthetic
Natural fibres grow from plants (cotton, flax, hemp) or animals (wool, silk). Synthetic fibres are manufactured from petrochemicals (oil/coal). Natural = generally breathable and biodegradable. Synthetic = generally more durable, quick-drying, and crease-resistant but environmentally problematic.
💡 General rules: Natural = cool, absorbent, breathable, biodegradable, but can crease/shrink. Synthetic = strong, quick-dry, crease-resistant, but poor breathability, made from non-renewable oil, and not biodegradable (except Biopol®).
📝 Most-Examined Questions — 1.11 Textiles
Q1 · Most examined · 4 marks4 marks
Explain two reasons why polyester is suitable for making raincoats.
💡 Think about what a raincoat needs to do: resist water, be lightweight, dry quickly. Two separate reasons, each with a property + explanation of why it matters for a raincoat specifically.
1
Dries quickly: Polyester has poor water absorbency, meaning it does not soak up rain. Water sits on the surface and runs off or evaporates rapidly. This is essential for a raincoat — the wearer stays drier and the garment does not become heavy when wet.
2
Durable and crease resistant: Polyester fibres are strong both wet and dry and resist abrasion. A raincoat is subject to movement and rubbing against bags and surfaces — polyester's durability ensures the garment maintains its protective properties over time without wearing through or creasing badly.
Q2 · Most examined · 4 marks4 marks
Explain two reasons why cotton is a suitable fibre for making towels.
💡 Think about what a towel needs to do: absorb water effectively, be soft against skin, withstand frequent hot washing. Name the property → explain why it matters for a towel.
1
Highly absorbent: Cotton fibres are made of cellulose, which absorbs large amounts of water rapidly. This is the most important property for a towel — it needs to draw moisture away from the body quickly and effectively after bathing.
2
Withstands frequent washing at high temperatures: Cotton can be washed at 60–90°C, which is essential for hygiene — towels must be sanitised regularly. Cotton fibres are strong enough to withstand this without deteriorating, unlike some synthetic fibres that would be damaged by high temperatures.
Q3 · Compare · 4 marks4 marks
Compare the properties of wool and polyester as fibres for making a fleece jacket. State which you would recommend and justify your choice.
💡 Compare = give properties of BOTH. Recommend = choose one and say WHY it is more suitable for THIS specific product (fleece jacket — worn outdoors, needs warmth, needs to dry quickly).
1
Wool: Naturally warm (crimp traps air); breathable and absorbent; repels rain; hangs well; creases drop out. But: dries slowly; can shrink; heavy when wet; can irritate skin; expensive.
2
Polyester: Strong wet or dry; dries very quickly; crease and stain resistant; lightweight; easy care; can be recycled. But: poor absorbency; does not breathe as well as wool; made from oil — not environmentally friendly.
3
Recommendation — Polyester: For a fleece jacket, polyester is more appropriate. The fleece structure compensates for warmth (air is trapped between fibres), and polyester's key advantage — drying quickly when wet — is critical for outdoor clothing. Wool becomes very heavy when wet and dries slowly, making it impractical for active outdoor use. Polyester is also lighter, easier to care for, and less expensive to produce at scale.
Q4 · Retrieval · 3 marks3 marks
