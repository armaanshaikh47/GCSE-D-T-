Non-Renewable Energy
Fossil Fuels
An energy source that cannot be replaced once it has been used. Fossil fuels (coal, oil, gas) formed over millions of years and will eventually run out. The opposite is renewable.
"Coal is a non-renewable energy source — once all the coal in the ground is burned, it cannot be replaced or replenished."
💡 Re-new = make new again. Non-renewable = cannot be made new again. Think: finite, running out, cannot be replaced.
Fossil Fuels
Fossil Fuels
Coal, oil, and natural gas — formed from the remains of plants and animals that lived millions of years ago, compressed underground over time into energy-rich fuels.
"Burning fossil fuels such as coal and oil releases CO₂ into the atmosphere, contributing to global warming and climate change."
💡 A fossil is the remains of an ancient plant or animal. Fossil fuels = fuels made from ancient fossils. They took millions of years to form.
Turbine
Fossil Fuels
A machine with rotating blades that converts the movement of a fluid (water, steam, or wind) into mechanical energy, which then drives a generator to produce electricity.
"When coal is burned, the heat converts water into steam, and the steam spins a turbine at high speed to generate electricity."
💡 Think of a fan — a turbine looks like a giant fan that spins to make electricity. Used in coal, wind, tidal, and hydroelectric power.
Combustion
Fossil Fuels
The process of burning a fuel in the presence of oxygen to release energy in the form of heat and light. Most fossil fuels generate energy through combustion.
"The combustion of natural gas in a power station releases heat energy, which is used to boil water and drive a steam turbine."
💡 Combust = to catch fire and burn. Combustion = the burning process. Related word: internal combustion engine (used in petrol cars).
Renewable Energy
Renewables
Energy from natural sources that are replenished naturally and will not run out — including sunlight, wind, water flow, and plant matter. They produce little or no greenhouse gas emissions.
"Solar and wind are renewable energy sources because the sun will continue to shine and the wind will continue to blow indefinitely."
💡 Renew = to restore or replace. Renewable energy = energy that nature renews automatically and continuously. It never runs out.
Photovoltaic (PV)
Renewables
Technology that converts sunlight directly into electricity using solar cells made of silicon. When light hits the cell, it causes electrons to flow — creating an electric current.
"Photovoltaic panels on the school roof generate free electricity during daylight hours, reducing energy bills significantly."
💡 Photo = light (like photography). Voltaic = related to electricity (from Alessandro Volta, inventor of the battery). Photo + voltaic = light → electricity.
Biomass
Renewables
Organic material derived from living or recently living organisms — such as wood, crops, agricultural waste, and landfill gas — that is burned or converted to produce heat or electricity.
"The power station burns biomass — wood pellets and agricultural waste — to generate electricity, releasing far less CO₂ than coal."
💡 Bio = living/life. Mass = matter/material. Biomass = living or once-living material used as fuel. Examples: wood, crops, animal waste, landfill gas.
Biodiesel
Renewables
A fuel made from natural plants, vegetables, and fermented waste cooking oil. It can be used in diesel engines without modifying the engine and produces fewer harmful emissions than regular diesel.
"Some buses now run on biodiesel made from recycled cooking oil from restaurants — a sustainable and low-emission alternative to regular diesel."
💡 Bio = biological/living. Diesel = a type of fuel. Biodiesel = biologically-made diesel. Key advantage: no harmful chemicals; can use existing engines.
Tidal Barrage
Renewables
A large dam-like structure built across a tidal river, bay, or estuary. As the tide rises and falls, water is trapped and released through turbines to generate electricity. Tides are predictable and reliable.
"A tidal barrage built across a river estuary can generate electricity twice a day as the tide flows in and out through turbines in the structure."
💡 A barrage = a barrier/dam. A tidal barrage = a barrier that uses tidal water to spin turbines. Key fact: tides are predictable — unlike wind and solar.
Hydroelectric Power
Renewables
Electricity generated by the movement of water. A dam is built to store water at height; when released, the water flows through tunnels and spins turbines to produce large quantities of cheap electricity.
"Norway generates over 90% of its electricity from hydroelectric power using the country's many rivers and reservoirs."
💡 Hydro = water (like hydrate). Electric = electricity. Hydroelectric = water → electricity. Key fact: large amount of low-cost power. Disadvantage: expensive to build.
Battery / Cell
Power Systems
A portable device that stores chemical energy and converts it into electrical energy. A cell is a single unit; a battery is two or more cells connected together. Types include cylindrical (AA, AAA) and prismatic (flat, used in phones).
"A hearing aid uses a small battery cell because it needs a compact, portable power source that does not require a mains electricity connection."
💡 Cylindrical cells = round, like a AA battery. Prismatic cells = flat, like in a smartphone. Key: batteries are portable but can pollute if disposed of incorrectly.
Mains Electricity
Power Systems
Electricity supplied by the National Grid to homes, schools, and businesses through cables and sockets. It provides alternating current (AC) at 230 volts in the UK. Devices must be plugged in to use it.
"A refrigerator uses mains electricity because it needs a constant, reliable power supply 24 hours a day — something a battery cannot provide."
💡 Mains = the main supply (the power network). Mains electricity = supplied from the National Grid. Key facts: 230V in the UK; alternating current (AC); not portable.
Alternating Current (AC)
Power Systems
A type of electrical current where the flow of electrons reverses direction repeatedly — in the UK, this happens 50 times per second. Used in mains electricity because it can travel efficiently over long distances.
"Mains electricity uses alternating current which flows in one direction then the other 50 times per second, making it suitable for the National Grid."
💡 Alternate = to switch between two things. AC = current that alternates (switches direction). The opposite is DC (direct current) — used in batteries, which always flows one way.
Portability
Choosing Sources
Whether a power source can be easily carried and used away from a mains electricity supply. Portable devices (like phones) need portable power sources such as batteries or small solar cells.
"When designing a portable medical device, portability of the power source is critical — it must work in locations without access to mains electricity."
💡 Port = carry (like a seaport = place ships carry goods). Portable = can be carried. Portability = the quality of being easy to carry. Key question: Does the product need to move?
Power Output
Choosing Sources
The amount of electrical energy a source can reliably deliver. Some sources (like solar and wind) have variable output — they produce more or less electricity depending on weather conditions.
"Solar panels have inconsistent power output because they produce more electricity on sunny days than on cloudy days, which makes them unreliable as a sole power source."
💡 Output = what comes out. Power output = how much electricity comes out. Key concept: variable (changes) vs. consistent/reliable (always the same).
Environmental Impact
Choosing Sources
The effect a power source has on the natural environment — including air pollution, land use, habitat destruction, and CO₂ emissions. All energy sources have some environmental impact, even renewables.
"Although wind turbines have no emissions when operating, their environmental impact includes visual pollution and potential disruption to wildlife habitats."
💡 Impact = effect or consequence. Environmental impact = the consequences for nature. Key exam point: no energy source has zero environmental impact — always consider active AND passive impacts.
National Grid
Choosing Sources
The network of power stations, cables, and transformers that generates and distributes electricity across the country to homes and businesses. It must constantly balance supply with demand.
"Electricity generated at a wind farm is fed into the National Grid, which then distributes it to homes and businesses across the country."
💡 A grid = a network of lines (like a grid on a map). The National Grid = the national network of electrical cables that carries electricity everywhere in the country.

🪨 Fossil Fuels — Non-Renewable Energy Sources

Spec ref: 1.3.1a | Key question: How do coal, oil and gas generate energy, and what are their advantages and disadvantages?

📖
What is a fossil fuel?
Formed from the remains of plants and animals that died millions of years ago. They were compressed underground and slowly converted into energy-rich fuels. They are finite — once used they cannot be replaced.
🔥
How do they make electricity?
Most fossil fuels work by combustion (burning). The heat boils water → steam spins a turbine → turbine drives a generator → electricity is produced. This is called a thermal power station.
☁️
Environmental problem
Burning fossil fuels releases CO₂ and other greenhouse gases (sulfur dioxide, nitrogen oxides). These cause global warming, acid rain, and air pollution. Extraction also damages landscapes and risks environmental disasters.
🏭
Why do we still use them?
Despite their disadvantages, fossil fuels provide stable, large-scale, high-power electricity that renewable sources currently cannot always match. They are also relatively cheap to extract and convert.

💡 Exam insight: A common exam question asks you to justify why fossil fuels are still used despite their disadvantages. Key answer: they provide consistent, high-output electricity that renewables cannot always reliably match, especially at large scale.

🌿 Renewable Energy Sources

Spec ref: 1.3.1b–f | Key question: How do renewable sources work, and what are their advantages and disadvantages?

🔄
What makes it renewable?
A renewable energy source is replenished naturally by the environment. The sun keeps shining, the wind keeps blowing, and rivers keep flowing. They are not "used up" in the way fossil fuels are.
🌍
Environmental benefit
Most renewable sources produce zero or very low emissions during operation. This makes them crucial for tackling climate change and reducing our carbon footprint.
⚠️
Important limitation
Renewables are not without problems — they often have variable power output (dependent on weather), high initial setup costs, and can impact ecosystems or landscapes.
📊
UK context
Renewable energy sources now produce around 20% of the UK's electricity. The government has targets to increase this significantly to meet net-zero carbon emissions goals.

⚡ Power Systems — Powering Products & Devices

Spec ref: 1.3.2 | Key question: What are the different ways to power a product or system, and when should each be used?

A power system is a network of components that supply, transfer, and use electrical energy. Designers must choose the most appropriate system for each product. Here are the four main options:

🔋 Batteries & Cells

Use chemical energy to make electricity. Two types: cylindrical (D, C, AA, AAA — round, common, cheap, long life) and prismatic (flat, flexible, used in phones and laptops — shorter life but rechargeable).

✓ AdvantagesPortable; no mains connection needed; widely available; inexpensive cylindrical types
✗ DisadvantagesCan leak chemicals into soil; expensive to dispose of properly; prismatic types have shorter lifespan
📱 Examples: AA batteries in remote controls, prismatic cells in smartphones and hearing aids, medical implants
☀️ Solar Cells

Electronic devices containing two silicon layers that allow electrons to flow when sunlight hits them. Free energy once the panels are installed. Can provide local electricity for homes, schools, or small devices.

✓ AdvantagesFree energy once installed; non-polluting; suitable for off-grid locations; low-voltage versions good for small products
✗ DisadvantagesOutput depends on sunlight availability; solar farms can look unsightly; high initial cost
🏠 Examples: Solar panels on rooftops, small solar cells in garden lights, solar-powered calculators and model cars
🔌 Mains Electricity

Supplied through the National Grid using alternating current (AC) at 230 volts. The most powerful and consistent supply available. Products must be plugged into a socket — so they are not portable.

✓ AdvantagesConsistent, high-power supply; no running out; powers demanding appliances; already connected to most buildings
✗ DisadvantagesNot portable; can be produced using dirty energy (fossil fuels); requires safe wiring and connectors
🍳 Examples: Refrigerators, washing machines, kettles, televisions, power tools
💨 Wind Power

Converts wind movement into electricity via a bladed rotor driving a rotating shaft in a generator. Can range from large offshore wind farms to small home turbines. Output depends on wind strength.

✓ AdvantagesNo emissions in operation; can be built at home (small versions); offshore farms produce significant electricity
✗ DisadvantagesDependent on wind — unpredictable output; less useful on a small scale; visually controversial
🏠 Examples: Offshore wind farms feeding the National Grid; small domestic turbines on rural homes
💡 Key exam insight: When an exam question asks you to choose a power source for a specific product, always link your choice to the product's context. Ask yourself: Does it need to be portable? Does it need high power? Where will it be used? What does the environment look like?

🎯 Choosing Appropriate Energy Sources

Spec ref: 1.3.3 | Key question: What five factors must a designer consider when selecting an energy source for a product?

A useful acronym: P – E – P – C – C  (Portability, Environmental impact, Power output, Circuit connections, Cost)

🎒
1. Portability of the Power Source
Can the product be used away from a mains electricity supply? Remote working, outdoor use, and medical devices often require portable, battery-powered or solar-powered solutions that do not need to be plugged in.
🔍 Example: A portable glucose monitor needs a battery — it cannot rely on mains electricity as it is used on the move.
🌿
2. Environmental Impact
No energy source is completely "clean." Designers must consider active impacts (emissions during use, e.g. CO₂ from burning coal) and passive impacts (visual impact of wind farms, habitat loss from dams). Transport and disposal of equipment also have environmental costs.
🔍 Example: Solar panels have no emissions during operation, but their manufacture uses energy and rare materials.
⚡
3. Power Output
Different products need different amounts of power. Renewables like solar and wind have variable output — they produce less energy at night or on calm days. This makes them unreliable as the sole power source for high-demand applications without battery storage backup.
🔍 Example: A hospital needs a consistent, high-power supply — it cannot rely solely on wind power due to its unpredictable output.
🔌
4. Circuit / System Connections
Designers must consider how the power source will connect to the product — including the type of plugs, connectors, and terminals needed. Different power sources require different connection methods (e.g. battery terminals vs mains plug vs solar cell connectors).
🔍 Example: Switching from batteries to mains power in a product requires redesigning the circuit connection to handle 230V AC safely.
💷
5. Cost
The designer must consider both the initial setup cost (how much the power system costs to install or build into the product) and the running cost (how much it costs to keep it powered over time). Batteries have low setup costs but ongoing replacement costs; solar has high setup but near-zero running costs.
🔍 Example: A solar-powered street light has a high initial cost but zero running costs, making it cost-effective over many years.

Activity 3 — True or False?

Test understanding
📝 Instructions: Read each statement carefully. Press True or False. You will get immediate feedback explaining why the answer is correct. This tests understanding, not just memory.
1. Solar panels have no environmental impact because they produce zero emissions during operation.
❌ False. Solar panels have zero emissions during operation, but they do have an environmental impact — manufacturing them uses energy and rare materials, and large solar farms can change local ecosystems and ecology.
2. Gas produces fewer CO₂ emissions per unit of energy than coal, making it the least polluting of the fossil fuels.
✅ True. According to the textbook, gas combustion emits carbon dioxide at about half the rate of coal. This is why gas is considered the "cleanest" fossil fuel, though it is still a significant greenhouse gas emitter.
3. Tidal power is an unpredictable energy source because we cannot know when the tides will come in or go out.
❌ False. This is a common mistake! Tides are actually predictable and stable — we can calculate exactly when high and low tides will occur years in advance. This makes tidal power more reliable than wind or solar, which depend on unpredictable weather.
4. Prismatic battery cells are found in devices like smartphones and laptops because they are flat and flexible, fitting compactly inside slim products.
✅ True. Prismatic cells are flat and flexible, making them ideal for the thin profile of modern smartphones and laptops. They can be more expensive to manufacture and have a shorter lifespan than cylindrical cells, but their compact shape makes them essential for portable electronics.
5. Hydroelectric power is a non-renewable energy source because it uses water, which can run out in times of drought.
❌ False. Hydroelectric power is classified as a renewable energy source because water is replenished naturally through the water cycle (rain refills rivers and reservoirs). It is true that drought can reduce output, but the water source itself is renewable — unlike coal or oil, which cannot be replaced.
6. When choosing an energy source, a designer must consider both the initial setup cost AND the long-term running cost.
✅ True. For example, a solar panel has a high initial setup cost but near-zero running costs. A battery-powered device has a low initial cost but ongoing battery replacement costs. Designers must evaluate the total cost of ownership, not just the upfront price, to make the best decision.

Name something directly — one word or one short sentence is usually enough. No explanation needed.
1 mark each
Describe
Say what something is or how it looks/works. Give features. No need to explain why.
1–2 marks
Explain
State what + give the reason WHY. Use: "because…", "this means that…", "therefore…" — a reason is essential for full marks.
2–4 marks
Evaluate
Discuss advantages AND disadvantages, then reach a conclusion or judgement. Always consider both sides.
6–9 marks
Justify
Give reasons to support a decision or choice. Explain WHY the choice is the best option for the specific context.
3–4 marks
Compare
Identify similarities AND differences between two or more things. Use: "whereas", "in contrast", "similarly", "on the other hand".

✍️ Sentence Frames for Energy Questions

The orange sections are where you add your own content. Study the examples, then practise replacing the content with a different energy source.

For EXPLAIN questions — advantage (2 marks)
One advantage of [energy source] is that [advantage]. This means that [consequence / why this is beneficial].
"One advantage of solar power is that it produces electricity with zero emissions during operation. This means that it does not contribute to air pollution or greenhouse gas emissions, making it a sustainable energy source for reducing a company's carbon footprint."
For EXPLAIN questions — disadvantage (2 marks)
One disadvantage of [energy source] is [disadvantage]. This is a problem because [explain the negative consequence].
"One disadvantage of wind power is that its power output is variable and unpredictable. This is a problem because on calm days the turbines produce little or no electricity, meaning that a backup energy source is needed, which adds to cost and complexity."
For EVALUATE questions — balanced argument (6–9 marks)
One benefit of [energy source] is [advantage + explanation]. However, a significant drawback is that [disadvantage + explanation]. Furthermore, [second point]. Overall, [balanced conclusion].
"One benefit of hydroelectric power is that it produces a large amount of low-cost electricity reliably, with no emissions during operation. However, a significant drawback is that constructing a dam is extremely expensive and can damage ecosystems, displacing wildlife and sometimes local communities. Furthermore, the reservoir created may affect the local water cycle. Overall, hydroelectric power is an excellent renewable option for countries with suitable geography, but the high initial environmental and financial cost must be carefully considered."
For JUSTIFY questions — choosing a power source (3–4 marks)
The most appropriate power source for [product] is [power source]. This is because [reason 1 linked to product context]. Furthermore, [reason 2], which means [benefit].
"The most appropriate power source for a portable blood pressure monitor is batteries. This is because the device must be used in locations without access to mains electricity, such as in a patient's home or outdoors. Furthermore, batteries are compact and lightweight, which means they do not add significant size or weight to the device, maintaining its portability."
Useful comparison language (for Compare questions)
  • In contrast,... introduces a difference between two sources
  • Similarly,... shows a shared feature of two sources
  • Whereas [X]..., [Y]... directly compares two things in one sentence
  • Unlike [X], [Y]... highlights a key difference
  • Both [X] and [Y]... shows similarity before a contrast
  • However, both sources... returns to a shared feature after a contrast

Question 1 · Fossil Fuels4 marks
Explain two reasons why coal is classed as a non-renewable energy source.
💡 Language hint: You need TWO separate reasons, each explained. Use: "One reason is... This is because... A second reason is... This means that..." Each explained reason = 2 marks.
1
Reason 1: Coal was formed from the remains of plants and animals that lived millions of years ago, compressed underground over vast periods of time.
2
Explanation 1: This means that coal cannot be replenished or replaced at any meaningful timescale — once the coal reserves are depleted through burning, they are gone permanently.
3
Reason 2: Coal is classified as non-renewable because the rate at which humans currently consume it is vastly greater than any natural rate of replacement.
4
Explanation 2: As a result, global coal reserves are expected to run out within a few hundred years if current usage continues, making it a finite resource.
✏️ Key vocabulary to use: non-renewable, finite, replenished, depleted, fossil, reserves, current usage
Question 2 · Power Systems2 marks
Explain one reason why solar cells are more environmentally friendly than rechargeable batteries as a power source.
💡 Language hint: State ONE clear reason AND explain the environmental benefit. Use: "Solar cells are more environmentally friendly because... This means that..."
1
Solar cells generate electricity using only sunlight and produce no harmful emissions or chemical waste during operation.
2
Rechargeable batteries, in contrast, contain chemicals such as lithium and heavy metals that can leach into the soil and water if not disposed of correctly, causing environmental damage. Solar cells do not carry this risk once installed.
✏️ Key vocabulary: emissions, chemical waste, photovoltaic, dispose, leach, contaminate, environmentally friendly
Question 3 · Choosing Energy Sources4 marks
Explain two factors that determine the most appropriate energy source for powering a bedroom desk lamp.
💡 Language hint: Think about the product — a desk lamp is used at a fixed location, indoors, and needs reliable, consistent light. Choose two factors from: portability, environmental impact, power output, circuit connections, cost. Explain HOW each factor affects the choice.
1
Factor 1 — Portability: A desk lamp is used in a fixed location in the home and does not need to be moved, so portability is not required. This means that mains electricity is suitable — the lamp can simply be plugged into a wall socket, providing a reliable and consistent power supply.
2
Factor 2 — Power output: A desk lamp needs a consistent, reliable power supply to produce a steady level of light. Mains electricity provides a consistent 230V supply, whereas batteries would drain over time and reduce light output, and solar cells would not provide power at night when the lamp is most needed.
✏️ Key vocabulary: fixed location, portable, mains electricity, consistent power supply, reliable, circuit connections, 230 volts
Question 4 · Evaluate (Extended)9 marks
Evaluate the use of a wind system for generating electricity in a remote countryside location.
💡 Language hint: For 9 marks, you must give multiple advantages AND disadvantages, with detailed explanations for each. Use the evaluation sentence frame. End with an overall conclusion. Aim for at least 4 developed points. Use connectives: Furthermore, However, In contrast, Overall...
1
Advantage 1: Wind power produces no emissions during operation, meaning it does not contribute to air pollution or greenhouse gas emissions. For a remote location trying to reduce its carbon footprint, this is a significant environmental benefit.
2
Advantage 2: Wind turbines can be installed in remote countryside areas that are not connected to the National Grid. This makes them particularly valuable as an off-grid power source for rural homes or agricultural buildings, eliminating the high cost of extending mains electricity cables to the location.
3
Advantage 3: Once installed, wind turbines have very low running costs as there is no fuel to purchase, and wind is freely available. Over time, this makes them cost-effective despite the high initial setup expense.
4
Disadvantage 1: However, the power output of wind turbines is variable and unpredictable — on calm days, little or no electricity is generated. This means a backup power source or battery storage system would be required to ensure a consistent supply, adding to the overall cost and complexity.
5
Disadvantage 2: Furthermore, wind turbines can be considered visually unsightly, and some rural communities object to their installation as they alter the character of the natural landscape. Planning permission may be difficult to obtain.
Overall conclusion: Wind power is a well-suited energy source for a remote countryside location because it provides an off-grid, emissions-free supply that can significantly reduce long-term energy costs. However, the variable power output means that battery storage or a backup system must be incorporated into the design, and the visual impact must be carefully considered in planning.
✏️ Key vocabulary: emissions, off-grid, variable output, backup, running costs, visually unsightly, planning permission, landscape

🚨 Common ESL Mistakes in Energy Questions — and How to Fix Them
❌ Avoid writing this✅ Write this insteadWhy?
"Solar energy is very good for the environment""Solar energy produces zero carbon emissions during operation, reducing its contribution to global warming"Be specific — name the environmental benefit
"Wind power is bad because there is no wind sometimes""Wind power has variable power output because it depends on wind speed, which is unpredictable"Use the correct technical vocabulary
"Renewable energy is better than fossil fuels""Renewable energy produces fewer greenhouse gas emissions during operation, however fossil fuels provide a more consistent and reliable power output"Avoid simple value judgements — show nuance
"Coal makes electricity by burning""Coal is burned to produce heat, which converts water into steam. The steam spins a turbine, which drives a generator to produce electricity"Explain the full process with correct terminology
